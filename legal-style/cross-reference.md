# Cross-Reference: Where the Three Oregon Style Sources Diverge

> **Purpose**: Document the specific points where the three Oregon legal style sources give
> different or conflicting rules, and specify which governs when.
> **Last updated**: 2026-02-20

---

## Authority Hierarchy

When working with a document, apply this priority order:

| Document type | Governing source |
| --- | --- |
| Court opinions (Court of Appeals, Supreme Court, Tax Court) | Appellate Courts Style Manual |
| Bills and session law | LC Form & Style Manual |
| Statutory language (meaning, grammar, construction) | LC Drafting Manual |
| ORS citations in court documents | Appellate Courts Style Manual § III.A.5 |
| ORS citations in bills and measures | LC Form & Style Manual |
| Defined terms in bills | LC Drafting Manual Ch. 7 |
| Amendment formatting | LC Form & Style Manual Ch. 6 |

When both LC sources and the Appellate Manual could apply (e.g., you are analyzing a statute cited
in a court document), prefer the source specific to what you are *writing* — not what you are
reading.

## 1. Serial (Oxford) Comma

**This is the most common source of confusion.**

| Source | Rule | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| LC Drafting Manual | **No serial comma** | `men, women and children` |
| LC Form & Style Manual | **No serial comma** | `A, B and C` |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | **Uses serial comma** (follows Chicago Manual) | `A, B, and C` |

**Applies to**: Every list of three or more items.

**Which governs**: Use no serial comma when drafting or analyzing statutes. Use serial comma in
court opinions.

**Common mistake**: Treating statutory text and court opinion text as if the same comma convention
applies. They do not.

---

## 2. ORS Citation Form: Ranges

**Both sources cite ORS sections in the same basic format** (`ORS 174.100`), but differ on how
they express ranges.

| Source | Narrative form | Citation form |
| --- | --- | --- |
| LC Form & Style Manual | `ORS 174.010 to 174.100` | same |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | `ORS 30.150 to 30.175` | `ORS 30.150 - 30.175` |

**Key points:**

- "To" in both sources means "to and including" (both endpoints and everything between)
- The Appellate manual uses a hyphen with spaces (` - `) in citation form; LC always uses "to"
- Neither source uses "et seq." — always set out first and last section

---

## 3. ORS Sub-Citation Terminology

When referring to parts of an ORS section, the Appellate manual specifies precise terminology that
may differ from common usage.

**Appellate manual rule**: Numbered paragraphs within a section are **subsections**; lettered
paragraphs are **paragraphs**.

| Notation | Appellate term | Common term |
| --- | --- | --- |
| ORS 305.220(1) | subsection (1) | subsection (1) ✓ |
| ORS 305.220(1)(a) | paragraph (a) of subsection (1) | paragraph (a) ✓ |
| ORS 305.220(1)(a)(A) | subparagraph (A) | subparagraph (A) ✓ |

**When referring to (1)(a) collectively**, Appellate: use "paragraph (1)(a)" not "subsection (1),
paragraph (a)."

→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/citations.md`](appellate-style-manual/citations.md) § III.A.5
→ LC: [`lc-drafting-manual/numbering-formatting.md`](lc-drafting-manual/numbering-formatting.md)

---

## 4. Oregon Constitution Citation Form

| Source | Form |
| --- | --- |
| LC Form & Style Manual | `Article IV, section 1, Oregon Constitution` |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | `Or Const, Art I, § 17` |

Both forms are internally consistent within their respective sources. Do not mix forms (e.g., do
not write `Or Const, Art IV, section 1`).

**Subsections:**

- LC: `section 1(4)(d), Article IV, Oregon Constitution`
  (or `subsection (4) of section 1, Article IV, Oregon Constitution`)
- Appellate: `Or Const, Art IV, § 1(4)(d)` (or `Or Const, Art IV, § 1(4)`)

→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/citations.md`](appellate-style-manual/citations.md) § III.A.2
→ LC: [`lc-form-style-manual/ors-citations.md`](lc-form-style-manual/ors-citations.md) § 3

## 5. Quotation Mark Punctuation Placement

**Major difference** — punctuation placement with closing quotation marks.

| Source | Rule | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| LC Form & Style Manual (Oregon legislative) | Punctuation goes **outside** closing quotation marks | `"license", or "license".` |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | Punctuation (commas, periods) goes **inside** closing quotation marks | `"license," or "license."` |

**Context matters**: This rule applies when quoting a word or phrase. Both sources agree that when
the punctuation is not part of the quoted material (e.g., a colon or semicolon at the end of a
sentence that quotes something), those go outside.

→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/quotations.md`](appellate-style-manual/quotations.md)
→ LC: [`lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md`](lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md) § 4

---

## 6. Ellipsis Form

| Source | Form | Symbol |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | Three asterisks (partial omission) | `* * *` |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | Five asterisks (block omission) | `* * * * *` |
| LC Form & Style Manual (within quoted text) | Three periods (standard typographic) | `...` |

**Note**: The Appellate manual's asterisk convention applies specifically within quoted text in
court opinions. The LC manual's rules govern legislative text, not quoted passages within opinions.
When a court opinion quotes a statute, the Appellate manual's asterisk convention applies.

→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/quotations.md`](appellate-style-manual/quotations.md) § II.C
→ LC: [`lc-form-style-manual/bill-formatting.md`](lc-form-style-manual/bill-formatting.md)

---

## 7. "Shall" vs. "Must" — Legislative vs. Court Usage

| Source | Shall | Must | May not |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| LC Drafting Manual | Mandatory duty (active voice, named actor) | Condition precedent or passive imperative | Prohibition (preferred) |
| LC Form & Style Manual | Mandatory duty | Condition precedent | Prohibition |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | Generally follows Garner; courts may use "must" broadly | Broadly mandatory; less precise than LC usage | Standard prohibition |

**Practical implication**: When analyzing statutory text, apply LC definitions of these terms (they
reflect legislative intent). When writing a court opinion analyzing what a statute "shall" require,
the LC meaning governs the statute, but Garner/Bluebook conventions govern your prose.

→ LC: [`lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md`](lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md) § 4
→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/style-grammar.md`](appellate-style-manual/style-grammar.md) § III

---

## 8. Tense in Text

| Source | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| LC Drafting Manual | **Present tense** throughout operative statutory provisions |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | **Past tense** for narrating facts; **present tense** for stating legal rules and describing what statutes provide |

**Court opinion practice**: "The trial court found... The statute provides... We hold..."

**Statutory practice**: "A person who commits... The Director shall issue..."

→ LC: [`lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md`](lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md) § 2

---

## 9. Numbers: Spell Out vs. Numerals

| Source | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| LC Form & Style Manual | Spell out one through ten; use numerals for 11 and above |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | Numerals generally; spell out when beginning a sentence or as required by Garner |

**Exception (both sources)**: Always use numerals for statute numbers, dollar amounts, percentages,
dates, and section references.

→ LC: [`lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md`](lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md) § 5
→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/style-grammar.md`](appellate-style-manual/style-grammar.md) § II.D

---

## 10. "Prior to" vs. "Before" and Similar Word Pairs

| Pair | LC Drafting Manual | Appellate Manual |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "prior to" / "before" | Use "before" — "prior to" is archaic legalese | "before" preferred; "prior to" disfavored |
| "subsequent to" / "after" | Use "after" | "after" preferred |
| "in order to" / "to" | Use "to" | "to" preferred |
| "shall" / "will" | "shall" = duty; never "will" | Courts may use "will" in prose; "shall" in legal rules |
| "absent" / "without" | Use "without" — "absent" as preposition disfavored | "without" or "in the absence of" preferred |

Both sources generally agree that plain English is preferred over archaic legalese — the LC manual
is more prescriptive about the specific substitutions.

→ LC: [`lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md`](lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md) § 6
→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/style-grammar.md`](appellate-style-manual/style-grammar.md) § III.A

---

## 11. "This State" vs. "Oregon"

| Source | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| LC Form & Style Manual | In statutory text, always use **"this state"** — not "Oregon" |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | No such restriction; use "Oregon" or "this state" as appropriate |

**Why**: Statutory text applies continuously and indefinitely; "this state" is geographically
precise and timeless in a way that the state name is not for constitutional purposes.

→ LC: [`lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md`](lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md) § 3

---

## 12. Italics in Case Names

| Source | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | Case names in court opinions are **italicized** |
| LC Drafting Manual / Form & Style | When bills cite cases (rarely), case names are not italicized in bill text |

**Note**: Italics apply to case names in the text and footnotes of court opinions. Bill text rarely
cites cases directly; when it does, the LC manual does not require italics.

→ Appellate: [`appellate-style-manual/citations.md`](appellate-style-manual/citations.md) § I.C

---

## 13. Periods in Abbreviations

| Source | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | **No periods** in abbreviations: `Or App`, `P3d`, `S Ct` |
| LC Form & Style Manual | **No periods** in legislative abbreviations: `ORS`, `OAR`, `ORCP` |

Both sources agree: abbreviations used in Oregon legal citation and drafting do not take periods.
This differs from The Bluebook's general practice (which the Appellate manual modifies).

---

## 14. Hyphenation: "Notwithstanding" Clauses

| Source | Convention |
| --- | --- |
| LC Drafting Manual | "Notwithstanding" begins the exception clause that comes first in sentence order |
| Appellate Courts Style Manual | No specific drafting rule; Garner discourages "notwithstanding" as opaque legalese |

**Practical note**: When analyzing what "notwithstanding" means in a statute, apply the LC rule (it
means the provision operates despite what the cited law otherwise says). When writing about it in a
court opinion, plain language alternatives may be appropriate in the surrounding prose.

→ LC: [`lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md`](lc-form-style-manual/grammar-punctuation.md) § 2
→ LC: [`lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md`](lc-drafting-manual/sentence-construction.md)

## Summary Table: Key Differences at a Glance

| Issue | LC (Bills/Statutes) | Appellate (Court Opinions) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Serial comma | **No** | **Yes** |
| ORS range form | "to" always | "to" (narrative); " - " (citation) |
| Oregon Constitution form | `Article IV, section 1, Oregon Constitution` | `Or Const, Art IV, § 1` |
| Punctuation + quotation marks | **Outside** | **Inside** (commas/periods) |
| Ellipsis in quoted text | Periods (`...`) in quoted statutes | Three asterisks (`* * *`) |
| Numbers | Spell out 1–10 | Numerals generally |
| Tense | Present tense always | Past (facts) / present (law) |
| "This state" | Required in statutory text | Not required |
| Italics in case names | N/A (bills rarely cite cases) | **Required** |
| "Prior to" | Use "before" | Prefer "before" |
