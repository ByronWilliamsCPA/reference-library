# Defined Terms — Oregon Legislative Drafting

> **Source**: Oregon LC Bill Drafting Manual, 2024 Edition (19th Edition), Chapter 7; ORS
> chapter 174
> **Scope**: When and how to define terms; "means" vs. "includes"; scope of definitions; ORS
> chapter 174 automatic definitions; act names; policy statements; legislative findings

---

## Quick Reference

| Rule | Standard |
|------|----------|
| "means" | Restrictive — term means exactly this and nothing else |
| "includes" | Expansive — term includes these things among others |
| "means and includes" | Never use — contradictory and meaningless |
| Definition placement | Beginning of act; or beginning of individual section if limited to that section |
| Definition order | Alphabetical within the definition section |
| Defined term format | Placed in quotation marks in the definition |
| Scope limitation | "As used in this section..." or "As used in sections ___ to ___ of this (year) Act..." |
| Capitalization | Defined terms used in the text are capitalized |
| ORS 174.100 | "Person" — includes corporations and other entities by default |
| ORS 174.102 | "Written" and "in writing" — includes electronic records (conditions apply) |
| ORS 174.104 | Numbers and gender — applies to all Oregon statutes |
| ORS 174.107 | "Signature" — includes electronic signatures (conditions apply) |
| ORS 174.109 | "Agency" — default definition for state agencies |

## 1. When to Define a Term

Define a term when:

- It is a technical or specialized term whose ordinary meaning might be misunderstood
- It is used in a specialized way that differs from the ordinary meaning
- It needs to include things not ordinarily within its meaning
- It needs to exclude things ordinarily within its meaning
- Precision is essential to enforcement or compliance

Do NOT define a term when:

- The ordinary meaning is clear and unambiguous
- The definition merely restates the ordinary meaning
- ORS chapter 174 already defines the term in the way you need it
- Defining the term might inadvertently exclude things you intend to include

**Rule:** A definition that says "X means X" (i.e., restates the obvious) adds nothing and
should be omitted.

---

## 2. "Means" vs. "Includes"

This distinction is fundamental and non-negotiable in Oregon drafting.

### "Means" — Restrictive

"Means" creates a complete, exhaustive definition. The defined term means exactly and only
what the definition states, and nothing more.

> "License" **means** a document issued by the Department authorizing a person to engage in
> the activity described in ORS ___ .

After this definition, "license" in the act means only documents issued by the Department —
no other documents, no other authorizations.

### "Includes" — Expansive

"Includes" is used when the definition enlarges the ordinary meaning of a term by adding
things that might not otherwise be within it. It is not exhaustive — the ordinary meaning of
the term still applies, and the listed items are in addition to it.

> "Household goods" **includes** furniture, clothing and appliances.

After this definition, "household goods" still means all things ordinarily considered
household goods, and specifically also includes furniture, clothing, and appliances.

**Use "includes" when you want to ensure that specific things are covered without limiting
the term to only those things.**

### "Means and Includes" — Forbidden

Never write "means and includes." The terms are logically contradictory — "means" creates an
exhaustive definition while "includes" creates an expansive one. Using them together produces
a meaningless definition. Choose one:

- If the list is exhaustive: use "means"
- If the list is illustrative or expands the ordinary meaning: use "includes"

### "Does Not Include" — Restrictive Exclusion

Use "does not include" to exclude something from the ordinary meaning of a term:

> "Employee" means a person employed by the state but **does not include** an independent
> contractor.

This is clear and unambiguous. Prefer it over attempting to enumerate everyone who is
included.

## 3. Placement of Definitions

### Bill-Wide Definition Section

When defined terms are used throughout the bill, place a definition section at the beginning
of the bill's operative text:

> SECTION 1. As used in sections 1 to ___ of this (year) Act:
>
> (1) "Director" means the Director of the Department of Consumer and Business Services.
>
> (2) "License" means a license issued under section ___ of this (year) Act.

After codification, the scope clause becomes "As used in ORS ___ to ___:" and is updated by
Legislative Counsel.

### Section-Specific Definition

When a term is used only in a single section, define it in that section:

> SECTION ___. (1) **As used in this section,** "qualified voter" means a person who is
> registered to vote under ORS 247.012 and has voted in at least one of the two most recent
> state general elections.

### Subsection-Specific Definition

If a term is used only in one subsection:

> (3) **For purposes of this subsection,** "imminent danger" means a situation that presents
> a substantial probability of injury or death within 24 hours.

---

## 4. Format and Order

### Format for Each Definition

Each definition occupies its own subsection or paragraph within the definition section.
The defined term is placed in quotation marks, followed immediately by "means" or "includes":

> (1) **"Director"** means the Director of the Department of Agriculture.
>
> (2) **"Farm product"** means a crop or livestock produced for sale.
>
> (3) **"Licensee"** means a person licensed under this (year) Act.

### Alphabetical Order

Definitions are listed in alphabetical order within the definition section.

### Defined Term Format in the Text

When a defined term appears in the operative text of the bill, it is capitalized (except for
defined terms that are ordinary common nouns used in a specific sense, where the context
makes the definition clear). The capitalization signals to the reader that the term has a
specific, defined meaning.

### Quotation Marks Only in the Definition Section

Quotation marks appear around the term only in the definition itself. Do not put quotation
marks around defined terms every time they appear in the operative text — capitalize them.

---

## 5. Scope Limitations

The scope clause at the beginning of a definition section controls where the definitions
apply. Use precise language:

**Applies to the entire act:**

> As used in sections 1 to ___ of this (year) Act:

After codification, this becomes:

> As used in ORS ___ to ___:

**Applies only to this section:**

> As used in this section:

**Applies to specific sections:**

> As used in sections ___ and ___ of this (year) Act:

**Never use "this Act" without specifying section range.** "This Act" is ambiguous once the
act is codified across multiple ORS chapters.

---

## 6. ORS Chapter 174 — Automatic Definitions

ORS chapter 174 contains definitions that apply to all Oregon statutes without needing to be
expressly adopted in each bill. Before defining a term, check whether ORS chapter 174 already
defines it in the way you intend.

### ORS 174.100 — "Person"

> "Person" includes individuals, corporations, associations, firms, partnerships, limited
> liability companies and joint stock companies.

Because this definition applies automatically, most bills do not need to define "person."
However, if you need to modify the meaning (to include or exclude specific entities), define
it expressly in the bill.

**Critical distinction:** "Person" (ORS 174.100) includes legal entities. "Individual" is
not defined in ORS 174.100 and refers only to a human being. Use the correct term — see
`sentence-construction.md` section 13.

### ORS 174.102 — "Written" and "In Writing"

When ORS requires something to be "written" or "in writing," this includes electronic records
if the parties agree or if the context permits. Review the current text of ORS 174.102 for
specific conditions.

### ORS 174.104 — Numbers and Gender

Words used in the singular include the plural. Words used in the plural include the singular.
Words used in the masculine include the feminine. Words used in the feminine include the
masculine. These rules apply throughout the ORS.

Consequence: You rarely need to write "person or persons" — "person" covers both.

### ORS 174.107 — "Signature"

"Signature" includes electronic signatures under conditions specified in the statute. Review
ORS 174.107 when "signature" is used in a bill.

### ORS 174.109 — "Agency"

Provides a default definition of "agency" for Oregon statutes. If your bill uses "agency"
to refer specifically to state agencies, check whether ORS 174.109 applies or whether a more
specific definition is needed.

### ORS 174.110 — "May" and "Shall"

This statute provides that in all Oregon laws, "may" is permissive and "shall" is mandatory.
This codifies the drafting rules — you do not need to state this in each bill.

### ORS 174.120 — "Or" and "And"

"Or" may be read as "and" and vice versa, if the context requires it. This is a rule of
construction applied by courts — not a license to be imprecise. Draft "and" and "or"
carefully.

## 7. Act Names (Short Titles)

An "act name" is a short title for a bill that becomes an informal reference for the statute
once enacted. Act names are optional and should be used only when the act has the scope and
significance to warrant a name that will be used in practice.

**Typical placement:** Early in the bill, after any definition section.

**Standard language:**

> SECTION ___. Sections ___ to ___ of this (year) Act may be known and cited as the "[Name]
> Act."

Or, if the act is to be codified under a specific ORS section:

> SECTION ___. This (year) Act may be known as the "[Name] Act."

**Rules:**
- The "may be known as" formulation avoids mandating use of the short title — it is a
  popular rather than official name.
- Avoid creating act names for every bill — reserve them for major legislation.
- The act name should be memorable, descriptive, and free of jargon.

## 8. Policy and Purpose Statements

A policy or purpose statement articulates the Legislature's intent in enacting the bill. These
statements can be useful in statutory interpretation but are not operative — they do not
impose duties or grant rights.

**Standard placement:** After any definition section and act name, before substantive
provisions.

**Standard language:**

> SECTION ___. The Legislative Assembly finds and declares that:
>
> (1) [finding]; and
>
> (2) [finding].
>
> The purpose of sections ___ to ___ of this (year) Act is to [state the purpose].

Or simply:

> SECTION ___. The purpose of sections ___ to ___ of this (year) Act is to [state the
> purpose].

**When to include:**

- When the bill represents a significant policy shift requiring explanation
- When ambiguity in the operative provisions might be resolved by reference to stated intent
- When the political context requires a clear statement of purpose

**When to avoid:**

- When the purpose is self-evident from the operative provisions
- When an overly narrow purpose statement might limit the scope of otherwise broad provisions
- When the purpose statement conflicts with or limits operative provisions

**Caution:** Purpose statements can create unintended interpretive problems. A purpose
statement that is more restrictive than the operative text may narrow the statute's effect in
ways not intended.

## 9. Legislative Findings

Legislative findings are formal statements of facts or conclusions that the Legislative
Assembly finds to be true. They typically support the constitutionality or policy basis of
the legislation.

**Standard placement:** After the definition section and act name section.

**Standard language:**

> SECTION ___. The Legislative Assembly finds that:
>
> (1) [finding];
>
> (2) [finding]; and
>
> (3) [finding].

**When to include:**

- When the bill raises constitutional questions (especially commerce clause, due process)
- When factual predicates are needed to justify the regulation
- When there is a specific statutory requirement for findings (e.g., certain federal funding
  statutes require state legislative findings)

**When to avoid:**

- When findings are speculative or lack factual support (courts examine them)
- When the findings might be used against the state in litigation
- When findings create representations that could create legal obligations

**Important:** Legislative findings are not operative — they do not create rights or duties.
Courts give them weight in statutory interpretation but are not bound by them.

---

## 10. Cross-References to Other Definitions

When a definition in one part of the ORS is needed in a bill that covers related territory,
you may incorporate it by reference rather than repeating it:

> "Pesticide" has the meaning given that term in ORS 634.006.

This keeps definitions consistent across statutes and avoids maintaining duplicate definitions
that might diverge over time.

---

## 11. Defining by Enumeration

Sometimes a definition consists of a list of items rather than a general statement:

> "Controlled substance" means:
>
> (a) A substance listed in ORS 475.005;
>
> (b) A substance listed in schedule I through V under the federal Controlled Substances Act;
> or
>
> (c) A substance designated by the State Board of Pharmacy under ORS 475.035.

When using "means" with a list, the list is exhaustive. Make sure the list covers everything
intended.

---

## 12. Common Errors in Definitions

- [ ] Using "means and includes" — choose one
- [ ] Defining a term that ORS chapter 174 already defines the same way
- [ ] Using "individual" in a definition when "person" is needed, or vice versa
- [ ] Scope clause that is too broad (covers other statutes) or too narrow
- [ ] Definition section placed after substantive provisions (should be first)
- [ ] Defined terms not listed in alphabetical order
- [ ] Using quotation marks around defined terms throughout the operative text (only in the
     definition)
- [ ] Omitting the scope clause entirely ("As used in this section" etc.)
- [ ] Circular definition: "License means a license issued under this section."
- [ ] Policy statement that contradicts or is narrower than the operative provisions
- [ ] Legislative findings that are not verifiably factual
