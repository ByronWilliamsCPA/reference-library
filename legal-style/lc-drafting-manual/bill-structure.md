# Bill Structure — Oregon Legislative Drafting

> **Source**: Oregon LC Bill Drafting Manual, 2024 Edition (19th Edition), Chapters 5, 6, 10,
> 12, and 13
> **Scope**: Formal parts of a bill; bill organization; special clauses; effective dates;
> creating, amending, and repealing law

---

## Quick Reference

| Element | Rule |
|---------|------|
| Heading | "AN ACT" |
| Title | "Relating to [subject];" followed by special matter clauses |
| Single subject | Required — Article IV, section 20, Oregon Constitution |
| Enacting clause | "Be It Enacted by the People of the State of Oregon:" |
| Section numbering | SECTION 1., SECTION 2. (all caps, period) |
| Default effective date | January 1 of the year following passage (ORS 171.022) |
| Emergency clause | Takes effect on passage; prohibited for tax regulation acts |
| Repeal format | Body specifies the repeal; repealed text need not be set forth |
| Amendment format | Bracket deleted text in italics; bold new text |
| New law placement | "Added to and made a part of ORS chapter ___" |

## 1. Formal Parts of a Bill

Every bill contains these formal parts in this exact order:

1. Heading
2. Title
3. Enacting clause
4. Body (operative text)

Some bills also include a preamble between the title and the enacting clause, but this is
uncommon and rarely used in modern Oregon bills.

### 1.1 Heading

The heading is "AN ACT" in all capital letters, centered at the top of the bill.

```text
AN ACT
```

### 1.2 Title

The title begins with "Relating to" and states the subject of the bill in a phrase. The title
is not a summary — it simply identifies the subject. It follows the heading.

**Basic form:**

```text
Relating to dogs;
```

**With specific matter clauses:**

```text
Relating to dogs; amending ORS 609.010 and 609.020; and prescribing an effective date.
```

### Title Construction Rules

**"Relating to" always first.** The "Relating to" phrase comes before any specific matter
clauses.

**Specific matter clauses** announce specific actions taken by the bill. Common specific
matter clauses:

- "amending ORS ___" — when amending existing statutes
- "amending section ___ of this (year) Act" — when amending sections within the same bill
- "repealing ORS ___" — when repealing existing statutes
- "creating new provisions" — when adding entirely new sections not assigned ORS numbers
- "prescribing an effective date" — when the bill includes an effective date section
- "declaring an emergency" — when the bill includes an emergency clause

**ORS references in the title.** All ORS sections amended, repealed, or renumbered must be
individually listed in the title.

**Order in the title.** The "Relating to" phrase comes first. Specific matter clauses follow
in no fixed order, but "declaring an emergency" or "prescribing an effective date" typically
appear last. Clauses are separated by semicolons. The title ends with a period.

**Single subject rule.** Article IV, section 20, of the Oregon Constitution requires that a
bill embrace only one subject and its matters properly connected therewith. The "Relating to"
clause identifies that subject. A bill covering genuinely unrelated subjects violates this
rule. Connected matters (definitions, administrative provisions, penalties) that further the
single subject are not separate subjects.

**Revenue bills.** A bill for raising revenue must include in the title: "and providing for
revenue raising that requires approval by a three-fifths majority."

### 1.3 Enacting Clause

The enacting clause is always:

```text
Be It Enacted by the People of the State of Oregon:
```

This clause appears immediately after the title (or after the preamble, if one is used). It
is not numbered.

### 1.4 Preamble (Uncommon)

A preamble consists of "Whereas" clauses that explain the Legislature's reasons for enacting
the bill. Preambles are rare in modern Oregon bills. They are not operative text — courts give
them limited weight in statutory interpretation. When used:

- Each "Whereas" clause ends with a semicolon and "and"
- The final "Whereas" clause ends with "now, therefore,"
- No comma after "Whereas"
- The preamble precedes the enacting clause

## 2. Organization of the Bill Body

### Typical Order of Sections

Chapter 6 of the manual prescribes the standard order of sections in a bill. Where all these
elements are present, they should appear in this sequence:

1. Definition section ("As used in sections ___to___ of this (year) Act...")
2. Act name (short title) section
3. Policy or purpose statement
4. Legislative findings
5. General substantive provisions
6. Administrative provisions (powers and duties of agencies)
7. Procedures (hearings, reviews, appeals)
8. Penalty provisions
9. Saving clause(s)
10. Severability clause
11. ORS additions and renaming provisions
12. Repeal sections
13. Effective date / operative date section
14. Emergency clause (if applicable)

Not every bill contains all these elements. Most bills have only a few.

### One Subject

Place all provisions relating to the same subject in the same bill. If legislative counsel
determines a requester's proposal involves two unrelated subjects, two bills should be drafted.

### Temporary vs. Permanent Provisions

Temporary provisions (such as sunset provisions, operative dates, or transitional rules) must
be placed in separate sections from permanent provisions. Do not mix permanent law and
temporary law in the same section.

## 3. The Title — Detailed Requirements

### "Relating to" Phrase Scope

The "Relating to" phrase must be broad enough to encompass all substantive provisions of
the bill. A provision with no reasonable connection to the stated subject may be subject to
challenge. However, the phrase need not mention every aspect — "Relating to motor vehicles"
covers licensing, registration, traffic regulation, and safety requirements.

### Amending Existing Sections

When a bill amends existing ORS sections, each amended section must be listed in the title.

> Relating to housing; **amending ORS 90.100, 90.300 and 90.400;** and declaring an
> emergency.

### Adding New Sections Not Assigned ORS Numbers

When a bill adds entirely new statutory sections that will not be codified in the ORS:

> Relating to housing; **creating new provisions;** and declaring an emergency.

### When Both Apply

> Relating to housing; **amending ORS 90.100; creating new provisions;** and declaring an
> emergency.

### Referendum Clause

When a bill refers a measure to the voters, the title includes:

> "referring this Act to the people for their approval or rejection."

## 4. Special Clauses

### 4.1 Saving Clause

A saving clause preserves rights, remedies, proceedings, or contracts that would otherwise
be affected by the repeal or amendment of existing law. Saving clauses prevent the creation
of unintended retroactive effects.

**Standard saving clause (general):**

> SECTION ___. The repeal of [ORS section] by section___ of this (year) Act does not affect
> any right, liability or proceeding that existed before the effective date of this (year) Act.

**Preserving pending proceedings:**

> SECTION ___. Any proceeding pending before the Director under ORS___ on the effective
> date of this (year) Act continues under ORS ___ as amended by this (year) Act.

**Note:** ORS 174.070 provides that the repeal of a law does not affect pending actions or
rights accrued before the effective date of repeal. Drafters typically include explicit saving
clauses anyway because the statute may not cover every situation.

### 4.2 Severability Clause

A severability clause provides that if one provision of the bill is found unconstitutional,
the remainder survives.

**Standard severability clause:**

> SECTION ___. If any provision of this (year) Act or its application to any person or
> circumstance is held invalid, the invalidity does not affect other provisions or applications
> that can be given effect without the invalid provision or application.

**When to include:** Severability clauses are not always necessary — courts apply severability
analysis even without one. However, include one when the bill contains provisions that are
likely to face constitutional challenge and that are genuinely severable from the rest of
the bill.

**When NOT to include:** Do not include a severability clause if the unconstitutionality of
one provision would necessarily defeat the purpose of the entire act.

### 4.3 Construction Clause

A construction clause specifies how a bill should be read in relation to other law. Common
uses:

- To specify that the bill is cumulative with federal law
- To specify that the bill does not limit common law rights
- To state that the bill does not affect other pending legislation

> SECTION ___. Nothing in this (year) Act limits the authority of the [agency] under federal
> law.

## 5. Effective Dates

### 5.1 Default Effective Date

Under ORS 171.022, Acts of the Legislative Assembly take effect on January 1 of the year
following the year in which the session was held (i.e., the odd-numbered year after a
regular session), unless the bill provides otherwise.

Most bills do not need an effective date section because the default applies.

### 5.2 Specific Effective Date Section

When the bill should take effect on a date other than the default, include an explicit
effective date section:

> SECTION ___. This (year) Act takes effect on July 1, [year].

The effective date section is placed near the end of the bill, after repeal sections and
before the emergency clause (if any).

### 5.3 Emergency Clause — Takes Effect on Passage

An emergency clause makes the act take effect immediately upon the Governor's signature.
It is used only for genuine emergencies — situations requiring law to take effect before the
next January 1.

**Standard emergency clause:**

> SECTION ___. This (year) Act being necessary for the immediate preservation of the public
> peace, health and safety, an emergency is declared to exist, and this (year) Act takes
> effect on its passage.

**Prohibition:** Article IX, section 1a, of the Oregon Constitution prohibits using an
emergency clause on "any act regulating taxation or exemption." This is a distinct concept
from "bill for raising revenue" — it is broader and covers tax exemption legislation.

**Three-fifths majority:** A bill containing an emergency clause requires approval by
three-fifths of the members of each house.

### 5.4 Operative Date

An operative date differs from an effective date. The effective date is when the law takes
effect (i.e., when it is legally in force). The operative date is when the substantive
provisions of the law actually begin to operate or apply.

> SECTION ___. This (year) Act takes effect on the 91st day after the date on which the
> (year) regular session of the Legislative Assembly adjourns sine die.

Or for a specific operative date on a provision:

> SECTION ___. Sections 1 and 2 of this (year) Act apply to conduct occurring on or after
> January 1, [year].

### 5.5 Sunset Provisions

A sunset provision automatically terminates a law on a specified date. Sunset provisions
apply to the entire act or to specific sections.

Sunset a section by adding a subsection to the substantive provision:

> (**___**) This section is repealed on [date].

Or create a separate section:

> SECTION ___. Section___ of this (year) Act is repealed on January 2, [year].

The date is typically January 2 (rather than January 1) to allow for the filing of a final
report or action before termination.

## 6. Creating New Law

### 6.1 Adding New Sections to an Existing ORS Chapter

When a bill creates entirely new statutory law to be added to an existing ORS chapter, use
this standard language at the end of the substantive provision:

> SECTION ___. **Section ___ of this (year) Act is added to and made a part of ORS chapter
> ___ .**

If the new sections form a new series within the chapter:

> SECTION ___. Sections___ to ___of this (year) Act are added to and made a part of ORS
> chapter___ and are designated as a series beginning with ORS ___and ending with ORS
>___ .

### 6.2 New Sections Not Assigned ORS Numbers

Some new provisions are temporary, transitional, or otherwise inappropriate for codification.
These do not receive "added to and made a part of" language. They simply appear as numbered
sections in the bill without a codification instruction.

### 6.3 Internal References to New Sections

Within the same bill, reference new uncodified sections by their bill section number and the
year:

> As provided in **section 4 of this (year) Act** ...

### 6.4 New Chapter

If the new sections form an entirely new ORS chapter:

> SECTION ___. ORS chapter___ , as created by this (year) Act, is entitled "[title of
> chapter]."

## 7. Repeals

### 7.1 General Repeal Format

A repeal is accomplished by a section in the body of the bill. The text of the repealed law
is not set forth in the bill.

**Single section repeal:**

> SECTION ___. ORS 97.190 is repealed.

**Multiple section repeal:**

> SECTION ___. ORS 97.190, 97.200 and 97.210 are repealed.

**Range repeal:**

> SECTION ___. ORS 97.190 to 97.320 are repealed.

("ORS 97.190 to 97.320" means ORS 97.190 to 97.320, to and including the last section in
the range — see `numbering-formatting.md`.)

### 7.2 Conditional Repeal

When a repeal is conditional on some other event:

> SECTION ___. ORS 97.190, as in effect immediately before the effective date of this (year)
> Act, is repealed.

### 7.3 Saving Rights on Repeal

ORS 174.070 provides that repeal does not affect pending actions, vested rights, or accrued
penalties. But include an explicit saving clause when the situation calls for it (see
section 4.1 above).

## 8. Amendments to Existing Statutes

### 8.1 Amendment Format

When a bill amends an existing ORS section, the entire amended section must be set forth in
the bill. Oregon uses a bracket-and-bold convention to show changes:

- **Deleted text**: placed in brackets and italicized — *[like this]*
- **New text**: bolded — **like this**

**Example:**

> SECTION 1. ORS 609.010 is amended to read:
>
> 609.010. As used in ORS 609.010 to 609.190:
>
> (1) "Animal" means a dog, cat [or other domestic animal] **or livestock.**
>
> (2) **"Livestock" means cattle, horses, mules, donkeys, sheep, goats and swine.**
>
> [(2)] **(3)** "Owner" means the person having a property right in the animal.

### 8.2 Amendatory Language

The standard phrase for introducing an amended section:

> SECTION ___. ORS [section number] is amended to read:

After this phrase, the full text of the amended section appears, with brackets and bold
showing the changes.

### 8.3 Amending Multiple Sections

Each amended ORS section requires its own SECTION in the bill. Do not combine two different
ORS sections into the same bill section.

### 8.4 Amending New Sections Within the Same Bill

When a bill both creates a new section and then amends it in the same bill (double amending):

> SECTION ___. Section___ of this (year) Act is amended to read:

This is uncommon but occurs when a companion bill or contingency requires it.

### 8.5 Conflicting Amendments

When two or more bills passed in the same session amend the same ORS section differently,
Legislative Counsel resolves the conflict. If the drafter anticipates a conflict, include
a coordinating section specifying how the conflicts are resolved.

### 8.6 What Cannot Be Amended

A repealed statute cannot be amended — repeal a section in force. A previously repealed
section must be recreated as new law if it is to be revived.

## 9. Referendum Clause

A bill may refer the matter to voters for approval. The referendum clause appears at the end
of the bill before any emergency clause:

> SECTION ___. This Act is referred to the people for their approval or rejection at the next
> regular general election held throughout this state, in the manner provided by law, and if
> approved by the people it takes effect January 1 of the year following the general election.

A bill containing a referendum clause may not also contain an emergency clause — one makes
the act take effect immediately; the other defers it to a popular vote.

## 10. Ballot Measures vs. Bills

Initiative measures follow bill form with these differences:

- No measure summary or referendum clause
- Heading: "AN ACT" (statutory initiative) or "PROPOSED CONSTITUTIONAL AMENDMENT"
  (constitutional initiative)
- Internal year references: use "this (year of the general election) Act"
- Effective date: an approved initiative measure takes effect 30 days after the election
  (Article IV, section 1(4)(d))

---

## 11. Joint Resolutions

Joint resolutions are used for matters not requiring legislation — proposing constitutional
amendments, creating interim committees, directing state agencies. Key distinctions:

- Joint resolutions are not submitted to the Governor for signature
- Joint resolutions cannot make appropriations
- Resolving clause: "Be It Resolved by the Legislative Assembly of the State of Oregon:"
- Sections are designated as "(1)", "(2)" etc. (subsections), not "SECTION 1."

---

## 12. Common Structural Errors

- [ ] Title does not list all amended ORS sections
- [ ] Title does not include "creating new provisions" when new uncodified sections are added
- [ ] Emergency clause used on a tax regulation act (prohibited by Article IX, section 1a)
- [ ] Emergency clause used on a bill referring a measure to voters
- [ ] "Added to and made a part of" language missing for new codified sections
- [ ] Permanent and temporary provisions combined in the same section
- [ ] Effective date section missing when a non-default date is needed
- [ ] Amended section not set forth in full
- [ ] Repeal section missing from title
- [ ] Internal bill references use ORS-style numbering instead of "section ___ of this (year) Act"
