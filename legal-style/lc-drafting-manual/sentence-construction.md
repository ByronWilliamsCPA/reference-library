# Sentence Construction — Oregon Legislative Drafting

> **Source**: Oregon LC Bill Drafting Manual, 2024 Edition (19th Edition), Chapters 3 and 4
> **Priority**: Highest — these rules govern every sentence of every statute

---

## Quick Reference

| Rule | Standard |
|------|----------|
| Tense | Present tense throughout |
| Voice | Active voice; passive only when actor is unknown or irrelevant |
| Mandatory duty | "shall" |
| Discretionary permission | "may" |
| Condition precedent / passive imperative | "must" |
| Prohibition | "may not" (preferred over "shall not") |
| Serial comma | Omitted — "men, women and children" |
| "That" vs "which" | "that" = restrictive (no commas); "which" = nonrestrictive (use commas) |
| Person vs individual | "person" = includes entities; "individual" = humans only |
| Sex-neutral language | Required by ORS 174.129 |
| Provisos | Disfavored — use separate sentences or "except" / "notwithstanding" |

---

## 1. The Legislative Sentence

### Word Order

The legislative sentence follows a standard sequence of elements. When all elements are
present, they appear in this order:

1. Exception or limitation
2. Condition (case to which the rule applies)
3. Legal subject (the person or entity on whom the duty falls)
4. Legal action (the duty, permission, or prohibition)
5. Object and qualifications

**Example:**

> Notwithstanding ORS 97.130, if a person receives notice under subsection (2) of this
> section, the person shall file a written response with the department within 30 days.

The exception ("Notwithstanding ORS 97.130") comes first, then the condition ("if a person
receives notice"), then the legal subject ("the person"), then the legal action ("shall file"),
then the object and qualifications ("a written response with the department within 30 days").

### One Duty Per Sentence

Each sentence should impose one duty, grant one permission, or state one prohibition. If a
sentence requires conjunctive duties, consider using separate subsections in tabular form.

---

## 2. Tense — Always Present

Oregon statutes speak continuously and indefinitely. Use the simple present tense for all
operative provisions.

| Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|-----------|
| "A person who commits a violation..." | "A person who shall commit a violation..." |
| "The Director adopts rules..." | "The Director shall have adopted rules..." |
| "A license is valid for two years." | "A license shall be valid for two years." |

**Future tense** is acceptable only when the sentence genuinely refers to a future event:
> The Director shall submit the report by January 1 of each year.

---

## 3. Voice — Active Preferred

Active voice assigns the duty to a named legal subject and is almost always clearer than
passive. Passive voice obscures who bears the duty.

| Active (preferred) | Passive (avoid) |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| "The Director shall adopt rules." | "Rules shall be adopted." |
| "A licensee shall renew the license annually." | "The license shall be renewed annually." |

**Passive voice is acceptable** when the actor is genuinely unknown, irrelevant, or
deliberately omitted:
> "The fee shall be deposited in the General Fund."

Even in passive constructions, "must" is sometimes clearer than "shall" — see section 4.3
below.

---

## 4. Modal Verbs — The Four Terms

These four terms are the core of Oregon drafting. They are not interchangeable.

### 4.1 SHALL — Mandatory Duty

"Shall" imposes a mandatory duty on an identified legal subject. The subject must be capable
of performing the duty.

> The Department **shall** issue a license within 30 days after receiving a complete application.
>
> A person who violates this section **shall** pay a civil penalty of $500.

- Always active voice with "shall."
- "Shall" is inappropriate for conditions — use "must" for conditional requirements.
- "Shall" is inappropriate for things that are not duties of a person — do not write "The
  license shall be valid" (use "is valid" or "must be valid").

### 4.2 MAY — Discretionary Permission

"May" grants discretionary authority or permission. The actor chooses whether to act.

> The Director **may** waive the fee for good cause shown.
>
> A person **may** appeal the determination under ORS 183.482.

- Do not substitute "may" for "shall" to soften mandatory language.
- "May" creates permissive authority, not a mandate.

### 4.3 MUST — Condition Precedent or Passive Imperative

"Must" states a condition that must be satisfied before something else occurs, or creates a
passive imperative when no active legal subject can be identified.

**Condition precedent:**

> To qualify for the exemption, a person **must** submit an application before the deadline.
>
> A vendor **must** be licensed under ORS 670.600 to participate in the program.

**Passive imperative (when actor cannot be named):**

> The notice **must** be mailed at least 30 days before the hearing.

- "Must" in a condition precedent establishes what the actor must do to receive a benefit or
  avoid a penalty — it is not the same as "shall," which assigns a standalone duty.
- The distinction matters: "shall submit an application" = mandatory duty; "must submit an
  application" = condition to receive a benefit.

### 4.4 MAY NOT — Prohibition

"May not" is the preferred prohibition. "Shall not" is acceptable but disfavored.

> A person **may not** operate a vehicle without a valid license.
>
> The Director **may not** disclose the record without written consent.

**Why "may not" over "shall not":** "Shall not" is grammatically awkward because "shall"
signals a duty, and negating it creates the reading "it shall be the case that you do not" —
which is clumsy. "May not" directly removes permission.

---

## 5. Consistency

Use one term throughout the bill for each concept. Oregon drafting treats variation in
terminology as variation in meaning — courts will infer that different words mean different
things. Never vary word choice to avoid repetition.

- If you call it a "fee" in section 1, call it a "fee" in every subsequent section.
- If the actor is "the Director" in one provision, do not switch to "the Department" unless you
  mean to refer to a different actor.
- If you define a term, use it exactly as defined throughout — do not use synonyms.

---

## 6. Brevity

Omit words and phrases that add length without adding meaning.

| Verbose | Concise |
|---------|---------|
| "in the event that" | "if" |
| "at such time as" | "when" |
| "for the purpose of" | "to" |
| "in order to" | "to" |
| "by means of" | "by" |
| "with respect to" | "regarding" or "about" |
| "the sum of $500" | "$500" |
| "during the period of" | "during" |
| "prior to" | "before" |
| "subsequent to" | "after" |
| "no later than the time when" | "before" or "by" |
| "is authorized to" | "may" |
| "it shall be the duty of X to" | "X shall" |
| "shall have the power to" | "may" |
| "shall have the right to" | "may" |
| "shall have the duty to" | "shall" |

## 7. Tabular Arrangement

When a sentence contains multiple conditions, exceptions, or list items, break it into
subsections or paragraphs (tabular form) rather than running the elements together.

### When to Use Tabular Form

Use tabular arrangement when a sentence contains:

- Three or more items in a series
- Multiple conditions or exceptions
- Parallel alternatives that would be hard to read in a single sentence
- An enumeration of particulars to which a rule applies

### Tabular Punctuation Rules

- The introductory or "lead-in" line ends with a colon.
- Each item in the list ends with a semicolon, except the last item ends with a period.
- No "and" or "or" after each item except after the penultimate item (second-to-last).
- "And" signals all items must be satisfied (conjunctive).
- "Or" signals any one item is sufficient (disjunctive).
- Never mix "and" and "or" in the same tabulation without careful analysis.

**Conjunctive example:**

> To receive a license, a person must:
>
> (a) Submit a completed application;
>
> (b) Pay the required fee; and
>
> (c) Pass the examination.

**Disjunctive example:**

> The Director may suspend a license if:
>
> (a) The licensee fails to pay the annual renewal fee;
>
> (b) The licensee violates a rule adopted under this section; or
>
> (c) The licensee is convicted of a felony.

### Lead-In Rules for Tabular Sentences

The lead-in must grammatically connect to each item in the list. Read "lead-in + item" aloud
for each item — every combination must form a complete, grammatical sentence.

**Incorrect:**
> The Director may suspend a license:
>
> (a) Failure to pay the fee;

(Reading: "The Director may suspend a license failure to pay the fee." — ungrammatical.)

**Correct:**
> The Director may suspend a license if:
>
> (a) The licensee fails to pay the fee;

---

## 8. Conditionals — "If" vs "When"

- **"If"** introduces a condition that may or may not occur: "If the Director finds a
  violation..."
- **"When"** introduces a condition that will occur but the time is uncertain: "When the
  Director receives the application..."
- **"Whenever"** introduces a condition that may occur more than once: "Whenever a licensee
  changes address..."

Do not use "in the event that" — use "if."

---

## 9. Exceptions and Limitations

### "Except"

Use "except" at the beginning of a sentence to create a limited exception to a general rule.
The sentence following "except" states the general rule; the exception clause qualifies it.

> Except as provided in subsection (3) of this section, a person shall obtain a license before
> operating a business.

### "Notwithstanding"

Use "notwithstanding" to override a conflicting provision — it signals that the current
provision controls despite any contrary provision in the cited law.

> Notwithstanding ORS 183.310 to 183.550, the Director may adopt temporary rules without
> providing notice.

### Provisos — Disfavored

The proviso ("provided, however, that...") is a holdover from archaic drafting and should not
be used in Oregon bills. Provisos create ambiguity about whether they limit the entire
preceding sentence or only the immediately preceding clause.

**Replace provisos with:**

- A separate sentence: "However, the Director may waive the requirement..."
- An "except" clause at the beginning: "Except as provided in subsection (3)..."
- A "notwithstanding" clause
- A separate subsection

---

## 10. Enumeration of Particulars

When a general term is followed by specific examples, the examples control the scope of the
general term. This creates the canon expressio unius est exclusio alterius (the mention of one
thing excludes others).

**Problem:** "including" before a list can either expand or limit the general term.

**Rule:** Use "including but not limited to" only when the list is illustrative and the general
term should not be limited by the examples. If the list is exhaustive, do not use "including."

**Alternative:** Use a definition with "includes" — see `defined-terms.md`.

When listing duties or acts, prefer parallel grammatical structure throughout the list:

> The Director shall:
>
> (a) Adopt rules;
>
> (b) Issue licenses; and
>
> (c) Investigate complaints.

Not:

> The Director shall:
>
> (a) Adopt rules; ← verb phrase
>
> (b) Licenses shall be issued; ← passive sentence ← incorrect
>
> (c) Investigation of complaints. ← noun phrase ← incorrect

## 11. Modifiers

### Placement

Place modifiers immediately adjacent to the word they modify. A modifier placed ambiguously
may attach to the wrong referent.

**Ambiguous:**
> A person shall obtain a license from the Director, who must be qualified.

("Who must be qualified" — is this the person or the Director?)

**Clear:**
> A qualified person shall obtain a license from the Director.

### "Only"

"Only" is a troublesome modifier. Place it immediately before the word or phrase it modifies.

**Ambiguous:** "The Director may only issue a license if..."

(Does "only" modify "may," "issue," or "if..."?)

**Clear:** "The Director may issue a license only if..."

### Dangling Modifiers

Avoid participial phrases at the beginning of a sentence that do not modify the grammatical
subject.

**Incorrect:**
> After reviewing the application, the license is issued.

(The license is not reviewing the application.)

**Correct:**
> After reviewing the application, the Director issues the license.

Or better:

> The Director shall issue the license after reviewing the application.

---

## 12. "Which" vs "That"

**"That"** introduces a restrictive (defining) clause — one that is essential to identifying
which particular person or thing is meant. No commas around the clause.

> The license **that** the applicant submitted is incomplete.

(Without "that the applicant submitted," we would not know which license.)

**"Which"** introduces a nonrestrictive (descriptive) clause — one that adds information about
something already identified. Use commas to set it off.

> The application, **which** must be submitted on the department's form, requires three
> signatures.

(The application is already identified; the "which" clause adds descriptive information.)

**Test:** If removing the clause changes the meaning of the sentence, use "that." If the
clause could be omitted without changing the core meaning, use "which."

---

## 13. "Person" vs "Individual"

**"Person"** includes corporations, limited liability companies, partnerships, associations,
unincorporated organizations, and other legal entities, as well as human beings. This follows
ORS 174.100.

> A **person** who engages in the business of money lending shall obtain a license.

(Applies to businesses and individuals alike.)

**"Individual"** refers exclusively to a human being.

> An **individual** who participates in the program must be at least 18 years of age.

Use "person" unless you specifically intend to exclude legal entities. Using "individual"
where "person" is needed limits the statute to humans and may create unintended exemptions
for corporations.

---

## 14. Sex-Neutral Language

ORS 174.129 requires Oregon statutes to use sex-neutral language. Oregon drafting avoids
masculine or feminine pronouns as generic referents.

### Techniques

**Repeat the noun:**

> When a **person** receives the notice, the **person** shall respond within 30 days.

(Not: "When a person receives the notice, **he or she** shall respond.")

**Rewrite in plural:**

> **Persons** who receive notice shall respond within 30 days.

**Restructure the sentence:**

> A response must be filed within 30 days after receiving notice.

**Avoid:** "his or her," "he/she," "s/he," or alternating "he" and "she."

The most common Oregon solution is to repeat the noun. This can seem repetitive, but it is
unambiguous and consistent with drafting style throughout the Oregon Revised Statutes.

## 15. Numbers

### When to Spell Out Numbers

Spell out numbers that can be expressed in one or two words:

- One through ninety-nine: "twenty-five days," "forty"
- Round numbers: "one hundred," "five thousand"
- Numbers at the beginning of a sentence (or restructure to avoid leading with a numeral)

Use numerals for:

- Numbers that require three or more words: "125," "$2,500"
- Numbers used as codes, measurements, or technical values: "ORS 97.190," "Grade 8"
- Percentages: "10 percent" (not "10%")
- Money amounts: "$500," "$1,250,000"

### Money

Use "$" followed by numerals for amounts. Do not write "dollars and cents" — write "$12.50."
Do not write "the sum of $500" — write "$500."

### Fractions

Spell out simple fractions: "two-thirds," "one-half."

---

## 16. Punctuation Rules

### Colon in Tabular Lead-Ins

The introductory line in a tabular arrangement ends with a colon.

> The following persons shall register with the department:

### Semicolons in Lists

Each item in a tabular list ends with a semicolon. The last item ends with a period (or a
period after "and" or "or" following the penultimate item).

### No Oxford Comma

Oregon drafting omits the serial comma. Items in a series are separated by commas, with no
comma before the final "and" or "or."

> "A, B and C" (not "A, B, and C")
> "A, B or C" (not "A, B, or C")

### Parentheses Around Subsection Designators

Subsection designators in the body of the bill are always surrounded by parentheses:
(1), (2), (a), (b), (A), (B), (i), (ii).

In text that refers to a subsection, always include the full reference including parentheses:
"as provided in subsection (2) of this section."

### Em Dashes and En Dashes

Dashes are used sparingly in legislative drafting. When needed, use an em dash with no
surrounding spaces for a parenthetical interruption:

> The Director—acting through the department's enforcement division—may investigate.

## 17. Word Usage — Preferred and Prohibited Terms

### Preferred Substitutions

| Avoid | Use |
|-------|-----|
| "utilize" | "use" |
| "obtain" (when not literal) | "get" or rewrite |
| "commence" | "begin" |
| "terminate" | "end" or "expire" |
| "subsequent" | "later" or "after" |
| "prior" (adjective) | "earlier" or "before" |
| "pursuant to" | "under" or "as provided in" |
| "heretofore" | "before the effective date of this (year) Act" or specify the date |
| "hereinafter" | "in this section" or specify the reference |
| "herein" | "in this section" or "in this (year) Act" |
| "aforesaid" | identify by name |
| "said [noun]" | "the [noun]" or repeat the defined term |
| "such [noun]" | "the [noun]" or the defined term |
| "same" (as pronoun) | repeat the noun |
| "any" (when meaning "every") | "each" or restate the obligation |
| "shall be deemed to be" | "is" |
| "is authorized to" | "may" |
| "it shall be unlawful to" | "A person may not" |
| "on the part of" | rewrite to name the actor |

### "And/Or"

Never use "and/or." Use either "and" or "or" and choose the one that correctly states the
legal relationship. If ambiguous, use separate provisions.

### "Any"

"Any" is ambiguous — it can mean "every" or "some" depending on context. In most cases,
replace with the appropriate noun or a specific qualifier:

- "any person" → "a person" (general rule) or "each person" (each-and-every obligation)
- "any other provision" → identify the specific provision

### "Shall" for Non-Duties

Do not use "shall" for provisions that state a fact or describe a condition rather than
imposing a duty on a person. Use "is" or the simple present.

> "The effective date **is** January 1, 2025." (not "shall be")
> "The license **is** valid for two years." (not "shall be valid")

### "He or She," "His or Her"

Avoid. Use sex-neutral constructions (see section 14 above).

## 18. Capitalization

### Capitalize

- "Director" (and other official titles) when referring to the specific office: "the Director
  of the Department of Revenue." Do not capitalize when used generically: "Each agency
  director shall..."
- "Department," "Board," "Commission," etc. when used as the name of the specific body: "the
  Department of Consumer and Business Services."
- "Legislative Assembly," "Senate," "House of Representatives," "Governor."
- "State of Oregon" and "Oregon" when used as the legal name of the state.
- Defined terms are capitalized when used after definition (see `defined-terms.md`).

### Do Not Capitalize

- Generic role descriptions: "a peace officer," "a licensed contractor," "a resident."
- "act" when used generically; but "this (year) Act" when referring to the current bill.
- "section," "subsection," "paragraph," "subparagraph" — lowercase even in references:
  "as provided in subsection (2) of this section."
- "county," "city," "district" when used generally.

---

## 19. Spelling

Oregon drafting follows Webster's Third New International Dictionary as the standard reference.
When a word appears in multiple acceptable spellings, prefer the first entry in Webster's.

**Common style choices:**

- "judgment" (not "judgement")
- "acknowledgment" (not "acknowledgement")
- "employee" (not "employe")
- "canceled" (not "cancelled")
- "focused" (not "focussed")

---

## 20. Abbreviations

Abbreviations are generally not used in the body of a statute. Spell out words fully.

**Exceptions:**

- "ORS" for Oregon Revised Statutes — always use "ORS" in citations
- "U.S.C." for United States Code
- "$" for dollar amounts preceded by a numeral: "$500"
- "%" is not used — write "10 percent"

"Oregon Revised Statutes" is not spelled out each time — "ORS" is the accepted abbreviation
throughout the document.

---

## 21. Hyphenation

Compound adjectives used before a noun are typically hyphenated. Compound adjectives used
after a noun are typically not hyphenated.

> "A full-time employee" (hyphenated before noun)
> "The employee works full time." (no hyphen after noun)

Some compounds have become standard without hyphens: "law enforcement officer,"
"public health."

---

## 22. The "-Person" and Gender-Neutral Titles

Use gender-neutral compound terms: "chairperson" not "chairman." When a title already used in
statute is masculine or feminine, the sex-neutral language requirement of ORS 174.129 applies
and the drafter should use the neutral form going forward.

---

## 23. Common Errors Checklist

- [ ] "Shall" used for a condition rather than a duty (use "must")
- [ ] "Shall not" used for a prohibition (use "may not")
- [ ] "May" used where a mandatory duty is intended (use "shall")
- [ ] Passive voice without a named actor
- [ ] "Individual" used where entities must be included (use "person")
- [ ] Proviso ("provided, however, that") used instead of separate sentence or "except"
- [ ] "And/or" — replace with one or the other
- [ ] "Said" or "such" used as a pronoun — replace with the noun
- [ ] Future tense where present tense is correct
- [ ] Oxford comma present — remove it
- [ ] Modifier placement ambiguous
- [ ] Tabular list where "and"/"or" appears after every item (should be only after the
     penultimate item)
- [ ] Lead-in clause that does not grammatically connect to each list item
- [ ] "Which" used for a restrictive clause (use "that")
- [ ] "That" used for a nonrestrictive clause (use "which")
- [ ] Numbers spelled out when numerals are correct, or vice versa
