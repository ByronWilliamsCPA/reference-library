# Oregon Legislative Measure Formatting Rules

**Source**: LC Form and Style Manual for Legislative Measures, 2025-2026 Edition
**Scope**: Physical format and structure of bills, joint resolutions, concurrent resolutions,
resolutions, joint memorials, memorials, constitutional amendments, and amendments to measures.

---

## Quick Reference: Measure Types and Purposes

| Measure Type | Purpose | Adopting Body |
|-------------|---------|---------------|
| Bill | Creates, amends, or repeals law; appropriates money; prescribes fees | Both houses (signed by Governor) |
| Joint resolution | Proposes constitutional amendment; creates interim committee; gives agency directions | Both houses |
| Concurrent resolution | Affects operations of both houses; expresses commendation or sympathy | Both houses |
| Resolution | Affects single house procedures; expresses opinion on public matter | Single house |
| Joint memorial | Addresses Congress, President, or other governmental body | Both houses |
| Memorial | Addresses Congress, President, or other governmental body | Single house |

## 1. Bill Format and Structure

### 1.1 Formal Parts of a Bill

Every bill has four required formal parts, in this order:

1. **Heading** (bill number, LC number, session information)
2. **Title** (the relating clause and description of legislative action)
3. **Enacting clause**
4. **Body** (the substantive sections)

**Example of a complete bill structure**:
```
79th OREGON LEGISLATIVE ASSEMBLY--2017 Regular Session

                                     NOTE: Matter in boldfaced type in an amended section
                                           is new; matter [in brackets and italic type] is
                                           existing law to be omitted.

House Bill 3202
Sponsored by Representative SMITH

CHAPTER .................................................

AN ACT

Relating to employment; creating new provisions; amending ORS 659A.030.

Be It Enacted by the People of the State of Oregon:

SECTION 1. ORS 659A.030 is amended to read:
  659A.030. (1) ...
```

### 1.2 Bill Title (Relating Clause)

**Rule**: Every bill must have a title. The title serves as the single-subject identifier. It begins with "AN ACT" on a line by itself, followed by a new line beginning "Relating to" (lowercase "elating").

**Form**:
```
AN ACT
Relating to [subject]; [action clauses]; ...
```

**Rule**: The title contains one or more of these standard clauses:

| Clause | When Used |
|--------|----------|
| `creating new provisions` | Bill creates new law not amending existing ORS |
| `amending ORS {section}` | Bill amends existing ORS section(s) |
| `repealing ORS {section}` | Bill repeals existing ORS section(s) |
| `amending ORS {section} and repealing ORS {section}` | Bill both amends and repeals |
| `prescribing an effective date` | Bill specifies an early or delayed effective date |
| `declaring an emergency` | Bill includes emergency clause |
| `providing for referral to the people` | Bill includes referendum clause |

**Rule**: Amended and repealed ORS sections are listed in numerical order.

**Example**:
```
AN ACT
Relating to employment; creating new provisions; amending ORS 659A.030, 659A.040 and 659A.050;
repealing ORS 659A.060; declaring an emergency; and prescribing an effective date.
```

**Rule**: All clauses in the bill title are separated by semicolons. "And" precedes the last clause. A period ends the title.

**Rule**: The order of clauses in the bill title follows this sequence:
1. Relating to [subject]
2. Creating new provisions (if applicable)
3. Amending ORS [sections] (if applicable, in numerical order)
4. Repealing ORS [sections] (if applicable)
5. Declaring an emergency (if applicable)
6. Prescribing an effective date (if applicable)
7. Providing for referral to the people (if applicable)

### 1.3 Enacting Clause

**Rule**: Every bill must have an enacting clause immediately after the title. The enacting clause is always the same text.

```
Be It Enacted by the People of the State of Oregon:
```

**Rule**: The enacting clause is set on its own line. It is not numbered. No section number precedes it.

### 1.4 Body of a Bill

**Rule**: The body of a bill consists of numbered sections. Sections are designated "SECTION 1.", "SECTION 2.", etc. in all-capital letters, followed by a period.

**Rule**: The body is organized in this preferred order:
1. Sections that add new uncodified provisions
2. Sections that amend existing ORS (in numerical order of the ORS sections being amended)
3. Sections that repeal ORS sections (in numerical order)
4. Definitions section (if applicable, and if not part of the amended ORS)
5. Operative date provisions
6. Effective date or emergency clause (last section)

### 1.5 Typography Conventions in Bills

**Rule**: Bills use three typographic styles with specific meanings:

| Type | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Boldfaced** | New matter being added | **The employer shall provide notice.** |
| *[Brackets and italic]* | Existing matter being deleted | *[The employer may provide notice.]* |
| Lightface (roman) | Existing law being retained without change | The employee has the right to |

**Rule**: These conventions apply only to bills amending existing law. Entirely new sections (sections creating new law not amending an existing ORS section) are set entirely in boldfaced type.

**Rule**: In amendments to bills (floor or committee amendments), boldfaced type indicates new matter being inserted into the bill, and brackets-and-italic type indicates matter being deleted from the bill.

## 2. ORS Section Formatting in Bills

### 2.1 Setting Forth an Amended ORS Section

**Rule**: When a bill amends an ORS section, it must set forth the full text of the section as amended — not just the changed portions. The full section text appears under the amending clause.

**Rule**: The ORS section number appears at the start of the section text, set at the left margin in lightface type (because it is existing law). It is followed by a period and a space.

**Example of an amended section**:
```
SECTION 3. ORS 659A.030 is amended to read:
  659A.030. (1) It is an unlawful employment practice for any employer to refuse to hire,
discharge or discriminate against any individual in compensation or in terms, conditions or
privileges of employment because of race, color, religion, sex, sexual orientation, national
origin, marital status or age if the individual is 18 years of age or older.
  [(2) This section does not apply to an employer who employs fewer than six persons.]
  **(2)** **This section applies to an employer who employs one or more persons.**
```

### 2.2 Deleting Material from an ORS Section

**Rule**: Material being deleted from an existing ORS section is enclosed in square brackets and set in italic type: *[deleted text]*.

**Rule**: The brackets and the text between them are set in italic type. Do not use bold for deleted material.

**Examples**:
```
The [board] **commission** may adopt rules.           (replacing "board" with "commission")
The employer [may] **shall** provide benefits.        (replacing permissive with mandatory)
The employer shall [provide written notice].           (deleting a phrase entirely)
```

### 2.3 Inserting New Material into an ORS Section

**Rule**: All new material inserted into an existing ORS section is set in boldfaced type.

**Examples**:
```
The employer shall provide **written** notice.                     (inserting "written")
The **division** director shall adopt rules.                       (inserting new entity)
```

### 2.4 Internal Renumbering After Deletion or Insertion

**Rule**: When deleting or adding a subsection, paragraph, or subparagraph, the remaining units must be renumbered in the amended version. The old numbers appear in brackets-and-italic; the new numbers appear in boldfaced type.

**Example**:
```
[(3)] **(2)** The employer shall maintain records for three years.
[(4)] **(3)** Records shall be available for inspection.
```

### 2.5 Subsection, Paragraph, and Subparagraph Hierarchy

**Rule**: The hierarchy of subdivision levels in an ORS section is:
- **Section**: The top level (e.g., ORS 174.100)
- **Subsection**: Numbered (1), (2), (3), ...
- **Paragraph**: Lettered (a), (b), (c), ...
- **Subparagraph**: Capitalized letter (A), (B), (C), ...
- **Sub-subparagraph**: Lowercase Roman numeral (i), (ii), (iii), ...

**Example of full hierarchy**:
```
174.100. (1) The agency shall:
  (a) Review applications; and
  (b) Issue permits, which shall include:
    (A) The applicant's name;
    (B) The permit number; and
    (C) The expiration date, which shall be:
      (i) One year from issuance for temporary permits; and
      (ii) Five years from issuance for permanent permits.
```

### 2.6 Leadlines (Section Headings within ORS)

**Rule**: Most ORS sections have a leadline — a brief descriptive heading that appears before the section text. Leadlines are part of the ORS and must be included when the section is set forth in a bill, but they are **not** amended as part of the section unless the amendment changes the section's subject matter so substantially that the leadline is inaccurate.

**Rule**: The bill's amending clause does not need to mention the leadline separately.

**Rule**: If the leadline needs updating, include the leadline in the amended text with the appropriate typographic changes.

**Example**:
```
SECTION 3. ORS 174.100 is amended to read:
  174.100. [Definitions for ORS 174.100 to 174.130] **Definitions.** As used in ORS 174.100 to 174.130:
```

### 2.7 Source Notes in ORS

**Rule**: ORS sections often have a source note at the end showing the Oregon Laws citations from which the section was derived. Source notes are not part of the law and are not included in a bill when setting forth the ORS section text.

### 2.8 New Sections (Not Amending Existing ORS)

**Rule**: Entirely new sections that create new law (not amending an existing ORS section) are set entirely in boldfaced type, because all of the content is new.

**Example**:
```
SECTION 1. **The Environmental Justice Council is established, consisting of 13 members
appointed by the Governor. The council shall advise the Governor and state agencies on
matters relating to environmental justice.**
```

**Rule**: New sections do not have an ORS number assigned until after the session when ORS is updated. The bill section number (SECTION 1, SECTION 2, etc.) serves as the reference during the session.

### 2.9 Adding Sections to an Existing ORS Series

**Rule**: When a bill adds new sections that are intended to be part of an existing ORS series (a group of sections covering the same subject), the bill must identify where the new sections fit. The adding clause specifies the ORS section after which the new section is to be added.

**Form**:
```
SECTION {N}. ORS {last section of series} is amended by adding a new section to read:
```

**Rule**: If multiple sections are being added to the same series, each uses its own adding clause.

### 2.10 Blank Slug Flush (Series Placeholder)

**Rule**: When a new section is added to a series but the bill does not amend any existing member of that series, a "blank slug flush" section is used. This section contains only a blank line formatted to trigger codification at the correct location.

## 3. Bill Components: Special Clauses and Provisions

### 3.1 Definitions

**Rule**: When a bill defines terms for use within its provisions, the definitions section typically appears as the first substantive section (after any amending clauses for ORS sections that contain definitions for the same chapter). Defined terms are set in quotation marks on first definition.

**Form**:
```
SECTION 1. As used in this 2025 Act:
  (1) "**Agency**" means a state agency as defined in ORS 174.111.
  (2) "**Director**" means the Director of the Department of Environmental Quality.
```

**Rule**: If definitions are being added to an existing ORS section that already contains definitions for the same chapter, the new definitions are inserted into that existing section by amendment, not in a separate new section.

### 3.2 Name Change Provisions

**Rule**: When a bill renames an agency, position, or entity, a name change provision must reconcile the old name with the new name in all other statutes.

**Standard form**:
```
SECTION {N}. The [old name] is renamed the [new name].
```

Or, more commonly:
```
SECTION {N}. In ORS [range], the term "[old name]" is changed to "[new name]."
```

### 3.3 Effective Date

**Rule**: The normal effective date for an Act is January 1 of the year following the session year, established by ORS 171.022. This default applies when no effective date provision is included in the bill.

**Rule**: An "early effective date" is a date earlier than the normal January 1 effective date but at least 91 days after adjournment sine die (the constitutional minimum). Such provisions use this form:

```
SECTION {N}. This {year} Act takes effect on the 91st day after the date on which the
{year} regular session of the {ordinal} Legislative Assembly adjourns sine die.
```

**Rule**: A "delayed effective date" is a date later than January 1 of the year following the session year:

```
SECTION {N}. This {year} Act takes effect on January 1, {later year}.
```

**Rule**: Some effective dates are conditioned on a legislative referral (if the Act includes a referendum clause):

```
SECTION {N}. This {year} Act takes effect on the date on which House Joint Resolution {number}
({year}) is approved by a majority of voters at the {election} Election, {month} {day}, {year}.
```

**Rule**: The effective date section, if included, is the last section of the bill (or next-to-last if an emergency clause is also included).

**Rule**: The bill title must mention "prescribing an effective date" if the bill contains an effective date section.

### 3.4 Emergency Clause

**Rule**: An emergency clause makes an Act effective on the date of the Governor's signature (or on the date the bill becomes law without signature). Emergency clauses are used when there is an immediate need and the normal 91-day wait would cause harm.

**Standard emergency clause form**:
```
SECTION {N}. This {year} Act being necessary for the immediate preservation of the public peace,
health and safety, an emergency is declared to exist, and this {year} Act takes effect on its
passage.
```

**Rule**: The emergency clause is always the last section of the bill.

**Rule**: The bill title must include "declaring an emergency."

**Rule**: Bills with emergency clauses may not be referred to the people by referendum under Article IV, section 1, Oregon Constitution, unless the emergency clause is removed.

**Rule**: An emergency clause does not affect when the bill's substantive provisions become operative. If the bill's provisions are not meant to take effect immediately but only after some delay, use an applicable date or operative date instead of (or in addition to) the emergency clause.

### 3.5 Applicable Date vs. Operative Date vs. Effective Date

These three date concepts are distinct:

| Term | Meaning | Example use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Effective date** | Date the Act itself becomes law | The Act takes effect |
| **Operative date** | Date a specific provision within the Act begins to operate | A new program starts operating |
| **Applicable date** | Date to which a provision applies (may be retroactive) | "Applies to transactions on or after..." |

**Operative date form**:
```
SECTION {N}. Section {N} of this {year} Act first applies to [events] occurring on or after [date].
```

**Applicable date form**:
```
SECTION {N}. The amendments to ORS {section} by section {N} of this {year} Act apply to [events]
on or after [date].
```

### 3.6 Referendum Clause

**Rule**: A referendum clause refers an Act to the people for their approval or rejection. The standard form:

```
SECTION {N}. This {year} Act shall be submitted to the people for their approval or rejection at
the [Primary/General/Special] Election held on [date].
```

**Rule**: A companion joint resolution is often needed to describe the ballot title and explanatory statement for the referred measure.

**Rule**: Bills referring acts to a special election must identify the specific election date. See ORS 254.056 for primary and general election dates; see ORS 171.185 for legislatively prescribed special election dates.

**Rule**: The bill title must include "providing for referral to the people" (or similar language).

### 3.7 Savings (Application) Clause

**Rule**: A savings clause preserves existing rights, obligations, or proceedings that would otherwise be affected by an amendment or repeal. Common form:

```
SECTION {N}. The repeal of ORS {section} by section {N} of this {year} Act does not affect any
pending action, proceeding or liability under ORS {section} as it existed immediately before the
effective date of this {year} Act.
```

### 3.8 Severability Provision

**Rule**: Oregon statutes include a general severability provision at ORS 174.040, which applies to all acts. A bill-specific severability provision is **unnecessary and should not be included** unless there is a specific reason to override or supplement ORS 174.040.

### 3.9 Sunset (Limited Duration) Provisions

**Rule**: A sunset provision causes the repeal of specified provisions on a specified date.

**Standard sunset form**:
```
SECTION {N}. Section {N} of this {year} Act is repealed on [date].
```

**Rule**: For interim committees and task forces lasting only one interim, the sunset date is December 31 of the year following the session year:
```
SECTION 2. Section 1 of this 2025 Act is repealed on December 31, 2026.
```

**Rule**: For entities lasting longer than one interim, use a date that is January 2 of any year or June 30 of an even-numbered year.

### 3.10 Reports to the Legislative Assembly

**Rule**: When a bill requires an agency or other entity to report to the Legislative Assembly, the standard form is:

```
SECTION {N}. {The agency} shall report to the {committee} of the Legislative Assembly related
to {subject} in the manner provided by ORS 192.245 {by date}.
```

**Rule**: ORS 192.245 governs reports to the Legislative Assembly. Do not set forth its requirements in the bill — just cite ORS 192.245.

### 3.11 Appropriation Provisions

**Rule**: Standard appropriation provision form:

```
SECTION {N}. There is appropriated to the {agency}, for the biennium beginning July 1, {year},
out of the General Fund, the amount of ${amount} for the purpose of {purpose}.
```

**Rule**: Appropriation bills (budget bills) have their own separate numbering sequence. Regular bills that include appropriations use the same SECTION numbering as their other sections.

**Rule**: The purpose of an appropriation is often omitted from appropriation bills (when obvious from context), but may be included in regular bills that contain an appropriation provision.

### 3.12 Expenditure Limitation Provisions

**Rule**: Expenditure limitation provisions restrict how much an agency may spend from specified non-General Fund sources. Standard form:

```
SECTION {N}. The {agency} may expend, from {fund or account}, for the biennium beginning
July 1, {year}, not more than ${amount} for the purposes of {purpose}.
```

### 3.13 Lapse Language

**Rule**: When a bill appropriates money but needs to ensure that unspent funds revert to the General Fund at the end of the biennium (rather than being carried over), lapse language is added. Standard form:

```
SECTION {N}. Any moneys remaining in the {account} on June 30, {year}, that are unexpended,
unobligated and not subject to any conditions shall revert to the General Fund.
```

### 3.14 Preamble (Optional)

**Rule**: A preamble may be added before the enacting clause to state the legislature's findings or intent. Preambles are optional and rarely used in Oregon legislative practice. When used, the preamble consists of one or more "Whereas" clauses followed by the enacting clause.

```
WHEREAS it is the policy of this state to...;

WHEREAS current law does not adequately...;

now, therefore,

Be It Enacted by the People of the State of Oregon:
```

**Rule**: Preambles are not operative law — they are not codified and do not create rights or obligations. Substantive provisions must appear in the body of the bill.

## 4. Unit and Section Captions

### 4.1 Unit Captions

**Rule**: Bills organizing new provisions into named units (Parts, Subparts) may include unit captions. Unit captions are centered, in all-capital letters, and set in boldfaced type if new.

**Example**:
```
**PART 1**
**GENERAL PROVISIONS**
```

### 4.2 Section Captions

**Rule**: Individual bill sections may have a caption describing their content. Section captions appear immediately after the section number, set in parentheses.

**Example**:
```
SECTION 1. (Definitions.) As used in this 2025 Act: ...
```

**Rule**: Section and unit captions are used sparingly. They are appropriate when a bill creates a large, self-contained new scheme of law that will benefit from organization.

**Rule**: ORCP rules do not use unit and section captions — they use a lettered-section system instead.

## 5. Joint Resolution Format

### 5.1 Formal Parts of a Joint Resolution

1. **Heading** (joint resolution number, LC number)
2. **Title** (identifying subject and action)
3. **Resolving clause**
4. **Body** (numbered sections or clauses)
5. **Referendum clause** (if proposing a constitutional amendment)

### 5.2 Resolving Clause

**Rule**: Joint resolutions use a resolving clause instead of an enacting clause.

```
Be It Resolved by the Legislative Assembly of the State of Oregon:
```

### 5.3 Joint Resolution Proposing a Constitutional Amendment

**Rule**: A joint resolution proposing a constitutional amendment includes:
- An amending clause identifying the constitutional provision
- The amended constitutional text in full (with typographic conventions showing additions and deletions)
- A referendum clause

**Form of amending clause for constitutional amendment**:
```
SECTION 1. The Constitution of the State of Oregon is amended by adding a new section to be
added to and made a part of Article [X] to read:
```

Or, when amending an existing constitutional section:
```
SECTION 1. Section [N] of Article [Roman], Oregon Constitution, is amended to read:
```

**Rule**: Constitutional referendum clause (for legislative referral):
```
SECTION {N}. This amendment to the Oregon Constitution shall be submitted to the people of
Oregon for adoption or rejection at the [Primary/General] Election held on [date].
```

### 5.4 Constitutional Amendment Typography

**Rule**: The same typographic conventions as bills apply within a proposed constitutional amendment:
- New text: **boldfaced type**
- Deleted text: *[brackets and italic type]*
- Retained text: lightface

### 5.5 Adding a New Article to the Oregon Constitution

**Rule**: When proposing an entirely new article:

```
SECTION 1. The Constitution of the State of Oregon is amended by adding a new article, to
be numbered Article [Roman numeral] and entitled "[Title]," to read:
```

### 5.6 Constitutional Revision

**Rule**: A constitutional revision (as opposed to an amendment) is a comprehensive overhaul of the constitution or a major portion of it. Revisions require a different referral process under Article XVII, section 2, Oregon Constitution. The joint resolution uses a revising clause:

```
SECTION 1. Article [Roman numeral] of the Oregon Constitution is revised to read:
```

## 6. Concurrent Resolution Format

### 6.1 Formal Parts of a Concurrent Resolution

1. **Heading**
2. **Title** (optional; often just the subject)
3. **Resolving clause**
4. **Body**
5. **Sine die provision** (if applicable)

### 6.2 Resolving Clause

```
Be It Resolved by the Legislative Assembly of the State of Oregon:
```

### 6.3 Sine Die Provision

**Rule**: Concurrent resolutions for adjournment include a sine die provision:

```
SECTION 1. The {year} {Regular/Special} Session of the {ordinal} Legislative Assembly of
the State of Oregon is hereby adjourned sine die.
```

## 7. Resolution Format (Single-House)

### 7.1 Formal Parts of a Resolution

1. **Heading**
2. **Resolving clause** (house-specific)
3. **Body**

### 7.2 Resolving Clause — House Resolution

```
Be It Resolved by the House of Representatives of the State of Oregon:
```

### 7.3 Resolving Clause — Senate Resolution

```
Be It Resolved by the Senate of the State of Oregon:
```

## 8. Joint Memorial Format

### 8.1 Formal Parts of a Joint Memorial

1. **Heading**
2. **Introductory statement** (identifying recipients)
3. **Preamble** (Whereas clauses)
4. **Resolving clause** (address clause)
5. **Body**
6. **Copy distribution clause** (identifying who receives copies)

### 8.2 Address Clause

**Rule**: Joint memorials use an address clause naming the recipient:

```
To the President and the Congress of the United States of America:
```

### 8.3 Introductory Statement

**Rule**: The introductory statement identifies the nature of the document:

```
We, your memorialists, the Legislative Assembly of the State of Oregon, in legislative
session assembled, respectfully represent as follows:
```

### 8.4 Copy Distribution Clause

**Rule**: The copy distribution clause identifies who receives copies of the memorial.

**Standard form for congressional memorial**:
```
COPIES to the President of the United States, the Speaker of the House of Representatives,
the Majority Leader of the Senate and each member of the Oregon Congressional Delegation.
```

---

## 9. Memorial Format (Single-House)

### 9.1 Formal Parts of a Memorial

Same structure as joint memorial, but the resolving clause uses the single-house form:

```
We, your memorialists, the [House of Representatives/Senate] of the State of Oregon, in session
assembled, respectfully represent as follows:
```

## 10. Amendments to Measures (Floor and Committee Amendments)

### 10.1 Purpose and General Rules

**Rule**: Amendments to a bill or other measure change the text of the measure by specifying changes at specific page and line numbers of the printed measure. Amendments may not make "generic" changes — every change must be precisely specified by location.

**Rule**: Amendments must identify the specific version of the measure being amended. If a measure has been amended previously, the amendment must specify whether it is amending the original, the first engrossed, the second engrossed version, etc.

### 10.2 Page and Line Number References

**Rule**: Every amendment specifies the change by its location in the printed measure using page and line numbers.

**Forms**:
```
Page {N}, line {N}, ...
Page {N}, line {N}, delete "{text}" and insert "{text}"
Page {N}, line {N}, after "{text}" insert "{text}"
Page {N}, line {N}, delete "{text}"
Page {N}, lines {N} through {N}, delete
```

**Example**:
```
Page 2, line 14, delete "employer" and insert "person".
Page 3, lines 4 through 8, delete and insert:
"  (2) The commission shall adopt rules under ORS chapter 183 to implement this section."
```

### 10.3 Inserting New Material in Amendments

**Rule**: New material inserted into the bill text by an amendment is set in **boldfaced type** in the amendment document.

**Rule**: The amendment text itself uses quotation marks to set off the text being inserted:

```
Page 2, line 14, after "employer" insert "**or contractor**".
```

### 10.4 Deleting Material in Amendments

**Rule**: Material being deleted from a bill by an amendment is identified by quoting it within the amendment and marking it with *[brackets and italic]* or by simply identifying it for deletion.

**Examples**:
```
Page 2, line 14, delete "employer" and insert "person".

Page 3, lines 4 through 8, delete.

Page 4, after line 10, delete the following:
"  (2) The board shall adopt rules."
```

### 10.5 Restoring Bracketed Material

**Rule**: If a bill amendment proposes to restore text that appears in the bill in brackets-and-italic type (i.e., to un-delete matter the bill proposed to delete from ORS), the amendment:

```
Page {N}, line {N}, delete [*[text to be restored]*] and retain "text to be restored".
```

Or the amendment specifies that the brackets and italic formatting should be removed.

### 10.6 Inserting New Paragraphs

**Rule**: When an amendment inserts an entire new paragraph or subsection, the amendment specifies the location (after what existing text) and provides the full text in quotation marks.

**Example**:
```
Page 3, after line 10, insert:
"  **(3) The director shall submit an annual report to the Legislative Assembly in the manner
provided by ORS 192.245 on or before September 15 of each year.**"
```

### 10.7 Amending the Bill Title in an Amendment

**Rule**: Amendments sometimes require updating the bill title — for example, if an amendment adds a new ORS section to be amended. The bill title amendment specifies the exact change to the title text.

**Example**:
```
Title, line {N}, after "amending ORS 174.010" insert "; amending ORS 174.020".
```

### 10.8 Quotation Marks in Amendments

**Rule**: Quotation marks in an amendment document always indicate the exact text being inserted or that the amendment is quoting from the bill. Quotation marks that appear within the inserted text must be converted: double quotation marks in the bill text become single quotation marks within the amendment's quotation marks.

### 10.9 Bold and Lightface in Amendment Text

**Rule**: Within the amendment's quoted text (the material being inserted into the bill), use the same typographic conventions as in the bill itself:
- **Boldfaced**: New text being added to the bill (which may itself be new or amended law)
- *[Brackets and italic]*: Text being deleted from the bill
- Lightface: Existing text being carried over

### 10.10 Handling Numbers and Monetary Sums in Amendments

**Rule**: When an amendment changes a number or monetary sum that appears as numerals in the bill, specify the deletion and insertion precisely.

**Example**:
```
Page 3, line 7, delete "[$500,000]" and insert "**$750,000**".
```

### 10.11 Renumbering in Amendments

**Rule**: When an amendment inserts or deletes a subsection, paragraph, or other numbered unit, all subsequently numbered units in that section must be renumbered. The renumbering is specified in the amendment.

**Example**:
```
Page 3, line 15, delete "(3)" and insert "**(4)**".
Page 3, line 19, delete "(4)" and insert "**(5)**".
```

### 10.12 Blanks in Amendments

**Rule**: If a bill contains a blank (a space left for a number, date, or other information to be filled in later), the amendment specifies what to insert in the blank.

**Example**:
```
Page 4, line 3, insert "**$250,000**".
```

### 10.13 Amending Preambles and Whereas Clauses

**Rule**: Amendments to preambles (Whereas clauses) are handled the same as amendments to body text — by page and line number.

### 10.14 Conference Committee Amendments

**Rule**: Conference committee amendments are identified with a special caption and must be signed by all conference committee members who agree. They follow the same formatting rules as other amendments.

### 10.15 Amendments to Enrolled Bills

**Rule**: Amendments to an enrolled bill (a bill that has passed both houses) are permitted only in very limited circumstances. They require a resolution of both houses and are formatted differently from regular amendments.

### 10.16 Amendments to Joint Resolutions, Concurrent Resolutions, Resolutions, and Memorials

**Rule**: Amendments to resolutions and memorials follow the same page-and-line format as amendments to bills. However, the conventions for boldfaced and bracket-italic type apply only to the bill's text, not to the amendment document itself.

## 11. Double Amending

### 11.1 What Is Double Amending

**Rule**: "Double amending" occurs when a bill amends an ORS section that is already being amended by another provision in the same bill. This requires a special citation form.

**Rule**: Each amending section in a bill amends the ORS section as it will read after all previous amendments in the bill are applied. The amending clause references the "note version" — the ORS section as amended by the earlier bill section.

**Form**:
```
SECTION {N}. ORS {section} as amended by section {earlier N} of this {year} Act, is amended to read:
```

### 11.2 Double Amending Across Bills

**Rule**: When two different bills both amend the same ORS section, and the second bill to pass needs to incorporate changes from the first, the second bill's amending clause cites the ORS section as amended by the first bill.

**Form**:
```
SECTION {N}. ORS {section} as amended by section {N} of {House/Senate} Bill {number} ({year}),
is amended to read:
```

---

## 12. Series (ORS Sections Grouped in Sequence)

### 12.1 Definition of a Series

**Rule**: An ORS "series" is a consecutive or near-consecutive sequence of ORS sections covering a unified subject. The series is referred to by citing the first and last section: "ORS 174.010 to 174.100."

### 12.2 Adding Sections to a Series

**Rule**: When a bill adds a new section that is to become part of an existing ORS series, the bill uses an adding clause that places the new section after the last existing section of the series.

**Adding clause form** (when the series ends at a specific section):
```
SECTION {N}. ORS {last section of series} is amended by adding a new section to read:
```

**Rule**: When adding multiple new sections to a series, each new section uses its own adding clause.

### 12.3 "Unadding" (Removing an Added Section)

**Rule**: When repealing a section that was added to a series, the repealing clause must also update any internal references within the series that cite the section being repealed.

## 13. Uncodified Law (Session Law Not Going into ORS)

### 13.1 When Uncodified Law Is Used

**Rule**: Not all bill provisions are codified into ORS. Provisions that are temporary, administrative, or transitional in nature typically remain as uncodified session law. They appear in Oregon Laws but are not assigned ORS numbers.

**Common uncodified provisions**:
- Effective date sections
- Emergency clauses
- Sunset/repeal sections
- One-time appropriations
- Transition provisions
- Savings clauses

### 13.2 Citing Uncodified Provisions

**Rule**: During the session, uncodified provisions are cited as "section {N} of this {year} Act." After the session, they are cited as "section {N}, chapter {N}, Oregon Laws {year}."

### 13.3 Repeal of Uncodified Session Law

**Rule**: To repeal an uncodified Oregon Laws section, the repealing clause uses the Oregon Laws citation:

```
SECTION {N}. Section {N}, chapter {N}, Oregon Laws {year}, is repealed.
```

## 14. Measure Numbering and Introduction

### 14.1 Bill Numbers

**Rule**: Bills are numbered consecutively within each house as introduced:
- House Bills: HB 2001, HB 2002, ...
- Senate Bills: SB 100, SB 101, ...

**Rule**: Bills introduced by interim committees during presession filing begin at HB 2001/SB 100 (first session bill numbers). Other measures have different ranges depending on the session.

### 14.2 Resolution and Memorial Numbers

**Rule**: Joint resolutions, concurrent resolutions, house resolutions, senate resolutions, joint memorials, house memorials, and senate memorials are each numbered in their own sequence.

### 14.3 Appropriation Bill Numbers

**Rule**: General Fund appropriation bills (budget bills) have designated numbers reserved for them. The Oregon Department of Administrative Services prepares the initial budget bills.

### 14.4 Short Titles

**Rule**: A short title may be assigned to a bill at introduction. The short title appears on the bill back and in the measure summary. It is a brief, descriptive name for the measure.

**Example**:
```
Short title: "Oregon Clean Energy Act"
```

**Rule**: The short title is informational only. It does not appear in the body of the bill text unless the bill itself creates a statutory short title for the new act.

### 14.5 Bill Backs

**Rule**: The bill back is the cover sheet of a draft measure. It shows:
- Bill number
- Short title (if any)
- Primary sponsor(s)
- Requester(s) (if any)
- Committee (if committee measure)

**Rule**: Requesters are public agencies, private organizations, or individuals other than legislators who formally request that a measure be introduced.

## 15. Measure Summaries

### 15.1 Required Components

**Rule**: A measure summary must accompany every measure upon introduction. Every measure summary consists of:

1. **Digest** — A paragraph at the beginning that summarizes the measure's contents simply, with a Flesch readability score of 60 or higher.
2. **Body** — Additional paragraphs describing the measure's provisions in detail.

### 15.2 Digest Format

**Rule**: The digest is one paragraph beginning with the measure type and action:

```
Requires [entity] to [action]. Creates [new provision]. Amends [ORS/subject matter]. Sunsets on [date].
```

**Example digests**:
```
Prohibits employer from requiring employee to attend employer-sponsored religious meeting.

Allows Department of Environmental Quality to adopt rules establishing minimum standards for
air quality monitoring in urban areas.
```

### 15.3 Measure Summary Form and Style

**Rule**: Measure summaries use active voice. Suggested active verbs include: requires, prohibits, allows, creates, establishes, directs, authorizes, repeals, amends, provides, specifies.

**Rule**: Measure summaries do not include ORS citations.

**Rule**: Measure summaries are written in the present tense (not "the bill would require" but "requires").

**Rule**: Flesch readability score of 60 or higher is required for the digest paragraph.

### 15.4 Example Summary — Standard Bill

```
Requires employer to provide written notice to employee before changing terms or conditions of employment.
Specifies contents of notice. Provides that employer may not reduce compensation or benefits
without 30 days notice. Creates cause of action for employee whose employer violates section.
```

### 15.5 Example Summary — Bill Proposing Constitutional Amendment

```
Proposes amendment to Oregon Constitution to allow state to hold and dispose of stock received from
publicly created technology or invested in Oregon technology. Refers proposed amendment to people
for adoption or rejection at [date] Primary Election.
```

### 15.6 Example Summary — Bill Creating Interim Committee or Task Force

```
Creates Interim Committee on [Subject]. Directs committee to study [subject]. Sunsets committee
on December 31, [year].
```

### 15.7 Example Summary — Budget Bill

```
Makes appropriations from General Fund for biennium beginning July 1, [year], to [agency] for
purposes specified. Limits expenditures by [agency] from [fund].
```

## 16. Initiative Measure Formatting

### 16.1 Form of Initiative Measures Creating Statutory Law

**Rule**: Initiative measures that create or amend statutory law follow the same format as bills, with modifications to the heading and title.

**Heading example**:
```
INITIATIVE PETITION
Filed with the Secretary of State, [date]

AN ACT
Relating to [subject]; creating new provisions; amending ORS [section]; ...
```

**Rule**: The enacting clause for a statutory initiative is the same as for a bill:
```
Be It Enacted by the People of the State of Oregon:
```

### 16.2 Form of Initiative Measures Amending the Oregon Constitution

**Rule**: Initiative measures proposing constitutional amendments use a resolution format with a referendum clause.

**Form**:
```
INITIATIVE PETITION
Filed with the Secretary of State, [date]

A MEASURE
Proposing amendment to the Oregon Constitution relating to [subject]; and specifying an effective date.

Be It Resolved by the People of the State of Oregon:
```

## 17. Budget and Appropriation Bill Format

### 17.1 Special Budget Bill Format

**Rule**: General Fund appropriation bills (budget bills) follow a standardized format with:
- Agency-by-agency appropriation sections
- Expenditure limitation sections (for non-General Fund spending)
- Lottery allocation sections (where applicable)
- Position limitation provisions

**Rule**: Budget bills use a specialized section numbering and cross-reference system unique to appropriation legislation.

### 17.2 Appropriation Section Form

```
SECTION {N}. There is appropriated to the {agency}, for the biennium beginning July 1, {year},
out of the General Fund, the amount of ${amount}.
```

### 17.3 Expenditure Limitation Form

```
SECTION {N}. The {agency} may expend from fees, moneys or other revenues, including Miscellaneous
Receipts, but not including lottery funds, for the biennium beginning July 1, {year}, not more
than ${amount}.
```

## 18. Explanatory Statements for Ballot Measures

### 18.1 Purpose

**Rule**: An explanatory statement explains a statewide ballot measure in plain language for the voters' pamphlet. It is prepared by a Legislative Counsel Committee pursuant to ORS 251.225, or by a committee appointed by the Secretary of State.

### 18.2 Format of an Explanatory Statement

**Components**:
1. **Caption block**: LC number, measure reference number, drafting date and drafter initials
2. **Disclaimer**: "THIS EXPLANATION HAS NOT BEEN REVIEWED OR APPROVED BY THE LEGISLATIVE COUNSEL COMMITTEE" (until approved)
3. **Measure number** (assigned by elections officials)
4. **Ballot title** (short title of the measure)
5. **Introductory statement** (how the measure reaches the ballot)
6. **Heading**: "Explanation / By the Legislative Counsel Committee pursuant to ORS 251.225."
7. **Body text** (numbered paragraphs, plain language, 500-word limit)

### 18.3 Introductory Statement Forms

**Legislative referral to primary election**:
```
Referred to the Electorate of Oregon by the Legislative Assembly to be voted on at the
Primary Election, May __, 2___.
```

**Legislative referral to general election**:
```
Referred to the Electorate of Oregon by the Legislative Assembly to be voted on at the
General Election, November __, 2___.
```

**Referendum petition**:
```
Submitted to the Electorate of Oregon by Referendum Petition to be voted on at the
General Election, November __, 2___.
```

### 18.4 Word Count and Style

**Rule**: The body text may not exceed 500 words. Microsoft Word's word count tool is used (anything with white space around it counts as a word, including bullets and dashes).

**Rule**: Use "Ballot Measure {number}" in the first reference to distinguish the measure from a legislative measure. Second and subsequent references may use "measure" or "ballot measure."

**Rule**: It is unnecessary to identify ORS sections amended or repealed by the ballot measure in the explanatory statement (the ballot measure text follows in the voters' pamphlet).

### 18.5 Amendments to Draft Explanatory Statements

**Rule**: When LC drafts amendments to an explanatory statement, insert between the caption and the introductory statement a boldfaced centered heading:

```
PROPOSED AMENDMENTS TO
DRAFT MEASURE EXPLANATION
```

Use brackets-and-italic and boldfaced type to show changes.
