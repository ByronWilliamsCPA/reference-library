# Oregon Legislative Grammar, Style, and Punctuation Rules

**Source**: LC Form and Style Manual for Legislative Measures, 2025-2026 Edition
**Scope**: All grammar, style, punctuation, and usage rules governing Oregon legislative
drafting.
**Primary chapter**: Chapter 1 (Legislative Style), Parts I and II.

---

## Quick Reference: Most Critical Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|:-----|:-------|
| Serial comma | OMITTED. Write "A, B and C" not "A, B, and C" |
| Shall vs. may vs. must | "Shall" = duty; "may" = discretion; "must" = condition |
| Tense | Present tense always. Never future ("will") |
| Subject | "this state" not "Oregon" (in statutory text) |
| Gender | Gender-neutral always. No "he," "she," "his," "her" |
| Quotation marks | Punctuation goes OUTSIDE quotation marks (Oregon rule) |
| "To" in ranges | Means "to and including" (inclusive on both ends) |
| "Including" | Does not limit the meaning of what precedes it |
| Defined terms | Capitalize when using the defined term |
| Numbers | Spell out one through ten; use numerals for 11 and above |

## 1. Typography and Basic Format

### 1.1 Abbreviations and Acronyms

**Rule**: Spell out abbreviations and acronyms on first use, followed by the abbreviation in
parentheses.

**Example**:

```text
Oregon Revised Statutes (ORS)
Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ)
```

**Rule**: Abbreviations used routinely in the legislative context (ORS, ORCP, OAR) do not
need to be spelled out.

### 1.2 Capitalization — General

**Rule**: Oregon legislative drafting uses capitalization sparingly. Capitalize only:

- Proper names (names of specific persons, places, official bodies)
- Titles of specific acts, statutes, or documents
- Defined terms (when the term has been defined in the same statute or bill)
- The names of the DCAM capability areas when referring to those specific areas

**Capitalize**:

```text
the Legislative Assembly
the Department of Environmental Quality
the Governor
the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court
"Agency" (when defined in the same statute)
```

**Do not capitalize**:

```text
a state agency (generic, not defined)
the director of an agency (generic)
an appropriation (generic concept)
```

### 1.3 Capitalization — Official Titles

**Rule**: Capitalize the official title of a specific individual office when used as a title
preceding or in place of the person's name, or when referring to the specific statutory office.
Do not capitalize a title when used generically.

**Examples**:

```text
Capitalize: The Governor shall appoint...
Capitalize: The Director of the Department of Environmental Quality shall...
Do not capitalize: The governor of any state may...
Do not capitalize: Each director shall file an annual report.
```

### 1.4 Capitalization — Oregon Constitution References

**Rule**: Capitalize "Article," "section," and "subsection" when referring to a specific
constitutional provision.

**Examples**:

```text
Article IV, section 1, Oregon Constitution
subsection (2) of Article IV, section 1
```

### 1.5 Compound Modifiers (Hyphenation)

**Rule**: Hyphenate compound modifiers that precede a noun when needed for clarity. Do not
hyphenate compound modifiers that follow the noun they modify.

**Examples**:

```text
Hyphenate: full-time employee, long-term care, state-funded program
Do not: The program is state funded. The employee works full time.
```

**Rule**: Do not hyphenate common compound terms that function as single words:

```text
healthcare, cleanup, groundwater, rulemaking (no hyphens)
```

**Rule**: Age modifiers: hyphenate when used as a compound modifier before a noun.

```text
an 18-year-old applicant (hyphenated before noun)
The applicant is 18 years old. (not hyphenated after noun)
```

## 2. Grammar Rules

### 2.1 Shall, May, and Must

**Rule**: These three words have precise, distinct meanings in Oregon legislative drafting,
defined by ORS 174.100(6).

| Word | Meaning | Use for |
|:-----|:--------|:--------|
| **Shall** | Creates a mandatory duty | Duties imposed on persons or entities |
| **May** | Creates a discretionary authority or permission | Discretionary actions; granting of rights |
| **Must** | States a condition required to be met | Conditions precedent, prerequisites |

**Examples**:

```text
DUTY (shall):
The director shall adopt rules.
The employer shall provide written notice.

DISCRETION (may):
The agency may grant an extension.
The applicant may request a hearing.

CONDITION (must):
To qualify for the exemption, the applicant must file a written request.
A permit must be obtained before construction begins.
```

**Rule**: Never use "shall" where "must" is intended (conditions), or "must" where "shall" is
intended (duties). Never use "will" to express a duty or condition — use "shall" or "must."

**Rule**: "Shall not" and "may not" both mean prohibition, but "shall not" is preferred for
statutory prohibitions.

**Examples**:

```text
Preferred for prohibition:
The employer shall not require an employee to...

Acceptable alternative:
The employer may not require an employee to...

Avoid:
The employer cannot require an employee to... (informal, ambiguous)
```

### 2.2 Present Tense

**Rule**: Oregon statutes are always written in the present tense. Do not use the future tense
("will," "shall" as future) in substantive provisions.

**Standard (present tense)**:

```text
The agency adopts rules under this section.
An employer who violates this section is liable for damages.
```

**Non-standard (avoid)**:

```text
The agency will adopt rules under this section.
An employer who violates this section will be liable for damages.
```

**Rule**: "Shall" in modern Oregon drafting functions as a present-tense duty marker, not a
future-tense verb. When "shall" appears, it means the duty exists now (in law), not at some
future time.

### 2.3 Plurals

**Rule**: Use the plural form of nouns when writing general rules that apply to a class of
persons or entities, to avoid gender issues and awkward singular constructions.

**Standard (plural)**:

```text
Employers shall provide written notice to employees.
Applicants must submit documentation with their applications.
```

**Avoid (singular with pronoun)**:

```text
An employer shall provide written notice to his or her employee.
An applicant must submit documentation with his or her application.
```

### 2.4 Gender-Neutral Language

**Rule**: All Oregon statutes must use gender-neutral language. ORS 174.129 requires
gender-neutral language in statutory text.

**Rule**: Do not use: he, she, his, her, him, himself, herself (as indefinite pronouns)

**Approved strategies**:

1. **Use plural**: "Employees shall provide their names" (not "An employee shall provide his
   or her name")
2. **Restructure to avoid pronoun**: "An applicant who does not submit the form on time may be
   denied a permit" (not "He must submit the form")
3. **Repeat the noun**: "The director shall adopt rules. The director shall file the rules with
   the Secretary of State." (not "The director shall adopt rules. He shall file them.")
4. **Use "the person"**: "the person's name" (not "his or her name")
5. **Use passive where appropriate**: "The form must be submitted on time" (instead of "He must
   submit the form on time")

**Avoid**:

```text
he or she (acceptable only when unavoidable; prefer plural)
his or her (same)
s/he (never acceptable in statutory text)
```

### 2.5 Active vs. Passive Voice

**Rule**: Use active voice when the actor is identified. Use passive voice only when the actor
is unknown, unimportant, or when the object of the action needs to be emphasized.

**Active (preferred when actor is identified)**:

```text
The director shall adopt rules.
The applicant must file the form.
```

**Passive (appropriate when actor is unknown or unimportant)**:

```text
The form must be filed within 30 days.
Rules shall be adopted pursuant to ORS chapter 183.
```

**Rule**: Never use passive voice to hide who is responsible for performing a duty. If a duty
exists, identify who bears it.

### 2.6 Singular and Plural Constructions

**Rule**: When a statute applies to both singular and plural, the singular construction is read
to include the plural and vice versa under ORS 174.127. This means singular statutory text
covers plural situations without separately specifying both.

**Standard (singular)**:

```text
An employer who employs 10 or more employees shall maintain records.
```

This is read to apply to each employer (plural: all employers) who employ (collectively) 10
or more employees.

### 2.7 "Including" and "Including but not limited to"

**Rule**: "Including" does not limit the meaning of what precedes it. It introduces examples,
not an exhaustive list. Therefore, "including but not limited to" is redundant and should not
be used.

**Standard**:

```text
The department may take action, including assessing a civil penalty, revoking a license and
requiring corrective action.
```

**Avoid**:

```text
The department may take action, including but not limited to assessing a civil penalty...
```

### 2.8 "Such" as a Demonstrative Adjective

**Rule**: Do not use "such" as a demonstrative adjective in place of "the," "that," "this,"
or "those."

**Avoid**:

```text
Such employer shall provide notice.
The inspector shall document such violations.
```

**Standard**:

```text
The employer shall provide notice.
The inspector shall document the violations.
```

### 2.9 "Said" as a Demonstrative Adjective

**Rule**: Do not use "said" as a demonstrative adjective. Use "the," "that," or the specific
noun.

**Avoid**:

```text
Said employer shall comply with this section.
```

**Standard**:

```text
The employer shall comply with this section.
```

### 2.10 "Forthwith," "Herein," and Similar Archaic Terms

**Rule**: Avoid archaic legal terms. Use plain English.

| Archaic | Plain substitute |
|:--------|:----------------|
| forthwith | immediately |
| herein | in this section (or specify) |
| hereinafter | [specify the term being introduced] |
| aforesaid | [repeat the term] |
| whereas | [use in preambles only] |
| witnesseth | [never use] |
| shall be deemed | is (or: constitutes) |
| shall be construed | [restructure to avoid] |

### 2.11 "Notwithstanding" and "Subject to"

**Rule**: "Notwithstanding {citation}" means "despite what {citation} says" — i.e., the
section being drafted overrides the cited provision.

**Example**:

```text
Notwithstanding ORS 174.100, an employer with fewer than five employees may...
```

**Rule**: "Subject to {citation}" means "in accordance with and limited by {citation}" — the
cited provision controls.

**Example**:

```text
Subject to ORS 183.355, the director shall adopt rules.
```

**Rule**: Both "notwithstanding" and "subject to" must cite the specific provision being
displaced or controlling. Do not use them without a specific citation.

### 2.12 "To," "Through," and "To and Including"

**Rule**: "To" in a statutory range means "to and including" — the ending boundary is
included.

**Example**:

```text
ORS 174.010 to 174.100
```

This includes ORS 174.010, ORS 174.100, and all sections in between.

**Rule**: "Through" is not used in Oregon legislative drafting for ranges. Use "to"
exclusively.

**Rule**: Do not add "and including" — "to" alone conveys the inclusive meaning.

### 2.13 "Or More" and "Or Less" Constructions

**Rule**: To create an inclusive boundary (boundary included), use "or more" or "or less."

**Examples**:

```text
10 or more employees (means: 10, 11, 12, ...)
less than $500 (means: $499.99, $499.98, ..., but not $500)
$500 or less (means: $500, $499.99, ...)
more than $500 (means: $500.01, $500.02, ...)
```

### 2.14 "Any" vs. "Each" vs. "Every"

**Rule**: "Any" is ambiguous and usually means "one or more" in context. "Each" emphasizes
individual application. "Every" is similar to "each" but less common in modern drafting.
Prefer "each" for precision when meaning "each individually."

**Examples**:

```text
Precise: Each employer shall maintain records.
Acceptable: Any employer who employs 10 or more employees shall...
```

**Rule**: "Any" is appropriate when used to mean "regardless of type" or "one or more."

```text
An applicant may submit documentation in any format approved by the department.
```

## 3. Spelling and Usage

### 3.1 "This state" vs. "Oregon"

**Rule**: In statutory text, use "this state" rather than "Oregon" when referring to the State
of Oregon.

**Standard**:

```text
The board shall promote economic development in this state.
A person who is a resident of this state may apply.
```

**Avoid**:

```text
The board shall promote economic development in Oregon.
A person who is a resident of Oregon may apply.
```

**Exception**: Oregon may be used when referring to the state by name in a context where
clarity requires it (e.g., in titles, or when contrasting with other states).

### 3.2 "Moneys" vs. "Money"

**Rule**: Oregon legislative drafting uses "moneys" (plural) when referring to public funds
or appropriated amounts.

**Standard**:

```text
All moneys appropriated to the agency shall be deposited into the General Fund.
The agency may expend moneys received from fees.
```

**Avoid**:

```text
All money appropriated...
The agency may expend money...
```

### 3.3 "Persons" vs. "People"

**Rule**: In statutory text, use "person" (or "persons" in plural) rather than "people" when
referring to individuals subject to the statute.

**Standard**:

```text
A person who violates this section is subject to a civil penalty.
Persons employed by a state agency shall comply with this section.
```

### 3.4 Disability Terminology

**Rule**: Use person-first language when referring to people with disabilities. Oregon law
mandates respectful terminology.

**Standard (person-first)**:

```text
a person with a disability
a person who has a physical impairment
a person with a mental health condition
```

**Avoid (disability-first)**:

```text
a disabled person
a physically impaired person
a mentally ill person
```

**Rule**: Follow the terminology used in the relevant federal statutes (ADA, Rehabilitation
Act) for consistency with federal law.

### 3.5 "Fiscal Year" vs. "Biennium"

**Rule**: Oregon state government operates on a two-year budget cycle (biennium). Use the
correct term:

- **Biennium**: The two-year budget period (e.g., the biennium beginning July 1, 2025)
- **Fiscal year**: A one-year financial period (used in some contexts, particularly for agency
  operations within a biennium)
- **Biennial**: Adjective meaning "occurring every two years"

### 3.6 "May not" vs. "Cannot"

**Rule**: Use "may not" or "shall not" to prohibit. Do not use "cannot" in statutory text.

**Standard**:

```text
An employer may not require an employee to submit to a polygraph examination.
The director shall not approve an application that does not meet the requirements.
```

**Avoid**:

```text
An employer cannot require an employee to submit to a polygraph examination.
```

## 4. Punctuation Rules

### 4.1 Serial Comma — OMITTED in Oregon Legislative Style

**Rule**: Oregon legislative drafting omits the serial comma (Oxford comma) before "and" or
"or" in a list of three or more items.

**Standard (no serial comma)**:

```text
The agency shall review, evaluate and approve applications.
The penalty may include a fine, suspension of license or revocation of license.
The employer shall provide notice to the employee, the union and the department.
```

**Non-standard (avoid)**:

```text
The agency shall review, evaluate, and approve applications.
```

**Rule**: This is a firm Oregon convention that differs from many style guides. Always omit
the serial comma.

### 4.2 Colon

**Rule**: Use a colon to introduce a list that follows an introductory clause ending with "as
follows," "the following," or similar phrases.

**Example**:

```text
The application must include the following:
  (a) The applicant's name and address;
  (b) A description of the proposed project; and
  (c) The required fee.
```

**Rule**: Use a colon to introduce a definition after the defined term.

**Example**:

```text
"Agency" means:
  (a) A state agency; or
  (b) A local government.
```

### 4.3 Semicolon

**Rule**: Use semicolons to separate items in a list within a section (subsections,
paragraphs, etc.).

**Rule**: The last item in a list of subsections or paragraphs that are all required
(conjunctive) ends with "; and":

```text
(a) Item one; and
(b) Item two.
```

**Rule**: The last item in a list of alternatives (disjunctive) ends with "; or":

```text
(a) Item one; or
(b) Item two.
```

**Rule**: When a list ends with items that are neither all-conjunctive nor all-disjunctive,
the connector appears only before the last item.

### 4.4 Period

**Rule**: End each section, subsection, paragraph, and sub-unit with a period.

**Rule**: When a section has numbered subsections, each subsection ends with a period (not a
semicolon, even if the subsections are related).

**Rule**: The last paragraph in a list of paragraphs ends with a period.

### 4.5 Quotation Marks — Punctuation Placement (Oregon Rule)

**Rule**: In Oregon legislative drafting, punctuation following a closing quotation mark goes
**outside** the quotation marks when the punctuation is not part of the quoted material. This
is the reverse of the standard American typographic convention.

**Oregon standard (punctuation outside quotes)**:

```text
The term "employer" means...
Insert "and" before "the".
Delete "board" and insert "commission".
```

**Not standard American convention (avoid in legislative context)**:

```text
The term "employer," means...  [comma would be inside quotes in standard style]
```

**Rule**: This rule applies particularly in amendment drafting, where quoted text is being
inserted or deleted. The punctuation of the amendment instruction goes outside the quotation
marks.

### 4.6 Quotation Marks — Defined Terms

**Rule**: When a term is first defined in a statute, enclose it in quotation marks.

**Example**:

```text
(1) "Agency" means a state agency as defined in ORS 174.111.
(2) "Director" means the Director of the Department of Environmental Quality.
```

**Rule**: After a term is defined, do not use quotation marks around it in subsequent uses
within the same bill section or ORS chapter. Instead, capitalize the defined term to signal
that it has a specific statutory meaning.

### 4.7 Apostrophe

**Rule**: Form possessives in the standard way. Use apostrophe + s for singular nouns
(including those ending in s):

```text
the agency's rules
the director's decision
the witness's testimony
```

**Rule**: For plural nouns ending in s, use the apostrophe after the s:

```text
the employers' records
the agencies' reports
```

**Rule**: Do not use apostrophes in plurals (never "agency's" to mean "agencies").

### 4.8 Hyphen in Compound Numbers

**Rule**: Hyphenate compound numbers from twenty-one through ninety-nine when spelled out.

**Examples**:

```text
twenty-one
sixty-five
```

### 4.9 Em Dash and En Dash

**Rule**: Em dashes (—) are used to set off parenthetical phrases within a sentence. They are
used sparingly in statutory text.

**Rule**: En dashes (–) are not typically used in Oregon statutory text. Use "to" for ranges.

## 5. Numbers and Figures

### 5.1 When to Spell Out Numbers

**Rule**: Spell out cardinal numbers from one through ten. Use numerals for numbers 11 and
above.

**Examples**:

```text
Spell out: one, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight, nine, ten
Numerals: 11, 12, 25, 100, 1,000
```

**Exception — beginning of a sentence**: Spell out a number if it begins a sentence (or
restructure the sentence to avoid starting with a numeral).

**Exception — series**: When a series of numbers includes both numbers below 11 and numbers
above 10, use numerals throughout for consistency.

```text
The agency may employ 5, 10 or 15 inspectors. (all numerals for consistency)
```

### 5.2 Ordinal Numbers

**Rule**: Spell out ordinal numbers from first through tenth. Use numerals with suffix for
eleventh and above.

**Examples**:

```text
first, second, third, ..., tenth (spelled out)
11th, 12th, 25th, 83rd Legislative Assembly (numerals with suffix)
```

### 5.3 Monetary Amounts

**Rule**: Use numerals for all monetary amounts. Use the dollar sign ($) with no space before
the amount.

**Examples**:

```text
$500
$10,000
$1,250,000
```

**Rule**: Do not use ".00" for whole dollar amounts:

```text
Standard: $500 (not $500.00)
Use cents only when needed: $499.99
```

**Rule**: "Moneys" in dollar ranges: use "from $X to $Y" or "between $X and $Y" for ranges.

### 5.4 Roman Numerals

**Rule**: Use Roman numerals for:

- Oregon Constitution article numbers (Article IV)
- Oregon Senate memorial designation numbers
- Oregon Legislative Assembly session numbers (the 83rd Legislative Assembly)

**Rule**: Do not use Roman numerals for ORS chapter or section numbers, bill section numbers,
or subsection designations.

### 5.5 Percentages

**Rule**: Use numerals and the word "percent" (not the % symbol) in statutory text.

**Standard**:

```text
five percent (in general text)
15 percent (for numbers above ten)
```

**Exception**: The percent symbol (%) may be used in forms, notices, and tables.

### 5.6 Fractions

**Rule**: Spell out simple fractions in statutory text:

```text
one-half
two-thirds
three-fourths
```

**Rule**: Do not use numerical fraction notation (½, ¾) in statutory text.

### 5.7 Decimal Numbers

**Rule**: Use numerals and a period for decimal amounts:

```text
3.5 percent
2.25 acres
```

### 5.8 Time

**Rule**: Express time using the 12-hour clock with a.m. and p.m. (not AM, PM, or A.M.,
P.M.).

**Examples**:

```text
8 a.m.
noon
midnight
5 p.m.
8:30 a.m.
```

### 5.9 Dates

**Rule**: Express dates using the month name spelled out, followed by the day (as a numeral),
followed by the year.

**Examples**:

```text
January 1, 2025
July 4, 2026
```

**Rule**: Do not abbreviate month names in statutory text.

## 6. Defined Terms

### 6.1 Introducing Defined Terms

**Rule**: When a statute defines a term, the definition section must make clear what document
or chapter the definition applies to.

**Standard scope language**:

```text
As used in this section: ...
As used in ORS 174.010 to 174.100: ...
As used in this chapter: ...
As used in ORS chapters 174 and 176: ...
```

### 6.2 Definition Form

**Rule**: Each definition follows the form:

```text
"{Term}" means {definition}.
```

Or, when the definition has multiple parts:

```text
"{Term}" means:
  (a) {First meaning or example}; or
  (b) {Second meaning or example}.
```

### 6.3 Using Defined Terms

**Rule**: After a term is defined, capitalize it when used in the same statute or bill to
signal that it has the specific statutory meaning.

**Example**:
If "Agency" is defined to mean a state agency as defined in ORS 174.111, then use "Agency"
(capitalized) whenever you mean a state agency as so defined.

### 6.4 Definitions That Incorporate Other Definitions

**Rule**: A definition may incorporate another statute's definition by reference:

```text
"Agency" has the meaning given that term in ORS 174.111.
```

Or, when modifying a referenced definition:

```text
"Agency" has the meaning given that term in ORS 174.111, but does not include the Department
of Revenue.
```

### 6.5 "Means" vs. "Includes"

**Rule**: "Means" creates a complete, exhaustive definition. "Includes" is non-exhaustive.

**Standard**:

```text
"Person" means an individual, corporation, limited liability company, partnership, ...
```

**Rule**: Avoid using "includes" as the primary definitional verb. If the definition must be
non-exhaustive, use "means" with "including" to introduce examples:

```text
"Facility" means a place, structure or installation, including a factory, warehouse or
storage terminal, used in the production of hazardous materials.
```

## 7. Special Connotations and Usage

### 7.1 "This State" in Statutory Context

Covered under Section 3.1 (Spelling and Usage). Always use "this state" in statutory text,
not "Oregon."

### 7.2 "Official Titles"

**Rule**: Use official titles as established by statute or rule. Do not invent informal titles.
When citing an official's title, use the full official title on first reference.

**Examples**:

```text
Correct: the Director of the Department of Environmental Quality
Incorrect: the DEQ director (informal on first reference)
```

**Rule**: The shorthand form may be used after the full title is established:

```text
First reference: the Director of the Department of Environmental Quality (Director)
Subsequent: the Director
```

### 7.3 Forms, Notices, and Tables Within Statutes

**Rule**: Forms, notices, and tables set forth within a statute are set between hairline rules
(horizontal lines in the document). They appear as printed documents within the statute.

**Rule**: Blanks in forms and tables are indicated by a blank line (___) or by "[blank]"
notation in the draft.

**Rule**: The percent symbol (%) may be used in forms, notices, and tables even though
"percent" is spelled out in the surrounding text.

**Rule**: Figures (numerals) are always used in forms, notices, and tables even for numbers
below 11.

### 7.4 Interstate Compacts and Model Acts

**Rule**: Interstate compacts and model (uniform) acts that are enacted into Oregon law may
retain their own terminology even if it differs from standard Oregon drafting style. However,
any provisions added by Oregon (outside the compact or uniform act text itself) must follow
Oregon drafting conventions.

**Rule**: The text of a compact or uniform act is typically set off between hairline rules to
distinguish it from Oregon-added provisions.

### 7.5 "Ex Officio"

**Rule**: "Ex officio" means membership by virtue of the office held, not by appointment. Ex
officio membership ends when a person leaves the office. Ex officio status does not determine
whether a member is voting or nonvoting — those must be specified separately.

**Example (correct)**:

```text
The Secretary of State is an ex officio nonvoting member of the council.
```

**Example (incorrect assumption to avoid)**:

```text
The Secretary of State is an ex officio member of the council. [Leaves voting status ambiguous]
```

## 8. Formatting Rules for Listed Provisions

### 8.1 How to Format Subsections and Paragraphs

**Rule**: Each subsection, paragraph, subparagraph, and sub-subparagraph begins a new indented
line with the designating number or letter in parentheses.

**Example of full hierarchy**:

```text
174.100. (1) The agency shall:
  (a) Review each application submitted under this section; and
  (b) Issue a determination within 60 days after receiving a complete application.

(2) An applicant may request an extension of the 60-day period by:
  (a) Filing a written request; and
  (b) Paying the required fee.
```

### 8.2 Conjunctive vs. Disjunctive Lists

**Rule**: Use "and" to link items that are all required (conjunctive — all must be satisfied).
Use "or" to link alternatives (disjunctive — at least one must be satisfied). The connector
appears before the last item only.

**Conjunctive (all required)**:

```text
The application must include:
  (a) The applicant's name;
  (b) The project description; and
  (c) The required fee.
```

**Disjunctive (alternatives)**:

```text
A permit may be revoked if the holder:
  (a) Violates this section;
  (b) Provides false information in the application; or
  (c) Fails to pay required fees.
```

**Rule**: When neither "and" nor "or" appears at the end of the last-but-one item, the list is
read as "and" (conjunctive) by default. To be safe, always include the connector explicitly.

### 8.3 Ending Punctuation in Listed Items

**Rule**:

- Items in a list that are simple words or short phrases (not complete sentences) end with a
  semicolon, except the last item which ends with a period.
- When the last connector is "and" or "or," it appears after the semicolon of the
  second-to-last item.

**Example**:

```text
The board consists of:
  (a) The Governor;
  (b) The Secretary of State;
  (c) The Treasurer; and
  (d) Two public members appointed by the Governor.
```

### 8.4 "The Following" as an Introductory Phrase

**Rule**: When introducing a list with "the following," follow it with a colon. The list items
are then paragraphs.

**Example**:

```text
The department shall adopt rules establishing the following:
  (a) Minimum standards for applications;
  (b) Deadlines for submissions; and
  (c) Appeal procedures.
```

## 9. Style Conventions Specific to Oregon Legislative Drafting

### 9.1 "This (Year) Act" Self-Reference

**Rule**: A bill refers to itself as "this {year} Act" where {year} is the session year.

**Examples**:

```text
this 2025 Act
this 2023 special session Act
```

**Rule**: Do not use "this bill," "this legislation," or "this measure" in the substantive
text. The correct self-reference is always "this {year} Act."

### 9.2 Referring to Currently Pending Measures

**Rule**: When a bill cross-references another pending bill in the same session, use the full
bill citation including the year:

```text
House Bill 2001 (2025)
```

### 9.3 Singular Agency, Multiple Instances

**Rule**: When a statute is intended to apply to each member of a class of agencies or
entities, use the singular form ("the agency," "an employer"). Do not use "the agencies" or
"employers" as if addressing the entire class collectively, unless that is the intended
meaning.

### 9.4 "May Not" for Prohibition

**Rule**: For prohibitions, use "may not" or "shall not." Both are correct in Oregon
legislative drafting. "Shall not" is the more traditional form. "May not" is increasingly
common.

**Both acceptable**:

```text
An employer shall not discriminate against an employee...
An employer may not discriminate against an employee...
```

**Avoid**:

```text
An employer is prohibited from discriminating against an employee... [acceptable but wordier]
An employer cannot discriminate against an employee... [informal; avoid in statutory text]
```

### 9.5 Effective vs. Operative vs. Applicable Provisions

**Rule**: These three concepts apply to different aspects of a provision's timing:

- **Effective**: When the Act or provision becomes law
- **Operative**: When a provision begins to operate (may be later than effective date)
- **Applicable**: What time period or transactions the provision covers (may be retroactive)

Draft provisions to use the precise word for the intended meaning:

```text
Effective: This 2025 Act takes effect on its passage.
Operative: Section 3 of this 2025 Act first becomes operative on January 1, 2026.
Applicable: The amendments to ORS 659A.030 by section 2 of this 2025 Act apply to acts
occurring on or after the effective date of this 2025 Act.
```

### 9.6 "Thereof," "Hereof," "Herein," etc

**Rule**: Avoid these archaic pronoun-adverbs. Restate the referent clearly.

**Avoid**:

```text
The provisions hereof shall apply...
The Director thereof shall...
```

**Standard**:

```text
The provisions of this section apply...
The Director of the department shall...
```

### 9.7 Conditional Constructions ("If...then")

**Rule**: Conditional provisions use "if" to state the condition and the main clause to state
the consequence. The "then" is omitted.

**Standard**:

```text
If an employer fails to provide notice, the employee may bring a civil action.
```

**Not**:

```text
If an employer fails to provide notice, then the employee may bring a civil action.
```

### 9.8 "In the Manner Provided by ORS..."

**Rule**: This phrase is used to incorporate another statute's procedural requirements without
restating them. It means: follow the procedure specified in the cited statute.

**Example**:

```text
The agency shall adopt rules in the manner provided by ORS chapter 183.
The task force shall report to the Legislative Assembly in the manner provided by ORS 192.245.
```

**Rule**: Do not use "in the manner provided in this section" — use "under this section" or
"as provided in this section."

### 9.9 "As Provided in" vs. "Under"

**Rule**: "As provided in" and "under" both mean "in accordance with" and are generally
interchangeable in Oregon drafting. "Under" is more concise and preferred.

**Standard**:

```text
Rules adopted under this section shall...
A permit issued under ORS 468.065 may be revoked if...
```

**Acceptable alternative**:

```text
Rules adopted as provided in this section shall...
```

## 10. Avoiding Common Errors

### 10.1 Ambiguous "Which" and "That"

**Rule**: "That" introduces a restrictive (defining) clause. "Which" introduces a
nonrestrictive (descriptive) clause. Use "that" when the clause is essential to the meaning;
use "which" (preceded by a comma) when the clause merely adds information.

**Restrictive (that)**:

```text
A permit that has been revoked may not be reinstated.
```

(The "that" clause defines which permits — only those that have been revoked.)

**Nonrestrictive (which)**:

```text
The permit, which was issued in 2020, has been revoked.
```

(The "which" clause adds information about a specific permit already identified.)

### 10.2 "Ensure" vs. "Assure" vs. "Insure"

**Rule**: In Oregon legislative drafting:

- **Ensure**: Use to mean "make certain that" (ensuring an outcome)
- **Assure**: Use to mean "give confidence to" (assuring a person)
- **Insure**: Use only in the context of insurance

**Examples**:

```text
The department shall ensure compliance with this section.
The director shall assure applicants that their information is confidential.
A carrier must insure each vehicle covered under the policy.
```

### 10.3 "Effect" vs. "Affect"

**Rule**:

- **Effect** (noun): result, outcome; (verb, rare): to bring about
- **Affect** (verb): to have an impact on; (noun, rare): emotional state

**Examples**:

```text
Noun: The effect of this rule is to reduce processing time.
Verb (affect): This section affects all employers in the state.
Verb (effect, rare): The department shall effect the necessary changes.
```

### 10.4 Redundant Legal Phrases

**Rule**: Avoid common redundant legal doublets and triplets. One precise word is better than
multiple imprecise synonyms.

| Redundant | Preferred |
|:----------|:---------|
| cease and desist | stop (or: cease) |
| null and void | void |
| any and all | all (or: any) |
| each and every | each (or: every) |
| free and clear | free (or: clear) |
| terms and conditions | terms (or: conditions) |
| full and complete | complete |
| over and above | above (or: in addition to) |

### 10.5 Imprecise Time References

**Rule**: Avoid imprecise time references. Be specific about deadlines and time periods.

**Avoid**:

```text
as soon as practicable (unless the statute defines this or flexibility is intended)
promptly
in a timely manner
without undue delay
```

**Prefer**:

```text
within 30 days after receiving the application
on or before December 31 of each year
not later than 60 days after the effective date of this section
```

### 10.6 "Shall Be Guilty Of" and Criminal Offense Language

**Rule**: Modern Oregon statutes do not use "shall be guilty of." Use "commits {offense}" or
"violates this section" and specify the classification.

**Standard**:

```text
A person who violates this section commits a Class A misdemeanor.
A person who violates this section is guilty of a Class A misdemeanor.
```

(Both forms are acceptable in Oregon; the second is more traditional.)

### 10.7 "Appropriate" and "Sufficient"

**Rule**: These words are vague and hard to enforce. Avoid using them as the primary standard
in a statute unless flexibility is genuinely needed.

**Avoid**:

```text
The agency shall take appropriate action.
The employer shall provide sufficient notice.
```

**Prefer**:

```text
The agency shall revoke the permit, assess a civil penalty or take other corrective action.
The employer shall provide written notice at least 30 days before the change takes effect.
```
