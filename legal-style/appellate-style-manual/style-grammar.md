# Style and Grammar: Oregon Appellate Courts Style Manual

> **Source**: Oregon Appellate Courts Style Manual (Updated 2023)
> **Applies to**: Court opinions (Oregon Court of Appeals, Oregon Supreme Court, Oregon Tax Court)
> **Contact for questions not covered**: publications@ojd.state.or.us

## Quick Reference

- **Numbers 1-9**: spell out in text; use numerals when listing a series of like objects
- **Numbers 10+**: use numerals (except at the start of a sentence)
- **Serial (Oxford) comma**: required before the conjunction in a series of more than two items
- **"Since"**: time only — use "because" to explain why
- **"While"**: temporal only — use "although" for adversative sense
- **Passive voice**: avoid whenever possible
- **Contractions**: not permitted in opinions (only in quotations)
- **"Prior to"**: disfavored — use "before"
- **"Absent"** (as a preposition): disfavored — use "without" or "in the absence of"
- **"Cite to"**: redundant — use "cite"
- **Possessives**: singular words ending in "s" take 's (witness's testimony); singular entity names ending in "s" take only an apostrophe (General Motors' complaint)
- Official dictionary: **Webster's Third New Int'l Dictionary** (unabridged ed 2002)

## Section I: Spelling, Font, and Treatment of Words

### I.A — Use of Italics and Roman Typeface

#### Foreign Words

**Rule**: Some foreign words and phrases commonly used in legal writing are **not italicized** (they have been absorbed into English legal usage):

| Not Italicized | | |
|---|---|---|
| ad hoc | etc. | pro tempore |
| ad hominem | habeas corpus | quid pro quo |
| ad valorem | mandamus | remittitur |
| de facto | per curiam | subpoena |
| en banc | pro rata | vice versa |

**Rule**: Less common foreign words and phrases are **italicized**. When using such words in the body of an opinion, use the periods and diacritical marks as set out below:

| Italicized | | |
|---|---|---|
| *a priori* | *i.e.* | *quantum meruit* |
| *ab initio* | *id.* | *quantum valebant* |
| *ad litem* | *in camera* | *quo warranto* |
| *amicus curiae* / *amici curiae* | *in limine* | *res ipsa loquitur* |
| *arguendo* | *in re* | *res judicata* |
| *certiorari* | *in toto* | *respondeat superior* |
| *contra* | *inter alia* | *scienter* |
| *coram nobis* | *ipse dixit* | *sic* |
| *de novo* | *ipso facto* | *sine qua non* |
| *dictum* | *non sequitur* | *stare decisis* |
| *dubitante* | *nunc pro tunc* | *sua sponte* |
| *duces tecum* | *per se* | *sub silentio* |
| *e.g.* | *post hoc* | *ultra vires* |
| *et al.* | *prima facie* | *vice* |
| *et seq.* | *pro hac vice* | *vis-à-vis* |
| *ex parte* | *pro se* | *viz.* |
| *ex post facto* (but Ex Post Facto Clause) | *qua* | *voir dire* |

#### Signals

**Rule**: Introductory signals such as *cf.*, *accord*, *see*, *e.g.*, *compare* / *with*, and *see generally* are italicized. "See" is not italicized when it functions as a verb and not as a signal.

**Examples**:
> Signal: *See*, e.g., OEC 401.
> Verb: For discussion on the merits, see Smith, 300 Or App at 150.

#### Case Names and Other Authorities

**Rule**: Case names are italicized, as are the titles of books and other authorities.

**Rule**: When a case or other authority takes a possessive ending, the apostrophe and "s" are **not** italicized.
> *Miranda*'s holding has been called into question.

#### I.B.2 — Do NOT Use Uppercase for the Following

**a. Generic terms** such as "federal" or "state."
> the state, state constitution, federal constitution, statute of frauds, statute of limitations
> BUT: Federal Land Bank (part of a full proper name); State of Oregon

**b. The words "chapter" or "section"** when referring to a specific chapter or section within a sentence.
> ORS chapter 10; Article I, section 9.
> EXCEPTION: Chapter 7 (when referring to the Bankruptcy Code)

**c. The terms "x-ray," "horizontal gaze nystagmus," "administrative law judge," and "driving under the influence."**
> A horizontal gaze nystagmus (HGN) test
> The doctor ordered x-rays of the claimant's lower back.
> The administrative law judge (ALJ)
> He was convicted of driving under the influence (DUII).

**d. The seasons or centuries.**
> It is now okay to wear white in the fall after Labor Day.
> In the eighteenth century, women could wear white only as a petticoat.

**e. The words "website" or "internet."**

### I.C — Numbers and Dates

#### In General

**Rule**: Numbers one through nine are spelled out when used in text, except when listing a series of like objects.

**Example**:
> Judges from five states came to the conference: 5 from Washington, 2 from California, 10 from Oregon, 6 from Idaho, and 1 from Alaska.

**Rule**: All numbers after nine are expressed as numerals, except when they begin a sentence.
> Thirty-four judges attended the conference.
> Defendant raises 10 assignments of error.

**Rule**: Very large numbers are expressed in figures followed by million, billion, etc.
> 5 million people; 2 billion particles; $10 million

**Rule**: All ordinal numbers are spelled out when used in narrative.
> Plaintiff's twelfth assignment of error is not well taken.
> I chased my cat Jethro off the sofa for the one hundredth time.

**Rule**: Ordinal numbers consisting of more than one word are hyphenated if the corresponding cardinal number is hyphenated (e.g., "eighty-second" because "eighty-two" is hyphenated, but "seven hundred and twelfth" is not hyphenated). Ordinal numbers are expressed numerically to identify reports and courts in citations (e.g., 9th Cir).

#### Fractions, Decimals, and Ratios

**Rule**: Fractions appearing in nonquoted text alone or with numbers less than 10 are spelled out; fractions appearing with numbers 10 or higher are expressed as numerals.
> Four and one-half years old
> 23 1/4

**Rule**: When fractions are expressed as numerals, insert a space between the whole number and the fraction. Do NOT use a hyphen ("10 2/3," not "10-2/3").

**Rule**: Use numerals when using a decimal point.
> .08 percent blood alcohol content

**Rule**: Use numerals when denoting ratios.
> 4:1 or ratio of 4 to 1

**Rule**: Spell out numbers when denoting measurement.
> The subject was five feet eight inches tall.

#### Percentages

**Rule**: Spell out the word "percent" when used in text. Use the percent sign (%) in tables.
> Fifteen percent of the people in 1993 voted against the ballot measure.
> In 1995, 15 percent of the people voted against the ballot measure.

#### Time

**Rule**: Include minutes and a.m. or p.m.
> Oral arguments begin at 8:00 a.m. (not "8 a.m.")
> The defendant was last seen at 8:00 p.m. (not "8 o'clock in the evening")

#### Dates

**Rule**: Three-part dates are set off with a comma between the day and the year and, generally, a comma after the year.
> Defendant appeals from the January 16, 1994, order.

**Rule**: When indicating an inclusive period of time, omit the comma after the first year.
> Defendant was on probation from June 14, 1980 to July 30, 1982.

**Rule**: When referring to a date by month and day, do not use endings with the day.
> the September 19 hearing (not September 19th)

**Rule**: When indicating time by month and year only, there is no comma before or after the year unless the sentence structure requires one.
> Three lawyers attended the April 1990 deposition.
> The trial, which was scheduled for June 1990, was postponed several times.

**Rule**: When indicating a period of several years, use "to" or "through," not a hyphen.
> Judge Caspar was on the bench from 1900 to 1921.
> EXCEPTION: Hyphens are used for tax years. Do not repeat the "19" for inclusive years (e.g., tax years 1995-97, but tax year 1999-2000). For years ending in double "00," repeat the entire sequence (e.g., 2000-2001).

**Rule**: A decade does not take an apostrophe.
> 1980s (not 1980's)

**Rule**: Indications of time take an apostrophe.
> 24 months' incarceration (or 24 months of incarceration)
> six weeks' time (or six weeks of time)

#### Money

**Rule**: When referring to dollars, use the dollar sign; do not spell out "dollars."
> $5 million; $2 billion

**Rule**: Use $25.00 if there are other mixed dollar and cents amounts referred to; otherwise use $25 if standing alone.
> Plaintiff sought $10,000.00 in attorney fees and $875.45 in costs.
> The court entered judgment in the amount of $10,000.

**Rule**: Use a comma in numbers larger than three digits.
> $1,500

### I.D — Acronyms / Initialisms

**Rule**: Acronyms (read as a single word, e.g., AIDS) and initialisms (pronounced letter by letter, e.g., ORCP) are spelled out upon first usage, followed by the acronym/initialism enclosed in parentheses.
> Mountain View Hospital District (MVHD) filed a motion for summary judgment.

**Rule**: Periods are usually omitted from acronyms and initialisms to improve readability.

**Rule**: The article preceding an acronym or initialism depends on how the abbreviation is pronounced: if it begins with a vowel sound, use "an"; if it begins with a consonant sound, use "a."
> an LCDC order; an ORAP provision; an ORCP ruling
> a LUBA opinion; a Umatilla County sheriff

**Rule**: Add an "s" when forming a plural (SSNs, UFOs, MDs, CCRs); add an apostrophe to show possession (LUBA's analysis; PERS's liabilities).

---

### I.E — Titles and Offices

**Rule**: Full titles are uppercased; short references are lowercased (with exceptions as noted in Section I.B.1.b).
> Chief Justice Balmer
> chief justice of the Supreme Court
> Your Honor
> The Honorable Thomas A. Balmer
> Queen Mary → the queen
> Dr. Smith (becomes Smith after first reference)

### I.F — Abbreviations

**Rule**: Ph.D. ("Ph.D." is preferred over "PhD" with no periods.)

**Rule**: Corpus Juris Secundum is abbreviated as CJS without periods.

**Rule**: "JNOV" instead of "j.n.o.v."

**Month abbreviations** (no periods, when appearing in citations):
> January – Jan; February – Feb; March – Mar; April – Apr; May – May; June – June; July – July; August – Aug; September – Sept; October – Oct; November – Nov; December – Dec

## Section II: Punctuation

### II.A — Apostrophes

**Rule**: If a singular word ends in "s," add " 's " to show possession.
> George Harris's house; witness's testimony; Clauss's land; PERS's liabilities

**Rule**: If a name ends in "z" or "x," add " 's."
> Sanchez's argument; Rex's children

**Exception**: Singular entity names that end in "s" take only an apostrophe.
> Boy Scouts' answer; General Motors' complaint

**Rule**: Form the possessive of most plural words by adding only an apostrophe.
> the Joneses' driveway; the boys' gym

**Rule**: Plural words that do not end in "s" take " 's " to form the possessive.
> children's toys; women's tournament

**Rule — Two or more possessors**: If possession is separate, each possessor takes an apostrophe. If the possession is joint, only the last possessor takes an apostrophe.
> mother's and father's children (separate: each parent's children with another partner)
> mother and father's children (joint: children they have together)
> mother's and father's arguments (separate arguments)
> mother and father's arguments (joint arguments)

**Rule**: Use an apostrophe with indications of time.
> 36 months' incarceration; two weeks' time

**Rule**: Do NOT use an apostrophe with "attorney fees" or with initialisms used as plurals (IRAs).

**Rule**: When a case name or other italicized authority takes a possessive ending, the " 's " is not italicized.
> *Miranda*'s holding has been called into question.

**Rule**: Sometimes the use of " 's " can be awkward. Use "of" instead when appropriate.
> For "Church at 295 S. 18th St.'s analysis," consider "the analysis of Church at 295 S. 18th St."
> For "ORS 123.456(1)(a)'s wording," consider "the wording of ORS 123.456."

---

### II.B — Colons

**Rule**: Colons may be used to separate a grammatically complete sentence from, for example, another grammatically complete sentence or a list.
> The court announced a general rule: Attorney fees may not be awarded in the absence of an authorizing statute or contract provision.
> The officer found several items in defendant's apartment: a scale, five plastic bags containing white powder, and $751 in small bills.

**Rule**: Colons may **not** be used after a grammatically incomplete thought.

**Examples**:
> Incorrect: The panel consisted of: Judge Yunker, Judge Bauman, and Judge Keenan.
> Correct: The panel consisted of three judges: Judge Yunker, Judge Bauman, and Judge Keenan.
>
> Incorrect: Defendant argues: (1) the trial court erred in admitting evidence, (2) the error was not harmless, and (3) alternatively, the trial court erred in denying his motion for directed verdict.
> Correct: Defendant makes three arguments: (1) the trial court erred in admitting evidence, (2) the error was not harmless, and (3) alternatively, the trial court erred in denying his motion for directed verdict.

**Exception**: Colons may be used after verbs to introduce block quotations only.
> ORS 123.456 provides:
> "Now is the time for all good citizens to come to the aid of their country."

**Rule**: If the quotation is not blocked, use a comma.
> ORS 123.456 provides, "Now is the time for all good citizens to come to the aid of their country."

#### Use of Uppercase After Colons

**Rule**: If the material following a colon is not a complete sentence, it does not begin with an uppercase letter.
> The court excluded three items: a gun, a ukelele, and a garden hose.

**Rule**: If the material following the colon is a complete sentence, it does begin with an uppercase letter.
> The rain in Spain does not fall mainly on the plain: It mainly falls in the northern mountains.

---

### II.C — Commas

**Rule**: Use a comma only when you know why you are using one.

#### Items in a Series

**Rule**: A series of more than two items should be separated by commas. The courts' convention is to use the **serial comma** (i.e., a comma before the conjunction in a series of more than two items), because it eliminates the possibility of misreading.
> Sheila invited Ben's parents, friends, and coworkers.
> I agree that defendant's brief is well written, his analysis is cogent, and his arguments are persuasive.

**Rule**: A series of only two items should not be separated by a comma, regardless of the length of the items.
> Plaintiff contends that the trial court erred when it granted defendants' motion for summary judgment on her claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress and when it denied her motion to exclude witnesses and reporters.

**Rule**: It may be more clear to separate two items in a series with a comma when the items themselves contain multiple elements.
> In support of his motion, defendant cited Oregon and Washington cases, and state and federal regulations.

#### Compound Predicates

**Rule**: Compound predicates (series of verbs) should be treated like any other series: if there are three or more verbs, use commas; if there are only two, do not.
> The defendant objected to the introduction of Yost's testimony, asked the court to call a recess, and requested permission to file a memorandum in support of her objection.
> The court denied plaintiff's motion for a judgment notwithstanding the verdict and granted his motion for a new trial.

#### Compound Sentences

**Rule**: The coordinating conjunctions in compound sentences (and, but, or, nor) should be preceded by a comma.
> Paul organized the outing, and everyone had a good time.
> Defendant's brief presented a difficult argument, but Gina understood it better after she attended oral argument.

**Exception**: When the clauses are short and there is no danger of misreading, the comma may be omitted.
> Anna sang and Becca played the flute.

#### Complex Sentences (Restrictive vs. Nonrestrictive Clauses)

**Rule**: When the dependent clause is restrictive (necessary to the meaning of the sentence), do NOT use a comma. When the dependent clause is nonrestrictive (not necessary to the meaning), use a comma.

**Examples illustrating the difference**:
> Restrictive (no comma): John and Mary did not marry because they wanted money. (They married for some other reason.)
> Nonrestrictive (comma): John and Mary did not marry, because they wanted money. (They did not marry; the reason was wanting money.)

**Rule**: "That" is restrictive and is used without a comma; "which" is nonrestrictive and is used with a comma. See the "That/Which" entry in Section III.A.

#### Parenthetical Elements

**Rule — Interrupting parenthetical elements**: Either use two commas or no commas to set off parenthetical elements that interrupt a clause or phrase. Generally, longer parenthetical elements should be set off by commas; shorter elements may go without.

**Examples**:
> Correct: The state argues, as an alternative basis for its second assignment of error, that the trial court should not have suppressed the computer disk because it was in plain view when the officers searched defendant's apartment.
> Correct: The state argues alternatively that Article I, section 9, does not require suppression in these circumstances.
> Incorrect: The state cites as an alternative ground justifying suppression, Article I, section 9, of the Oregon Constitution.
> Correct: The state cites, as an alternative ground justifying suppression, Article I, section 9, of the Oregon Constitution.

**Rule — Introductory parenthetical elements**: The longer the introductory parenthetical element, the more helpful it is to set it off with a comma. If omitting a comma could lead to misreading, include it.
> Yesterday I finished cleaning out my desk.
> First, defendant objects to the denial of his motion to strike.
> When this case was before the trial court for the first time, defendant was sentenced to death.

**Rule — Setting out "Inc." before parentheticals**: Usually "Inc." is set out with two commas. But when "Inc." is followed by a parenthetical element, omit the second comma.
> Time, Inc., is the parent company.
> Defendants Borg-Warner Automotive, Inc. (Borg-Warner) and Tenneco Automotive Operating Company, Inc. (Tenneco) allegedly manufactured * * *.

#### Appositives

**Rule**: Whether to use commas with an appositive depends on whether it is restrictive or nonrestrictive.
> My sister Jill conducted firefighter qualification tests last weekend. [Jill is restrictive — the author has more than one sister]
> My mother, Doris, stands on her head every day. [Doris is nonrestrictive — the author has only one mother]

### II.D — Dashes

**Rule**: Dashes are most commonly used to amplify and explain ideas, digress from the main idea, or create an abrupt break or sudden change in a sentence.
> He spent several hours explaining his case–a case that he knew could not be won.
> The attorney–who had been waiting three hours for a ruling on the motion–entered the courtroom in an angry mood.

**Rule**: A space can be added before and after a dash when used as punctuation in the text of a slip opinion; however, when published in the Advance Sheets, those spaces will be removed. In slip opinions, use two hyphens "--" not the typographical "–" dash.

---

### II.E — Hyphens

#### Do Not Hyphenate

**a. Words beginning with the following prefixes**: anti, bi, bio, co, counter, extra, infra, inter, intra, macro, micro, mid, mini, multi, non, over, pre, pro, pseudo, re, semi, sub, super, trans, ultra, un, and under.
> antitrust, nonprofit, coworker, subconstitutional, pretrial
> BUT: mid-nineteenth century.

**b. "Factfinder".**
> The factfinder took note of the witness's demeanor.

**c. "Patdown" (when used as a noun).**
> The assailant put his hands over his head during the patdown.
> When used as a verb, it should be shown as two words: The officer pat down the suspect.

**d. Adjective forms of compounds in which the first word is an adverb ending in "ly."**
> her eagerly awaited homecoming; his totally incompetent performance

**e. Compound modifiers that appear after a verb.**
> Defendant's examination was court ordered.
> BUT: The court-ordered examination was inconclusive. [hyphen when before the modified word]

**f. Percentages when used as adjectives.**
> 10 percent increase, two percent annual pay raise

**g. The word "email."**

**h. The words "well taken" unless used as a compound modifier.**

#### A Hyphen Is Used

**a. To join a prefix to a word beginning with a letter in uppercase or a number.**
> un-American, Anti-Federalist, pre-1998

**b. To join a prefix to a main word when the second element consists of two or more words.**
> pre-latency-period therapy, non-work-related activities

**c. When the last letter of the prefix (usually a vowel) is the same as the first letter of the following word and the result improves readability.**
> infra-area, non-neutral
> BUT: preexisting

**d. To avoid confusion and misreading.**
> re-cover (to cover again), not recover (to get back or regain)
> re-cite (to cite again), not recite (to read or declaim)
> co-conspirator (not coconspirator); co-counsel (not cocounsel)

**e. With the prefixes "cross" and "post" (in conformance with the Oregon Rules of Appellate Procedure).**
> cross-assign, cross-appellant, cross-appeals
> post-conviction, post-trial, post-certification

**f. When using these specific terms:**
> 9-1-1; I-5; case-in-chief; policy-making; decision-maker; second-guess; ex-husband; self-defense; ex-wife

**g. With adjective phrases containing numerals or compound modifiers appearing before the word modified.**
> three-year-old victim, five-foot-tall tree, 18-year marriage, 25-year minimum term, third-party beneficiary, case-by-case basis, full-time work (but "he worked full time"), common-law wife, court-appointed attorney, first-degree murder, death-penalty case, penalty-phase proceeding; two- and three-year-old children, self-defense, right-of-way, time-barred (but "the claim is time barred")

**h. When adding prefixes to acronyms.**
> pre-ORCP

### II.F — Punctuating Lists

**Rule**: If a list is introduced by a complete grammatical thought, use a colon at the end of the introductory sentence. If the list is NOT introduced by a complete grammatical thought, a colon may NOT be used.

**Examples**:
> Correct: Defendant assigns error to three rulings: (1) the denial of his motion to disqualify Dr. Demento as an expert; (2) the denial of his motion to suppress chemical evidence; and (3) the granting of plaintiff's motion for a new trial.
> Correct: Defendant assigns as error (1) the denial of his motion to disqualify Dr. Demento as an expert; (2) the denial of his motion to suppress chemical evidence; and (3) the granting of plaintiff's motion for a new trial.
> Incorrect: Defendant assigns error to: (1) the denial of his motion to disqualify Dr. Demento as an expert; (2) the denial of his motion to suppress chemical evidence; and (3) the granting of plaintiff's motion for a new trial.

**Rule**: Items in a list may be, but are not required to be, numbered.

**Rule**: Items in a list may be separated by either semicolons or commas. If the items are long or contain internal punctuation, use semicolons.

**Rule**: The first item in a list should not begin with an uppercase letter, unless the items are full sentences.

---

### II.G — Punctuating Parenthetical Elements

**Rule**: Nonrestrictive parenthetical elements can be set off by commas, dashes, or parentheses:
- Commas: close logical and syntactic relation to the rest of the sentence
- Dashes: more remote relation
- Parentheses: still more remote

---

### II.H — Semicolons

**Rule 1**: Use a semicolon to separate two independent clauses not joined by a conjunction.
> The defendant refused to testify; he later was convicted of murder in the first degree.

**Rule 2**: If the elements in a series are long and complex or contain internal punctuation, separate them with semicolons rather than commas.
> Defendant was charged with three counts of rape in the first degree, ORS 163.375; two counts of sexual abuse in the first degree, ORS 163.427; two counts of rape in the third degree, ORS 163.355; and one count of sexual abuse in the third degree, ORS 163.355.

**Rule 3**: A semicolon precedes the words *however*, *therefore*, *hence*, *thus*, *accordingly*, and *besides* when used to introduce a clause. A comma follows those words.
> The Supreme Court reversed and remanded the case; therefore, the Court of Appeals had to decide the case for a second time.
> The trial judge refused to suppress the evidence; thus, defendant was convicted on all counts.

## Section III: Word Usage and Conventions

### III.A — Word Pairs

**A / An**

**Rule**: Use "a" before words beginning with a consonant, words with a pronounced h, or a long u sound (eu), or with words such as "one."
> a historical; a habitual; a union; a Eucharist; a one-time deal

**Rule**: Use "an" before words beginning with a vowel or an unsounded "h."
> an eagle; an appellate judgment; an hour; an heir; an honor

**A / From a**

**Rule**: Both "Defendant appeals a judgment" and "Defendant appeals from a judgment" are correct. Use the construction the authoring judge prefers.

**Absent / Without**

**Rule**: "Without" or "in the absence of" is preferred. Avoid using "absent" as a preposition.
> Correct: Without any arguments against doing so, the trial court dismissed the jury.
> Incorrect: Absent any arguments against doing so, the trial court dismissed the jury.

**Administrative Law Judge / Hearing Officer**

**Rule**: In Oregon since 2003, the title "administrative law judge" is now used.

**Affect / Effect**

**Rule**: "Affect" is a verb meaning to have an influence on, bring about a change in, or touch the emotions of.
> The trial was affected by the constant media attention.

**Rule**: "Effect" is most commonly a noun meaning something brought about by a cause (a result or outcome). As a verb, it means to achieve a result, to execute, or to cause to occur.
> Noun: Her closing argument had no effect on the jury's final decision.
> Verb: The attorney was unable to effect any change in the system.

**Allow / Grant**

**Rule**: "Allow" is used to refer to a ruling on a petition. "Grant" is used to refer to a ruling on a motion.

**Alternate / Alternative**

**Rule**: "Alternate" means every other or substitute; "alternative" means providing a choice.
> She takes early lunch during alternate weeks.
> An alternative approach is to take no lunch at all.

**Although / While**

**Rule**: "Although" more properly introduces an aside; "while" is more properly used only in a temporal sense.
> Although many people disagree, Ian thinks that coffee is good for you.
> While I was in Paris, I decided to become a can can dancer.

**Amicus Curiae / Amici Curiae**

**Rule**: *Amicus curiae* can be used as an adjective describing the brief, or as a noun designating the party or person. *Amici curiae* is the plural form. The number of parties, not the number of briefs, dictates the use of singular or plural. It is acceptable to use *amicus curiae* or *amici curiae* for the first reference and *amicus* or *amici* thereafter.

**Appeal / Review**

**Rule**: A party "appeals" from a lower court as of right. A party "seeks review" in court of the action of an administrative agency or when the higher court has discretion whether to take the case. Within an administrative system, the correct term is "appeal."

Related documents: "notice of appeal," "petition for judicial review" (administrative proceeding), "petition for review" (petition to the Oregon Supreme Court).

**Because / Since**

**Rule**: "Because" explains why; "since" expresses time.
> Because he attended seven basketball games, David had a busy weekend.
> Since moving to a window office, John has been much more productive.

**Between / Among**

**Rule**: As a general rule, "between" is used when the sentence involves two things or when expressing a close relationship of any number of individual things. "Among" is used when the sentence involves more than two things.
> The jury had to decide between the death penalty and life in prison.
> His time was divided between school, sports, and work.
> His present conviction was just one among many.

**Rule**: "Between" is used to express one-to-one relations of many things (e.g., a treaty between four powers). "Among" is used to express collective and undefined relations (e.g., She was the best among all the attorneys).

**Circuit Court / Trial Court**

**Rule**: When referring to the lower court in a particular case, use "trial court." Use "circuit court" when referring to circuit courts generally or to general propositions about particular courts. Also, use "circuit court," not "trial court," in tag lines.

**Cite / Cite to**

**Rule**: "Cite to" is redundant; "cite" is preferred.
> Correct: In Smith, the court cited ORAP 5.45.
> Incorrect: In Smith, the court cited to ORAP 5.45.

**Compose / Comprise**

**Rule**: The parts compose the whole; the whole comprises the parts.
> Correct: Judge Haselton, Judge Armstrong, and Judge Duncan compose Department One.
> Correct: Department One comprises Judge Haselton, Judge Armstrong, and Judge Duncan.
> Correct: Department One is composed of Judge Haselton, Judge Armstrong, and Judge Duncan.
> Incorrect: Department One is comprised of Judge Haselton, Judge Armstrong, and Judge Duncan.

**Consecutively to / Concurrently with**

**Rule**: A consecutive sentence is served "consecutively to" another sentence. A concurrent sentence is served "concurrently with" another sentence.

**Consistent With / Consistently With**

**Rule**: For adverbial uses, "consistently with" (that is, "in a manner consistent with") is correct.
> The court decided the case consistently with its precedent.

**Criterion / Criteria**

**Rule**: "Criteria" is plural; it may not be used when discussing only one factor.
> The court primarily relied on the first criterion.

**Datum / Data**

**Rule**: "Data" is plural and takes a plural verb.
> The data support plaintiff's theory.

**Decree / Judgment**

**Rule**: The distinction between law and equity was abolished in Oregon in 1980. The final determination in all civil actions is now titled a "judgment." The appellate courts will substitute "judgment" for "decree" even when trial courts or statutes use "decree."

**Disinterested / Uninterested**

**Rule**: "Disinterested" means lacking a stake in an outcome. "Uninterested" means bored.
> The judge appointed a disinterested arbitrator.

**Driver's License / Driving Privileges**

**Rule**: Different statutes use different terms; they are not interchangeable. Check the statute at issue for the correct term.

**Employe / Employee**

**Rule**: In older statutes and opinions, "employe" and "subpena" were used. It is correct to use those shortened spellings when directly quoting those sources or to bracket in the missing letters.

**Expedient / Expeditious**

**Rule**: "Expedient" means a practical solution (with overtones of side-stepping tough choices). "Expeditious" means speedy.
> The lawyers decided that it would be expedient to waive oral argument.
> The court must decide all cases in an expeditious manner.

**Fewer / Less Than**

**Rule**: "Fewer" denotes countable things; "less" denotes things that can't easily be counted. Use "less" for time, money, and distance.
> If you have 10 items or fewer, then you can go to the express lane.
> I have less money than you.

**Finding / Holding**

**Rule**: A "finding" is a factual determination; a "holding" is an application of law to particular facts.
> The trial court found that the officer lacked subjective probable cause.
> The Court of Appeals held (not found) that the search was invalid because the officer lacked subjective probable cause.
> The Supreme Court stated (not held) that probable cause has both an objective and a subjective component. [general statement of law unconnected to particular facts]

"Determination" can refer to either.

**Forgo / Forego**

**Rule**: "Forgo" means to do without. "Forego" means to go before.
> I would rather eat late than forgo lunch altogether.

**Implicitly / Impliedly**

**Rule**: "Implicitly" is preferred.

**In Light of / In the Light of**

**Rule**: "In light of" is preferred.

**Loath / Loathe**

**Rule**: "Loath" means unwilling or reluctant. "Loathe" means to strongly dislike or hate.
> I am loath to go to the meeting today.
> I loathe the thought of eating tofu.

**May / Might**

**Rule**: "May" expresses authority. "Might" expresses possibility.
> The Secretary of State may certify a ballot measure.
> The Secretary of State might resign tomorrow.

**Of / For**

**Rule**: Most authorities consider the following to be more correct:
> Defendant was convicted of three counts of assault.
> Defendant appeals a judgment of conviction for assault.

**Over / Under — More Than / Less Than**

**Rule**: Generally, "over" and "under" are used in spatial relationships; they do not take the place of "more than" or "less than."
> The attorney has more than (not over) $5,000 in outstanding legal fees.
> He had less than (not under) $20 in his pocket.

**Pleaded / Pled**

**Rule**: "Pleaded" is the preferred form.

**Portion / Part**

**Rule**: A "portion" is a piece of a whole that can be divided into shares (as an estate or a pie). A "part" is a more general term connoting something less than the whole.
> Jenny argued that she had been deprived of her rightful portion of the estate.
> Jim challenged the part of the judgment that prohibited wearing bow ties.

**Proved / Proven**

**Rule**: "Proven" is an adjective; "proved" is the accepted past participle of "prove."
> Her attorney is a proven winner.
> The prosecuting attorney has proved his case.

**Right / Authority**

**Rule**: Generally, individuals have "rights" (e.g., the right against self-incrimination); a governmental body or representative needs "authority" to act (e.g., whether a city had authority to take property by eminent domain).

**Since / Because**

See "Because / Since" above.

**That / This; Those / These**

**Rule**: "That" is used as a demonstrative pronoun to refer to thought expressed earlier. "This" is used when the referent is yet to be mentioned. "Those" and "these" are similarly distinguishable.
> The letter was unopened; that in itself casts doubt on the inspector's theory.
> This is what bothers me: We have no time to consider late applications.

**That / Which**

**Rule**: "That" introduces a restrictive clause (necessary to the meaning of the sentence). Commas are not used with a restrictive "that clause."
> The decision that the judge made regarding suppression of evidence was contrary to the legal standard.

**Rule**: "Which" introduces a nonrestrictive clause (can be omitted without changing the meaning). Commas are required with a nonrestrictive "which clause."
> The decision, which caught the legal community by surprise, was contrary to the legal standard.

**Rule**: The meaning to be conveyed, not grammatical differences, dictates when to use "that" and "which."
> The lawnmower that I borrowed is in the garage. (Correct if there are many lawnmowers — identifies which one)
> The lawnmower, which I borrowed, is in the garage. (Correct if only one lawnmower — adds information only)

**Who / Whom**

**Rule**: "Who" is used as a subject. "Whom" is used as an object of a verb or preposition. When in doubt, try rethinking the sentence using "he" or "him."
> Who is listening? (Is he listening?)
> Defendant, who was not present in the courtroom, was found guilty.
> He asked whom he should speak to. (He should speak to him.)

**Rule**: Use "that," not "who" or "whom," when the antecedent is not a person.
> The bank that Kym uses gives away free blenders.
> Christy sued the company that made the defective golf clubs.

### III.B — Word Functions

#### One Word or Two Words

**Rule**: "Case Law" and "Grid Block" are always two words (unless being quoted).

**Rule**: "Cannot" is used as one word, except when using a "not only" construction.
> We cannot reach unpreserved error.
> She can not only pitch, but she can catch, too.

**Rule**: Other words are usually spelled as one word:
> caseworker, factfinder, forestlands, handwritten, insofar, landowner, lienholder, online, overarching, passersby, pickup, policyholder, statewide, timeframe, timeline, timesheet, voicemail, website

**Rule**: Some words are one word or two depending on how they function in the sentence:
> The cleanup crew arrived early to start the clean up.
> The trial was set over until tomorrow. The defendant requested a setover.
> The setback requirement provides for a six-foot easement. The house was set back from the property line.
> Her mother provides day care both privately and at a local daycare center.
> The subject was pat down by the officer. The officer who performed the patdown testified.

#### Contractions

**Rule**: Appellate opinions are formal documents and do not use contractions. When quoting, however, if the original uses contractions, use those contractions in the quotation.

---

### III.C — Word Usage

**Language**

**Rule**: "Language" technically refers to English, French, Spanish, etc. More correct alternatives: "the wording of ORS 809.222" or "the text of the statute."

**Letter Opinion**

**Rule**: "Letter opinion" is preferred to "opinion letter."

**Only**

**Rule**: "Only" modifies the word or phrase that comes immediately after it. Place it accordingly to avoid ambiguity.
> The jurisdiction of the court is constrained only by statute. (the only thing limiting jurisdiction is statute)
> The statute pertains to only state actors. (the only actors to whom the statute pertains are state actors)
> The statute pertains only to state actors. (alternative phrasing for same meaning)

**Parameter(s)**

**Rule**: This word will almost never be correct in legal writing; it is a technical scientific and mathematical term. Use "limits," "boundaries," or "borders" instead.

**Parties, Victims, Children, and Law Enforcement**

**Rule**: Generally, refer to parties by their position in the lower tribunal (plaintiff, defendant, claimant, etc.). Exceptions:
1. Domestic relations cases: "husband" and "wife"
2. Civil commitment proceedings: position on appeal (appellant, respondent)
3. Termination of parental rights: "mother" and "father"; children referred to as "child" or "children" or by first initials
4. Juvenile delinquency proceedings: "youth"
5. Juvenile dependency proceedings: "child"
6. Adoption cases: "child"

**Rule**: When using a word indicating a party's role as a substitute for the party's name, do not use the definite article "the" (e.g., plaintiff, petitioner). When using an abbreviated name (not party designation), use "the" (e.g., "the state," "the superintendent").

**Rule**: Do not use the full names of victims. Acceptable references:
- the victim
- the first or last name, but not both
- an initial (need not be related to the victim's actual name)

**Rule**: In dependency and termination cases, and criminal cases involving a child victim or witness, do not use the names of children. Use an initial. For readability, use a single initial; if using two initials (to differentiate siblings), do not use periods (e.g., AG, AM).

**Rule**: In FAPA cases, stalking cases, EPPDAPA cases, registration of foreign restraining order cases, and SAPO cases, do not use the name of a person protected by a court order. Instead, use a role-based description ("petitioner") or an initial.

**Rule**: When referring to specific law enforcement personnel, use the correct title (e.g., officer, deputy, trooper, detective).

**Post-Conviction Cases**

**Rule**: "Post-conviction court" is preferred to "post-conviction trial court." The petitioner in a post-conviction case is referred to as "petitioner"; the defendant is referred to as "the superintendent" or "the state."

**Previous / Prior**

**Rule**: "Previous" and "prior" as adjectives are equally acceptable.

**Prior to / Before**

**Rule**: "Prior to" is disfavored; "before" is preferred.
> Correct: Wife withdrew joint funds from a checking account before filing for dissolution.
> Incorrect: Wife withdrew joint funds from a checking account prior to filing for dissolution.

**That (relative pronoun)**

**Rule**: "That" as a relative pronoun or relative adverb is sometimes suppressed in informal writing; the preferred practice in opinions is to include it.
> The court held that the evidence had been wrongly admitted.

### III.D — Variant Spellings

**Rule**: Use "kidnapping," not "kidnaping."

**Rule**: Use "marshalling," not "marshaling."

## Section IV: Common Grammatical and Style Problems

### IV.A — Collective Nouns

**Rule**: Collective nouns require singular verbs when the group is functioning as a unit. When individual members of the group are acting independently, a plural verb is used. If a sentence seems awkward, insert the words "members of" before the collective noun and use the plural verb.

**Common examples**:
| Noun | Unit (singular) | Members (plural) |
|---|---|---|
| court | The court is not in session today. | The members of the court are in conference. |
| majority | The majority states that the assignment of error was not preserved. | — |
| jury | The jury has adjourned for the day. | The members of the jury have adjourned. |
| council | The Council on Court Procedures has adopted new rules. | The members of the council have adopted new rules. |

---

### IV.B — Parallel Construction

**Rule**: Sentence parts must match if a sentence is to make logical sense. Parts of a sentence that are parallel in meaning are parallel in structure.

**Examples**:
> Incorrect: The court held that the taxpayer was guilty of failing to report income, claiming fraudulent deductions, and in the treatment of ordinary income as capital gain.
> Correct: The court held that the taxpayer was guilty of failing to report income, claiming fraudulent deductions, and treating ordinary income as capital gain.

---

### IV.C — Passive Voice

**Rule**: Avoid using the passive voice whenever possible. The passive voice can create ambiguity about who is doing the acting in a sentence.

**Example**:
> See State ex rel Click v. Brownhill, 331 Or 500, 509, 15 P3d 990 (2000) (Durham, J., concurring) (ambiguity in statute arose because of the use of the phrase "shall not be used"; through use of passive voice, legislature failed to identify who "shall not use").

---

### IV.D — Verbs

#### Was/Were Agreement

> Ringo Starr argued the cause for the appellant. With him on the brief was The Beatles LLP. [singular]
> Mick Jagger argued the cause for respondent. With him on the brief were Keith Richards and the Rolling Stones, PC. [plural]

#### Past Perfect Tense

**Rule**: When referring to an action completed before another past time, use the past perfect tense.
> Incorrect: Petitioner testified at the post-conviction hearing that he asked his trial lawyer to investigate his alibi defense.
> Correct: Petitioner testified at the post-conviction hearing that he had asked his trial lawyer to investigate his alibi defense.

**Rule**: Past-perfect tense might not be necessary when describing testimony about an event without reference to another past time (e.g., "John testified that the light was green").

#### Subjunctive Mood

**Rule**: Use the subjunctive to express a condition that is contrary to fact or hypothetical.
> If Mary were queen of the world, then everyone would want to obey her.
> If the court were to take notice of defendant's arguments, then it would ignore long-standing principles of preservation.

**Rule**: Not every "if" clause takes the subjunctive.
> If defendant was a resident of Oregon in 1999, then he must pay taxes for that year. [not contrary to fact]

---

### IV.E — Active Voice

**Rule**: In the active voice, the subject of a sentence or clause performs the action of the verb. In the passive voice, the subject is not the actor. Generally, the passive voice is wordier and may be vague. The active voice is clearer and stronger and tells who did what to whom.

**Examples**:
> Active: I missed the deadline.
> Passive: The deadline was missed.

---

### IV.F — Gender-Neutral Wording

**Rule**: Gender-neutral terms are preferred, and gender-based pronouns are avoided except when referring to a specific person. Use "he or she" only when all other constructions fail.

**Examples**:
> letter carrier, not mailman
> flight attendant, not stewardess
> worker, not workman
> sales clerk, not salesman

---

### IV.G — Informal or Technical Terminology

**Rule**: An appellate opinion is a formal document. Instead of "said" or "says," use "states" or "stated" or one of the following words, depending on context:

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| adds | emphasizes | observes |
| argues | establishes | opines |
| compares | explains | points out |
| concludes | finds | posits |
| continues | insists | proposes |
| declares | maintains | suggests |
| disagrees | notes | thinks |

**Rule**: When using technical terms or terms of art, explain those terms.
