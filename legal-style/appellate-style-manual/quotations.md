# Quotations: Oregon Appellate Courts Style Manual

> **Source**: Oregon Appellate Courts Style Manual (Updated 2023)
> **Applies to**: Court opinions (Oregon Court of Appeals, Oregon Supreme Court, Oregon Tax Court)
> **Contact for questions not covered**: <publications@ojd.state.or.us>

## Quick Reference

- **50+ words** → block quotation (required)
- **49 or fewer words** → block quotation optional (for emphasis)
- Block quotations: single spaced, double indented, enclosed within quotation marks
- Ellipsis: use **three asterisks** (\* \* \*), not periods, separated by spaces
- Block omission: use **five asterisks** (\* \* \* \* \*)
- All punctuation placed **inside** quotation marks, except colons, semicolons, and question
  marks that do not appear in the original
- Do NOT use an ellipsis to begin a quotation (no leading ellipsis needed)
- Do NOT use an ellipsis to denote the omission of a citation or footnote — use a parenthetical
  instead

## Section I: Citations, Parenthetical Phrases, and Footnotes

### I.A — Placement

**Rule**: When setting out a block quotation, the citation and parenthetical phrases (if any)
are placed flush left on the next line with a double space between the quotation and the
citation.

**Example — Citation after block quotation**:

> "Defendant, of course, may argue that the safeguard for employees exceeds the ordinary
> standard of due care considering the nature of the risk and the foreseeability of injury.
> The court may explain the governmentally prescribed safeguard to the jury in an instruction
> that the jury may consider the safeguard in determining whether the defendant exercised due
> care."
>
> Shahtout, 298 Or at 605-06.

**Example — Parenthetical phrase after block quotation**:

> ORS 19.205(2)(a) provides, in part:
>
> "An order affecting a substantial right, and which in effect determines the action \* \* \*."
>
> (Emphasis added.)

**Example — Order of citations and parentheticals after block quotation**:

> As stated in Welker,
>
> "[t]his court has held that a motion is controlled by its substance, not its caption.
> \* \* \*"
>
> 332 Or at 313 (citations omitted).

**Rule — Narrative context**: In a narrative context, the citation (aside from the case title)
may follow the quotation.

**Example**:

> The petitioner included discussion from Smith v. Jones, where the court stated, "We don't
> know what they were thinking." 123 Or App 456, 460, 999 P3d 789 (2015). While mildly
> amusing, that did not further his argument.

**Rule — Special expression**: Words used to show special expression or give special effect
(slang, ironic usage, philosophical terms, terms of art) can be used within quotation marks.
If the same phrase is used repeatedly, consider whether repeating the quotation marks could
become distracting.

#### Footnotes Within Quotations

**Rule**: The preferred method for quoting text that contains a footnote is to omit the
footnote and indicate so by parenthetical.

**Example**:

> "The difficulty with that argument is that the state has not established the requisite nexus
> between appellant's mental illness and the potential substance abuse or failure to appreciate
> the risks of such abuse."
>
> State v. Linde, 179 Or App 553, 561, 32 P3d 78 (2002) (footnote omitted).

**Rule**: To retain a footnote within a blocked quotation, drop the quoted, blocked footnote
under the quotation, separated by a one-inch line, then proceed with new text (do not set the
quoted footnote as a new footnote at the bottom of the page).

**Example structure**:

> "[b]ut the question is one of Oregon law, not federal law, the federal court's decision was
> the earlier of the two, and it is the Oregon court's decision—not that of the Ninth
> Circuit—that is binding for purposes of the certification law.8 It follows from the foregoing
> that this court should not accept certification of the first question, unless some other
> discretionary factor dictates a contrary conclusion.
> \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_
> "8 We recognize that the district court appears to be concerned about an inconsistency in
> decisions on this subject."

#### Block Quotations Within Block Quotations

**Rule**: When formatting a second level of blocked quoted text within blocked quoted text, use
the standard indent for the first level of quotation, and then for the internal quoted text,
use a double indent for both the right and left margins. Use single quotation marks for the
inner quotation.

**Example**:

> "the state must prove that the defendant's consent was independent of, or only tenuously
> related to, the illegal police conduct. As the court explained,
>
> " 'consent is insufficient to establish the admissibility of evidence from a warrantless
> search if the state cannot prove that the consent was independent of, or only tenuously
> related to, any preceding violation of the defendant's rights under Article I, section 9.
> Unless the state is able to make that showing, then the defendant's consent cannot operate to
> validate a warrantless search because the defendant's consent itself derived from a violation
> of the defendant's rights under that state constitutional provision.' "
>
> Jones, 265 Or App at 678 (quoting State v. Yunker, 330 Or 388, 395, 457 P3d 890 (2013)
> (emphasis in Yunker)).

### I.B — Use of Parenthetical Phrases With Quotations Within Text

**Rule — Omitting internal quotation marks or brackets**: If internal quotation marks or
brackets are omitted, so indicate.

**Examples**:

> "By looking to only the current employment of the land, the law ignores the past and any
> intentions with regard to future use." Everhart v. Dept. of Rev., 15 OTR 76, 81 (1999)
> (internal quotation marks omitted).
>
> In Everhart v. Dept. of Rev., 15 OTR 76, 81 (1999), the court rejected that argument,
> stating, "By looking to only the current employment of the land, the law ignores the past
> and any intentions with regard to future use." (Internal quotation marks omitted.)

**Rule**: It is correct to add either a parenthetical indicating that internal quotation marks
are omitted, or to leave in the quotation marks and add a parenthetical indicating the source
that you are quoting — but NOT both.

**Rule — Differentiating emphasis**: Include a parenthetical phrase to differentiate emphasis
being added to quoted material from emphasis that was already in the original text.

**Examples**:

> "While mass-appraisal techniques may place heavy reliance upon cost, cost alone is not
> determinative of market value." Su v. Dept. of Rev., 15 OTR 305, 308 (2001) (emphasis
> added).
>
> "ORS 653.295, however, provides no remedy for discharge of an employee \* \* \*."
> Dymock v. Norwest Safety Protective Equipment, 172 Or App 399, 406, 19 P3d 934 (2001)
> (emphasis in original).

**Rule — Emphasis within a nested quotation**: When the material being quoted contains a
quotation, the text of which is emphasized, include a parenthetical phrase noting the source
of the emphasis (use "emphases" if plural).

**Example**:

> "the Brown court 'only acknowledged that perhaps strict liability should require the danger
> to be one endangering human life or safety, a different question from whether tort recovery
> should be limited to such an injury,' and that '[t]hat difference determines the decision in
> the present case.' "
>
> 164 Or App 438, 451-52, 994 P2d 134 (1999) (quoting Russell v. Ford Motor Company,
> 281 Or 587, 593-94, 575 P2d 1383 (1978) (emphases in Russell)).

**Rule — Multiple notations**: Multiple notations about a quotation may follow any order that
clearly communicates whether and how the text was altered. If in doubt, choose this order: "in
original," "omitted," and "added." It is also acceptable to use "first emphasis added; second
emphasis in original," etc.

**Example**:

> Id. at 523 (first emphasis added; second emphasis in original; internal quotation marks
> omitted).

**Rule — Subsequent history with parenthetical**: When a citation includes both a parenthetical
phrase and subsequent history, place the parenthetical after the subsequent history.

**Example**:

> See State v. Bickford, 157 Or App 386, 390 n 1, 970 P2d 234 (1998), rev den, 329 Or 589
> (2000) ("If a trial court could infer subjective probable cause from the arrest, we would
> never need to inquire into subjective probable cause for the arrest.").

**Rule — Parenthetical with altered quotation**: When a parenthetical contains a quotation that
has been altered, include an internal parenthetical and adjust the punctuation accordingly.

**Examples**:

> SAIF v. Walker, 330 Or 102, 116, 996 P2d 979 (2000) (stating that ORS 656.273(1) requires
> "proof, based upon medical evidence supported by objective findings" (emphasis added)).
>
> SAIF v. Walker, 330 Or 102, 116-17, 996 P2d 979 (2000) ("Under ORS 656.005(19), however,
> such 'objective findings' may include evidence of worsened symptoms." (Emphasis in
> original.)).

**Rule — Quoting a source**: When including works that the primary authority quotes, discusses,
or mentions, the lead-in words (e.g., "Quoting," "Citing") should be indicated parenthetically
and not italicized.

**Example — Nested citation parenthetical**:

> Badger v. Paulson Investment Co., Inc., 311 Or 14, 24, 803 P2d 1178 (1991) ("Apparent
> authority is created 'only by some conduct of the principal which, when reasonably
> interpreted, causes a third party to believe that the principal consents to have the apparent
> agent act for him on that matter.' " (Quoting Mattson v. Commercial Credit Business Loans,
> 301 Or 407, 422, 723 P2d 996 (1986) (emphasis in Mattson).)).

## Section II: Use of Uppercase, Brackets, and Ellipsis Within Quotations

### II.A — Use of Uppercase

**Rule**: Generally, the first letter of a quotation that reads as a full sentence must be in
uppercase.

**Examples**:

> The trial court issued a letter opinion that addressed defendant's motion as follows: "There
> is no evidence supporting the first change."
> The witness testified, "Although I am not a doctor, I play one on TV."

**Rule**: When a full-sentence quotation requires changing the initial letter to uppercase, use
a bracket.

**Examples**:

> The court observed, "[U]npreserved error will ordinarily not be reviewed on appeal."
> The examining physician's report declared, "[C]laimant was medically stationary as of
> February 12, 1992."

**Rule**: Do NOT use a bracketed lowercase letter to indicate that the initial letter was
uppercase in the original when the quotation is presented as a standalone full sentence.

**Example**:

> Incorrect: Defendant argued, "[t]he trial court erred in excluding witnesses from the
> courtroom."
> Correct: Defendant argued, "The trial court erred in excluding witnesses from the
> courtroom."

**Exception**: When a quotation that reads as a full sentence is incorporated into the
grammatical structure of the sentence (often preceded by "that"), do NOT uppercase the first
letter. Use brackets if the first letter of the quotation was uppercase in the original.

**Example**:

> The court observed that "[u]npreserved error will ordinarily not be reviewed on appeal."

---

### II.B — Use of Brackets

**Rule**: The use of brackets in quoted material indicates that something has been changed,
modified, or deleted from how it was originally published.

#### II.B.1 — Indicate a Change in Uppercase or Lowercase

**Example**:

> You should "[p]lace commas and periods inside quotation marks."

#### II.B.2 — Alter a Word

**Examples**:

> "Plaintiff place[s] substantial weight on its belief that the legislature intended to restore
> the common practice of exempting brooder houses."
> The prohibition was enacted to "ensure[ ] that dogs will have their day."

#### II.B.3 — Indicate Substituted or Added Words

**Example**:

> The court's "jurisdiction [is limited] to determining whether petitioners' assessment may be
> spread over a period of at least 10 years."

#### II.B.4 — Add Punctuation Where There Previously Was None (and the Punctuation Is Not Part of the Larger Sentence)

**Example**:

> The standard of care applicable to physicians requires the use of "that degree of care,
> skill[,] and diligence" as is used by physicians in the same or similar community.

**Rule**: If the comma is part of the larger sentence (not required by the quoted text itself),
no bracket is needed.

**Example**:

> The trial court concluded that "there is no issue of material fact," but it also concluded
> that defendant was not entitled to judgment as a matter of law. [No comma in original;
> brackets not needed, because comma is part of larger sentence.]

#### II.B.5 — Modify Punctuation That Appears in the Original Text

**Rule**: Punctuation in quoted text can be replaced with other punctuation using brackets.

**Example**:

> Original: "The Court of Appeals has 13 members, but they sit in panels of three."
> New: "The Court of Appeals has 13 members[;] \* \* \* they sit in panels of three."

**Rule**: If quoted material ends before the new sentence ends, punctuation appropriate for the
new sentence can be included inside the quotation marks.

**Example**:

> Original: Defendant's argument is beyond reproach and compels us to strike his brief and
> order him to pay petitioner's costs.
> New: In Jones, the court characterized the defendant's argument as "beyond reproach," struck
> his brief, and ordered him to pay the petitioner's costs.

**Rule**: If omitting quoted material at the end of a quoted sentence but ending the new
sentence with the partially quoted material, denote the omitted quoted material before the
period in the new sentence with an ellipsis.

**Example**:

> Original: A party shall make a notation of exception either orally on the record or in a
> writing filed with the court.
> New: ORCP 59 H(2) provides that "[a] party shall make a notation of exception either orally
> on the record or in a writing \* \* \*."

#### II.B.6 — Insert a Citation Within a Quotation

**Rule**: When quoting text that refers to a case by use of *supra* rather than a citation,
add the citation in brackets.

**Example**:

> "This court has declared that evidence is suppressed for violations of the Oregon
> Constitution 'to preserve \* \* \* rights to the same extent as if the government's officers
> had stayed within the law.' State v. Davis[, 295 Or 227, 234, 666 P2d 802 (1983)]."

**Rule**: Use a short citation form if the case has already been cited in full.

**Example**:

> "A discovery rule cannot be assumed, but must be found in the statute or limitations itself.
> Huff[, 322 Or at 462]."

**Rule — Brackets with emphasis**: When modifying quoted material to add emphasized text,
emphasize the brackets themselves in addition to the inserted emphasized text, only when the
words on both sides of the brackets are already emphasized.

**Examples**:

> Correct: "Plaintiff's [*first*] argument \* \* \*" (only added word emphasized; words around
> it are not emphasized)
> Correct: "*Plaintiff's* [*first*] *argument* \* \* \*" (brackets emphasized because
> surrounding words are emphasized)
> Incorrect: "*Plaintiff's* [first] *argument* \* \* \*" (brackets should be emphasized to
> match surrounding text)

**Rule — VAWA cases in quotations**: When quoting text that refers to a VAWA case using an
unredacted case title, use brackets to redact the name of the protected party to initials.

**Example**:

> " 'Danger' in this context means 'a threat of physical injury, not merely a threat of
> annoyance or harassment.' [K. R.] v. Erazo, 248 Or App 700, 706-07, 274 P3d 214 (2012)."

#### II.B.7 — Indicate a Significant Mistake in the Original ([Sic])

**Rule**: "[Sic]" is used to indicate that the author being quoted has used a word incorrectly
or has used an incorrect spelling that confuses or changes the meaning of the sentence.

**Rule**: Obvious typographical errors may be silently corrected. Any idiosyncrasy of spelling
(particularly from older works) is preserved (e.g., "defence").

**Rule**: When using [sic], it is italicized, enclosed in brackets, and placed after the
uncorrected word.

**Examples**:

> "The court's rational [*sic*] for its decision was based entirely on legal precedent."
> (signals that "rational" should read "rationale")
> "We are going to precede [*sic*] with our plans for the law suit." (signals that "precede"
> should read "proceed")

**Rule**: Bracketing and correcting an error instead of using [*sic*] is usually sufficient as
long as it does not alter the meaning. The use of [*sic*] should be avoided to point out minor
grammatical errors, to indicate disagreement with a word's usage, or to appear highhanded.

**Examples**:

> Incorrect: "The company, on its own volition, intervened in the trial Court [*sic*]
> proceedings \* \* \*."
> Correct: "The company, on its own volition, intervened in the trial [c]ourt
> proceedings \* \* \*."
> Correct: "The company, on its own volition, intervened in the trial court
> proceedings \* \* \*."
>
> Incorrect: "The lab technician dropped the vile [*sic*] of blood."
> Correct: "The lab technician dropped the vi[al] of blood."
> Correct: "The lab technician dropped the vial of blood."

**Rule**: Where a word or sentence would be correct either way, do not use [*sic*].

### II.C — Use of Ellipsis

#### II.C.1 — Partial Omission

**Rule**: Use **three asterisks** (\* \* \*), not periods, separated by spaces, to indicate:

- Omission of a word or words within a sentence
- Omission of sentences within a block quotation
- Omission of text after a subsection number

**Examples**:

> The claimant seeks compensation "for mental and emotional distress and \* \* \* damages."
>
> In amending ORS 656.273(1) in 1995, the legislature "neither repealed nor amended
> ORS 656.273(8) \* \* \* but it may have changed how ORS 656.273(8) is applied."

**Rule**: When quoting a full sentence except the end, use three asterisks followed by a period
to indicate omission of the end of the sentence.

**Example**:

> "The evidence directly establishes only the truth of the primary fact or facts from which an
> inference may be derived \* \* \*."

**Exception**: If quoting a full sentence except the end and the quoted material originally
ended with a punctuation mark (other than a period), use only a bracketed period to indicate
the change of punctuation (and, by inference, the end of the sentence).

**Example**:

> "A court may consider unpreserved error under the plain error doctrine only if the error is
> one of law[.]" (period replaces semicolon and the remainder of the sentence)

**Rule**: When including quoted material within a sentence and the new sentence ends before the
quoted material ended, an ellipsis is NOT needed.

**Examples**:

> Correct: If reasonable minds can disagree, then "any error is not plain."
> Incorrect: If reasonable minds can disagree, then "any error is not plain \* \* \*."

**Rule**: Use three asterisks (not followed by a period) to indicate omission of one or more
sentences at the end of a paragraph within (as opposed to at the end of) a block quotation.

**Example** (first \* \* \* ends a sentence prematurely; second \* \* \* omits an additional
sentence or sentences):

> "The majority's reading of the statute requires us to make two leaps of faith. The first
> leap is that, in 1965, the legislature used 'gear or equipment' to mean exactly what
> 'appliance' meant between 1921 and 1965. The second leap of faith is that the terms 'boats,
> fishing gear and vehicles' mean exactly what 'gear or equipment' means in ORS 506.655
> \* \* \*. \* \* \*
> "We decline to take those leaps for several reasons."

**Rule — Omission after subsection number**:

> "(1) As used in ORS 308A.050 to 308A.128, 'farm use' means the current employment of land
> for the primary purpose of obtaining a profit in money by:
>
> "(a) \* \* \* harvesting and selling crops."

**Rule — Beginning a block quotation not at the start of a sentence or paragraph**: An ellipsis
is NOT necessary to begin a quotation that starts somewhere other than the beginning of the
sentence or paragraph.

**Examples**:

> The issue in Merriweather was as follows:
> "[W]hether a circuit court order compelling witnesses to appear and testify before a grand
> jury was appealable under ORS 19.205(4) as the product of a 'special statutory proceeding.' "
>
> The court has previously explained that
> "neither ORS 279.340 nor any other provision setting out requirements for hours of labor by
> public employees, ORS 279.334 - 279.342, expressly states what is meant by the phrase
> 'directly employed' or indicates the categories of persons to whom the phrase applies."

**Rule**: Place the beginning of a block quotation flush left when the original quoted material
is not the first sentence of a paragraph.

**Example**:

> As this court has stated before,
> "[e]ven if that assumption is accurate, it is insufficient because even in a high crime
> neighborhood unprovoked flight does not invariably lead to reasonable suspicion \* \* \*.
> Like unprovoked flight itself, presence in a high crime neighborhood is a fact too generic
> and susceptible to innocent explanation to satisfy the reasonable suspicion inquiry."

**Rule**: Do NOT use an ellipsis if quoting only part of a sentence.

**Example**:

> Again, the legislature expressly provided that those actions be "commenced not later than
> two years after the date on which the plaintiff first discovered, or in the exercise of
> reasonable care should have discovered," the death, injury, or disease.

**Rule**: Do NOT use an ellipsis to denote the omission of a citation or footnote. Instead,
use "citation omitted" or "footnote omitted" in a parenthetical.

**Rule**: When quoting only part of a statute, rule, etc., use "in part."

**Example**:

> ORS 305.440 provides, in part, "If no appeal is taken to the Supreme Court, the decision of
> the court shall constitute a final determination of the matter."

#### II.C.2 — Block Omission

**Rule**: Use **five asterisks** (\* \* \* \* \*) to indicate omission of a block of text such
as paragraphs or sections, but not to indicate an omission of additional material after an
ending paragraph.

**Example**:

> OAR 635-005-0180 provides, in part:
>
> "It is unlawful for commercial purposes to take, land, or possess sea urchins:
>
> "\* \* \* \* \*
>
> "(3) Without first obtaining a permit \* \* \*."

**Rule**: If the original has additional text, it is not necessary to add three asterisks
following the period.
