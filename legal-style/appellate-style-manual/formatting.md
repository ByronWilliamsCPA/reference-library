# Formatting: Oregon Appellate Courts Style Manual

> **Source**: Oregon Appellate Courts Style Manual (Updated 2023)
> **Applies to**: Court opinions (Oregon Court of Appeals, Oregon Supreme Court, Oregon Tax Court)
> **Contact for questions not covered**: <publications@ojd.state.or.us>

## Quick Reference

- Font: Times New Roman, 13-point
- Slip opinions: double spaced
- Indented quoted material: single spaced
- Line numbers: along left-hand margin (except footnote section)
- Footnotes: bottom of the page on which they are referenced
- Title page: followed by body of opinion; AWOP cases have title page only
- Contractions: used only when quoting from a source in which they appear

---

## Section I: Title Page

### I.A — Date of Opinion

**Rule**: The date the opinion issues is located in the upper right-hand corner of the page
preceded by FILED:.

### I.B — Identifying Caption of the Appellate Court

**Rule**: The identification of the appellate court is centered on the page in uppercase letters.

**Examples**:

> IN THE SUPREME COURT OF THE STATE OF OREGON
> IN THE COURT OF APPEALS OF THE STATE OF OREGON
> IN THE OREGON TAX COURT REGULAR DIVISION
> IN THE OREGON TAX COURT MAGISTRATE DIVISION

**Rule — Nonprecedential memorandum opinions** (Court of Appeals): The following recitation
precedes the caption:

> This is a nonprecedential memorandum opinion pursuant to ORAP 10.30 and may not be cited
> except as provided in ORAP 10.30(1).

### I.C — Names and Roles of the Parties

**Rule**: Parties are generally listed in the order in which they appeared in the lower court or
tribunal, but using their appellate court designations (appellant, respondent, petitioner on
review, respondent on review, etc.). That information is generally taken from the originating
document filed for a case (e.g., the Notice of Appeal or Petition for Judicial Review).

**Rule**: In criminal cases, the STATE OF OREGON is the first party listed, followed by the full
name of the defendant.

### I.D — Identification Numbers

**Rule**: Each appellate case is assigned a number when filed, centered on the title page below
the names of the parties, preceded by any identifying number(s) from the court or agency in
which the case originated. If cases have been consolidated on appeal or review, both appellate
case numbers are listed.

**Case number prefixes:**

- Supreme Court cases: begin with "S"
- Court of Appeals cases: begin with "A"
- Tax Court–Regular Division cases: begin with "TC"
- Tax Court–Magistrate Division cases: begin with "TC-MD"

### I.E — En Banc

**Rule**: If a case is decided by the full court, that is noted in the first line starting at the
left-hand margin. No period follows the en banc designation.

**Rule**: The initial letter in each word is in uppercase on the title page (e.g., En Banc), but
when used within the text of an opinion, the term is in lowercase letters.

**Example**:

> Title page: En Banc
> Within text: "The Court of Appeals, sitting en banc, reversed."

### I.F — Court / Agency of Origination

**Rule**: Identifies where/how the case originated.

### I.G — Trial Court Judge

**Rule**: Identifies judge(s) who signed the appealable judgment(s) or order(s) that are the
subject of the appeal. The Supreme Court footnotes that information on the title page with an
asterisk.

### I.H — Argued and Submitted Date

**Rule**: Identifies when the case was submitted and whether it was argued. Some cases are
submitted on the record only.

### I.I — Names of Counsel

**Rule**: The attorney(s) for all parties to a case are named. If a party appears pro se (for
himself or herself), that is noted. A person or entity appearing as amicus curiae is also
identified here, along with the counsel of record. Counsel names are listed as they appear on
the signature line of the briefs filed in the case.

**Rule — Department of Justice (DOJ) attorneys**: Use official titles (Attorney General, Deputy
Attorney General, Solicitor General, Deputy Solicitor General, Assistant Attorney General), but
do not use any internal DOJ classification or position description, such as
"Attorney-in-Charge, Post-Conviction Section" or "Senior Assistant Attorney General."

**Rule — Office of Public Defense Services (OPDS) attorneys**: Use official titles (Chief
Defender, Criminal Appellate Section; Chief Defender, Juvenile Appellate Section; Deputy Public
Defender), but do not use any internal OPDS classification or position description, such as
"Chief Deputy," "Senior Deputy Public Defender," etc.

### I.J — Panel of Judges / Justices

**Rule**: The panel of judges (in the Court of Appeals, also denoting the Presiding Judge) or
the names of the Supreme Court justices deciding the case (if not heard en banc) are listed.
When denoting a judge who has retired or resigned, a judge's designation will reflect the status
of the judge at the time the opinion issues.

### I.K — Opinion Author(s)

#### Signed Opinions

**Rule**: The author's name is listed in uppercase. When there is a concurring or dissenting
opinion, the name of its author is listed on the title page after the decision line (e.g.,
Smith, J., dissenting.).

**Order of opinions**: majority; concurring; specially concurring; concurring in part,
dissenting in part; dissenting. If two or more justices/judges file a concurring or dissenting
opinion, the more senior justice's/judge's opinion goes first.

**Example reference**: State v. Dameron, 316 Or 448, 853 P2d 1285 (1993) (for order of
opinions).

**Rule — Nonparticipating justice**: When there is a nonparticipating justice in the Supreme
Court, that justice's name is footnoted on the title page.

#### Per Curiam Opinions

**Rule**: An opinion that summarily disposes of the case may be designated as Per Curiam. The
Per Curiam designation is also used for all lawyer discipline, Bar admission, and judicial
fitness matters before the Supreme Court.

**Rule**: Per Curiam opinions have the same weight of authority as a signed opinion; it is
therefore not necessary to indicate parenthetically whether a cited opinion is Per Curiam.

#### Affirmed by an Equally Divided Court

**Rule**: In rare circumstances, when the court is split evenly regarding the disposition of a
case, the case is deemed to be affirmed, although no signed majority opinion is issued.

### I.L — Disposition of Case

**Rule**: A brief statement of the court's holding on the title page. This holding is repeated
as the last line (set out as a separate paragraph) in the body of the opinion, called the "tag
line." The tag line serves as the court's formal disposition of the case and as further
instruction to the lower tribunal(s).

### I.M — Designation of Prevailing Party and Award of Costs

**Rule**: The last part of a Supreme Court or Court of Appeals title page denotes the prevailing
party and whether, and to whom, the court allows costs.

## Section II: Body of Opinion

### II — In General

**Rule**: Contractions are used only when quoting from a source in which they appear. Appellate
opinions are formal documents.

**Rule**: Due to the scrutiny to which a published appellate opinion is subjected, the court's
discussion, analysis, and holding need to be expressed clearly, succinctly, and carefully.

**Rule**: The opinion begins by restating the name of the authoring judge or justice, or by
using the Per Curiam designation in uppercase, set out separately as the first line.

**Rule — References to parties**: To the extent possible, the opinion refers to parties by
their lower tribunal designations (plaintiff, defendant, claimant, etc.). Exceptions:

1. Domestic relations cases: parties are referred to as "husband" and "wife"
2. Civil commitment proceedings: the person for whom commitment is sought is referred to by his
   or her position on appeal (appellant, respondent)
3. Termination of parental rights proceedings: parents are "mother" and "father"; children are
   "child" or "children" or sometimes by initials
4. Victims: proper names of victims are not used

**Rule — Long party names**: If a party has a long name, a shortened version is usually
developed for subsequent references, e.g., First Security Bank of the Northwest may be referred
to simply as "First Security" or "bank."

**Rule — References to other opinions**: When citing a concurring or dissenting opinion, the
author's last name is used. When a dissenting or concurring opinion refers to the majority
opinion, the majority opinion is referred to as such, not by its author.

### II.A — General Format

#### 1. Initial Paragraph — Introduction to the Case

**Rule**: The introductory paragraph sets out the general nature of the case, which includes the
results in any lower tribunal(s), the main issues on appeal/review, and the final disposition
of the court. That provides the framework for the details that follow.

#### 2. Statement of Facts

**Rule**: The pertinent facts of a case are set out in a concise and objective manner. Facts can
be organized chronologically, geographically, or by issue, witness, or actor. If an issue is
complex, the facts may be set out in general in the statement of facts and then in more detail
when discussing the specific issue.

#### 3. Discussion of Issues

**Rule**: The opinion addresses the dispositive issue(s) in a manner appropriate to the
circumstances of the case. One approach is to state the parties' positions, either in the order
in which they were raised below, discussed in the briefs, or dictated by circumstance; respond
to those arguments; and then provide an explanation for the result reached by stating the
authorities relied on.

#### 4. Disposition of Case

**Rule**: The final paragraph states the result and gives instructions when necessary. The
conclusion is followed by the tag line, a separate paragraph that sets out the court's final
ruling and serves as further instruction to the lower tribunal(s).

### II.B — Structural Tools

#### II.B.1 — Paragraph or Section Headings

##### Format: Headings

**Rule**: When used, headings for principal divisions within an opinion are centered and set out
in uppercase letters. Roman numerals are not used if subheads are omitted.

##### Format: Outline Method

**Rule**: Standard outline rules apply: if there is a heading designated I., there must be a
II.; if there is a subheading A., there must be a B.; etc.

**Levels of outlining**:

- Headings: indicated by Roman numerals (centered, uppercase)
- Subheadings: indicated by uppercase letters (flush left, italic text)
- Paragraphs: indicated by numerals (indented, italic)
- Subparagraphs: indicated by lowercase letters (indented twice, no italics)
- Subsubparagraphs: indicated by numbers within parentheses (indented further, no italics)

**Rules for subheading format**:

- Initial caps on all words of first-level subheadings except articles, prepositions, and
  conjunctions, unless the subheading reads as a complete sentence
- If subheading is a sentence, use a period and do not use initial uppercase after the first word
- Each new level starts at a new level of indentation, with an indent following the number or
  letter
- Paragraph heading is indented and italicized; do not use initial uppercase after the first
  word; use a period only if a sentence
- Subparagraph heading is indented twice, no italics

**Example structure**:

```text
I.  HEADING (centered, uppercase)
  A.  Subheading Example with Initial Uppercase
      [flush left, italicized subheading text]
      1.  Paragraph heading is indented and italicized.
          a.  Subparagraph heading is indented twice, no italics.
              (1)  Subsubparagraph heading is indented yet again, no italics.
```

**Rule — Case names in subheadings**: If the opinion subheadings only involve a single case
name, italicize the case name. If subheadings have both single-case and multi-case scenarios
(or only multi-case), format without italicizing, for internal consistency:

> A.  State v. Baker
> B.  State v. Jones and its Progeny

##### Bulleted or Numbered Lists

**Rule**: Bulleted lists are indented and generally used for items that need no particular order.

**Example**:

> - Car of little or no value
> - Boat valued at $10,000
> - Personal jewelry valued at more than $5,000 but less than $10,000

**Rule**: Numbered lists, with each numeral appearing inside a set of parentheses, help to
organize and display information to show relationship.

**Example**:

> Defendant argues as follows: (1) the trial court erred; (2) the error was not harmless;
> and (3) his conviction should be reversed.

#### II.B.2 — Quotations

**Rule**: When construing a statute or administrative rule, for example, the author generally
quotes the pertinent text. The purpose of quotation is to provide the reader with the
information necessary to understand the court's discussion of the issues and the law governing
its analysis. See the Quotation section for proper format.

#### II.B.3 — Footnotes

##### In General

**Rule**: Footnotes document sources of information or make ancillary references. Substantive
information is best addressed within the body of the opinion.

**Rule**: Footnote text begins on the same line as the superscripted footnote number, except
when the footnote begins with a block quotation.

##### Citations in Footnotes

**Rule**: When citing a case in a footnote for the first time (not previously cited in text or
footnote), use the full case citation. If the case has already been cited (in text or footnote),
use a short citation.

**Rule**: If a case is cited for the first time in a footnote, the first subsequent citation to
that case in the text must also be a full citation.

##### Referencing and Setting Out Footnoted Text

**Rule**: Footnote numbers, where applicable, are placed after periods, commas, colons,
semicolons, and quotation marks. Footnote numbers are placed after a closing parenthesis,
unless the footnote refers to material inside the parentheses.

**Rule**: Footnote numbers inserted within quoted material are set out using superscripted
brackets.

#### II.B.4 — Maps / Pictures / Appendices

**Rule**: When graphic information is necessary, a photograph, map, or chart is either appended
to the opinion or inserted within the text where applicable. When included, an appendix is
usually first described in narrative form. The appendix typically begins on a separate page at
the end of the opinion with the heading "Appendix."

### II.C — Writing Tools

#### II.C.1 — Fonts

##### Italics and Underscoring

**Rule**: Italic typeface is used within opinions to denote:

- Case names
- Introductory signals
- Less common foreign terminology
- Emphasis (used sparingly)

**Rule**: Excessive use of italics for emphasis is discouraged.

**Rule**: Underscoring is used only in quotations when the original source used underscoring for
emphasis or headings and the like.

##### Boldface and Uppercase

**Rule**: It is best to avoid using a variety of styles and fonts within an opinion. Use of
boldface or all uppercase letters in text is discouraged as a distraction to the reader. Italic
type generally is sufficient to show emphasis.

**Rule**: Avoid using UPPERCASE BOLDFACE ITALIC, as it is difficult to read.

#### II.C.2 — Make Smooth Transitions

**Rule**: When turning to a new issue or argument within an opinion, use introductory sentences
or paragraphs to indicate transition between discussions. Use signal words to connect thoughts
back to a preceding point or ahead to the next one (e.g., further, however, consequently).
Explore one idea per paragraph, relating each sentence to that central idea.
