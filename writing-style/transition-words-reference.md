# Transition Words Reference

> **Purpose**: Use transitions deliberately, not reflexively
> **Use**: Loaded by the `grammar-composition-editor` for composition review and by the
> `writing-style-editor` for AI pattern detection
> **Scope**: Professional and analytical writing

---

## Transition Categories

### Addition

Signal that the next point builds on the previous one.

- also, in addition, further, moreover, furthermore, equally important, what is more

**Use when**: the new point genuinely extends the previous one.

**Avoid when**: the connection is obvious without a transition. Two consecutive sentences about
the same topic rarely need "Additionally" between them.

### Contrast

Signal a shift, qualification, or opposing point.

- however, nevertheless, on the other hand, conversely, although, yet, still, by contrast,
  even so, nonetheless, that said

**Use when**: the reader would not expect the shift. Transitions like "however" and "but" earn
their place when they mark a genuine pivot.

### Cause and Effect

Signal that one point explains or results from another.

- because, therefore, consequently, as a result, thus, accordingly, for this reason, hence,
  so, it follows that

**Use when**: the causal connection needs to be made explicit. In analytical writing, causal
transitions should be precise — "therefore" implies logical necessity; "as a result" implies
empirical consequence.

### Sequence

Signal order or progression.

- first, second, third, next, then, finally, subsequently, meanwhile, previously, afterward

**Use when**: ordering matters and is not already clear from context. Numbered lists handle
sequence better than prose transitions for more than three items.

### Emphasis

Signal that the next point carries particular weight.

- indeed, in fact, notably, particularly, above all, most importantly, specifically

**Use when**: you need to draw attention to a single point that might otherwise be read as
equal to its neighbors.

### Example

Signal an illustration or instance.

- for example, for instance, specifically, to illustrate, as an example, such as, namely

**Use when**: the general statement needs concrete grounding. Prefer "for example" in most
cases; "to illustrate" works when the example is longer or more complex.

### Conclusion

Signal a closing summary or final point.

- in summary, to summarize, in conclusion, accordingly, therefore, in short, ultimately

**Use when**: the document is long enough that the reader benefits from a restatement.
Short documents (under 2 pages) rarely need concluding transitions.

## Usage Rules

### 1. Not Every Sentence Needs a Transition

Most sentences connect to their neighbors through content, not connective words. A transition
should appear only when the logical relationship would otherwise be unclear. If removing the
transition doesn't change the meaning, remove it.

### 2. Vary Your Transitions

Using the same transition word three times on a page is a tell. If you find yourself reaching
for "however" again, restructure the sentence or use a different contrast word.

### 3. Transitions Are Not Paragraph Starters by Default

Starting a paragraph with a transition is valid when the paragraph shifts direction from the
previous one. Starting every paragraph with a transition makes the writing feel formulaic.

### 4. Match Precision to the Relationship

- "Therefore" implies logical deduction — use it only when the conclusion follows necessarily
- "As a result" implies empirical consequence — use for observed outcomes
- "However" signals a genuine contrast — don't use it for minor qualifications
- "Furthermore" adds to the same argument — don't use it to change topics

## AI Pattern: Transition Overuse

AI-generated text overuses transitions, especially these three as paragraph openers:

- **"Additionally,"** — appears in AI text at roughly 3x the human rate
- **"Furthermore,"** — often used interchangeably with "Additionally" in the same document
- **"Moreover,"** — rarely appears in natural business writing; strong AI signal

**Detection heuristic**: If a document uses "Additionally," "Furthermore," or "Moreover" more
than once per page, flag for revision.

**Fix**: Delete the transition and read the paragraph without it. If meaning is preserved, leave
it deleted. If a connection is genuinely needed, choose a transition that fits the specific
relationship (addition, contrast, cause, etc.) rather than defaulting to a generic additive.
