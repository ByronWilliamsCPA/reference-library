# Words and Expressions Commonly Misused

> about correct usage are not copyrightable. Where EoS and CMS differ, the CMS ruling
> governs (Tier 2 authority). Entries are expanded beyond the 1918 original to include
> pairs that frequently appear in professional writing.

## Quick Reference Table

| --- | --- | --- |
| *affect* / *effect* | *Affect* = verb (to influence). *Effect* = noun (a result). Exception: *effect* as verb = to bring about. | §5.220 |
| *among* / *between* | *Between* for two items. *Among* for three or more. | §5.220 |
| *anxious* / *eager* | *Anxious* = worried, apprehensive. *Eager* = enthusiastic. | Paraphrase |
| *as* / *like* | *Like* governs nouns and pronouns, not clauses. Use *as* or *as if* when a verb follows. | §5.254 (analogous) |
| *assure* / *ensure* / *insure* | *Assure* = to convince a person. *Ensure* = to make certain. *Insure* = for insurance. | Widely recognized |
| *can* / *may* | *Can* = ability. *May* = permission or possibility. | §5.152 (analogous) |
| *comprise* / *compose* | The whole *comprises* its parts. The parts *compose* the whole. "Comprised of" is nonstandard. | §5.220 |

| *farther* / *further* | *Farther* = physical distance. *Further* = degree or extent. | §5.220 |
| *fewer* / *less* | *Fewer* for countable items. *Less* for quantities or continuous amounts. | §5.220 |

| *imply* / *infer* | A speaker *implies*. A listener *infers*. | §5.220 |

## Expanded Notes on High-Frequency Pairs

### *affect* / *effect*

*Affect* (verb): to have an influence on something.
"The amendment **affects** the definition in §174.010."

*Effect* (noun): a result or consequence.
"The **effect** of the amendment is to expand the definition."

*Effect* (verb, rare): to bring about or accomplish.
"The settlement **effected** a complete resolution."

Do not write "impact" as a verb when *affect* is available and more precise.

---

### *comprise* / *compose*

The whole comprises its parts. "The chapter **comprises** five sections." (Not "is comprised of.")
The parts compose the whole. "Five sections **compose** the chapter."

"Comprised of" is nonstandard. Avoid it.

---

### *fewer* / *less*

Use *fewer* for things that can be counted individually: "**fewer** than ten briefs."
Use *less* for quantities, amounts, or continuous measures: "**less** time," "**less** money."

Exception: *less* is standard with periods of time and distances used as single amounts: "**less** than ten
years," "**less** than five miles."

---

### *lay* / *lie*

| Tense | *lay* (to place) | *lie* (to recline) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Present | lay (the record) | lie (down) |
| Past | laid | lay |
| Past participle | laid | lain |
| Present participle | laying | lying |

"He **laid** the brief on the table." (Past tense of *lay*.)
"The document had **lain** on the docket for six months." (Past participle of *lie*.)

---

### *who* / *whom*

Substitute *he* for *who* and *him* for *whom* to check which is correct.
"Give the brief to whoever/whomever asks first." — "give the brief to **him** who asks" → **whoever**.
"The attorney **whom** you called." — "you called **him**" → **whom**.

---

### Entries to Avoid: AI-Common Misusage

These pairs also appear frequently in AI-generated prose with the wrong form:

- "impact" as verb → prefer *affect*
- "utilize" → prefer *use* unless technical context requires it
- "prior to" → prefer *before*
- "subsequent to" → prefer *after*
- "in order to" → prefer *to*
- "due to the fact that" → prefer *because*
- "at this point in time" → prefer *now*
- "in the event that" → prefer *if*

## Abbreviations and Latin Terms

> **Authority**: CMS §10.1–10.4 and §10.42–10.44. EoS does not address abbreviations.
> CMS governs throughout this section.

### Common Latin Abbreviations

| Abbreviation | Meaning | Rule |
| --- | --- | --- |
| *e.g.* | *exempli gratia* — "for example" | Followed by a comma in U.S. usage: "states, *e.g.*, Oregon and Washington." Use inside parentheses or before examples. |
| *i.e.* | *id est* — "that is" | Followed by a comma in U.S. usage: "the statute (*i.e.*, ORS 174.010)." Use only to restate or clarify. |
| *et al.* | *et alii* — "and others" | Period after *al.* only (not after *et*). Used for works with multiple authors in citations: "Smith et al." Not italicized in text. |
| *ibid.* | *ibidem* — "in the same place" | CMS now discourages *ibid.* in favor of shortened citations (§14.34). Acceptable in footnotes when referring to the immediately preceding source. |
| *cf.* | *confer* — "compare" | Signals that a cited source supports a related but different point. Distinguish from "see," which signals direct support. |
| *viz.* | *videlicet* — "namely" | Introduces a specific list. Prefer "namely" in most contexts; *viz.* is archaic outside legal and academic writing. |
| *supra* | "above" | Legal usage: refers back to a source already cited. "Smith, *supra* note 4, at 12." |
| *infra* | "below" | Legal usage: refers forward to a source to be cited. Avoid when possible; reorganize so *supra* can be used instead. |

### *e.g.* vs. *i.e.*

**Use *e.g.*** when you are giving one or more examples from a larger set:
"Oregon uses several citation forms, *e.g.*, ORS, OAR, and ORCP."
(There are more citation forms; these are just examples.)

**Use *i.e.*** when you are restating or defining the full scope:
"The governing statute (*i.e.*, ORS 174.010) defines the scope."
(ORS 174.010 *is* the governing statute; this is not a partial list.)

**Memory test**: *e.g.* = "for example" (think "example given"); *i.e.* = "that is" (think "in essence").

### Punctuation and Formatting Rules

- Both *e.g.* and *i.e.* are set off by commas in U.S. style: "states, *e.g.*, Oregon."
- Italicize Latin abbreviations on first use in formal documents; do not italicize in legal
  citation strings where they appear as part of the citation form.
- In running text, consider spelling out: "for example" instead of *e.g.*, "that is" instead
  of *i.e.* Plain language guidance favors the English equivalent.
- Never write *eg.* or *ie.* (missing periods). Both abbreviations require periods on each letter.
