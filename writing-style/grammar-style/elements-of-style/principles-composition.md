# Elementary Principles of Composition

> **Source**: William Strunk Jr., *The Elements of Style* (1918). Public domain.
> Original text reproduced verbatim or in close summary. Notes on White's revisions
> are paraphrased — not quoted — from the copyrighted 1959+ editions.

---

## Principle 1. Choose a suitable design and hold to it

A basic structural design underlies every kind of writing. Writers will in part follow this
design, in part deviate from it, according to their skills, needs, and purposes.

The first principle of composition, therefore, is to foresee or determine the shape of what
is to come and pursue that shape.

---

## Principle 2. Make the paragraph the unit of composition

The paragraph is a convenient unit; it serves all forms of literary work. As long as it holds
together, a paragraph may be of any length — a single, short sentence or a passage of great
duration.

Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence; end it in conformity with the beginning.

**CMS cross-reference**: CMS has no fixed rule on paragraph structure. Structure should serve
the content. Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis are legitimate. See `../cross-reference.md` #6.

---

## Principle 3. Use the active voice

The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive:

> I will always remember my first visit to Boston.

is better than:

> My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me.

The habitual use of the active voice makes for forcible writing. When a sentence is made
stronger, it usually becomes shorter. Thus brevity is a by-product of vigor.

**CMS cross-reference**: CMS §5.117 agrees on the preference for active but acknowledges
that passive has valid uses when the subject is unknown, unimportant, or should be
de-emphasized. "The motion was denied" is better than "The judge denied the motion" when
the judge's identity is irrelevant. See `../cross-reference.md` #2.

---

## Principle 4. Put statements in positive form

Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, noncommittal language.

> not honest
> not important
> did not remember
> did not pay any attention to

should be replaced by:

> dishonest
> trifling
> forgot
> ignored

The word *not* is a weak word; it makes a weak impression in writing. Consciously prefer the
positive form.

---

## Principle 5. Use definite, specific, concrete language

Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract.

If you say "a period of unfavorable weather set in," you have said nothing. Say what you mean:
"It rained every day for a week."

Vigorous writing is precise. Use words that mean exactly what you want to say. Do not write
*approximately* when you mean *about*, *frequently* when you mean *often*, *considerable* when
you mean *large*. The reader cannot tell what you mean if you don't say it precisely.

---

## Principle 6. Omit needless words

Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no
unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and
a machine no unnecessary parts. This requires not that the writer make all his sentences short,
or that he avoid all detail and treat his subjects only in outline, but that every word tell.

**Common deadwood to cut**:

| Wordy | Concise |
| --- | --- |
| the question as to whether | whether |
| there is no doubt but that | no doubt |
| used for fuel purposes | used for fuel |
| he is a man who | he |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject that | this subject |
| owing to the fact that | since |
| in spite of the fact that | though |
| call your attention to the fact that | remind you |
| the fact that he had not succeeded | his failure |
| the fact that I had arrived | my arrival |

---

## Principle 7. Avoid a succession of loose sentences

A series of sentences, each joined to the previous one by a loose connective (*and, but, so,
for, because*), produces a weak, monotonous effect:

> The Office of the Governor is located in the State Capitol, and it is open to the public, but access is
> restricted on weekdays, so you should call ahead to confirm availability.

Break this up. Vary sentence structure. Use subordination to show which ideas are more important.

---

## Principle 8. Express coordinate ideas in similar form

This principle is called parallel construction. An unskilled writer will say:

> The French, the Italians, Spanish, and Portuguese

where he should say:

> the French, the Italians, the Spanish, and the Portuguese

Or, using conjunctions:

> It is either right or wrong. (Not: It is either right or it is wrong.)

In parallel constructions, the coordinated elements must be grammatically alike: nouns with
nouns, verbs with verbs, phrases with phrases, clauses with clauses.

**CMS cross-reference**: §5.241–5.244. CMS agrees and provides additional guidance on
parallel constructions in correlative conjunctions (*both...and*, *not only...but also*).

---

## Principle 9. Keep related words together

The position of the words in a sentence is the principal means of showing their relationship.
The subject and the principal verb should not, as a rule, be separated by a phrase or clause
that can be transferred to the beginning.

**Avoid**: "Macbeth, realizing that he has defeated his enemy, sends messengers to the army
throughout Scotland."

**Prefer**: "Realizing that he has defeated his enemy, Macbeth sends messengers to the army
throughout Scotland."

Modifiers should be placed as close as possible to the word they modify.

---

## Principle 10. In summaries, keep to one tense

In summarizing the action of a drama, the writer should use the present tense. In summarizing
a poem, story, or novel, use the present: "He shows," "He advises."

**CMS cross-reference**: §5.130. CMS agrees: the "literary present" tense is standard for
discussing the content of any written work, whether a novel, statute, or legal opinion.
See `../cross-reference.md` #16.

---

## Principle 11. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end

The proper place for the word, or group of words, which the writer desires to make most
prominent is usually the end of the sentence:

> Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways.

Not:

> Since that time humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude, though it has advanced in many other ways.

The other prominent position in a sentence is the beginning. Any element that is not the
subject demands attention when placed first.

## CMS and EoS Alignment

Most of these 11 principles are consistent with CMS guidance or address composition matters
that CMS does not directly govern. The documented conflicts are:

- **Active vs. passive** (#3): CMS permits passive when appropriate. See `../cross-reference.md` #2.
