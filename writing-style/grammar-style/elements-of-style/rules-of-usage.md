# Elementary Rules of Usage

> **Source**: William Strunk Jr., *The Elements of Style* (1918). Public domain.
> Original text reproduced verbatim below. Where E.B. White's revisions (1959+) update
> a rule, the change is noted as "White's note" and paraphrased — not quoted.

---

## Rule 1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's

Follow this rule whatever the final consonant. Thus write,

> Charles's friend
> Burns's poems
> the witch's malice

**Exceptions**: the possessives of ancient proper names ending in *-es* and *-is*, the
possessive *Jesus'*, and such forms as *for conscience' sake*, *for righteousness' sake*.
But such forms as *Moses' laws*, *Isis' temple* are commonly replaced by *the laws of Moses*,
*the temple of Isis*.

*White's note*: The possessive of *it* is *its* (no apostrophe); *it's* means "it is." This is
one of the most common writing errors.

**CMS cross-reference**: §7.17–7.20. CMS agrees: add 's to most singular nouns and proper
names. Classical names (Jesus', Moses', Achilles') are the main exceptions.

---

## Rule 2. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last

> red, white, and blue
> gold, silver, or copper
> He opened the letter, read it, and made a note of its contents.

**CMS cross-reference**: §6.19. CMS requires the serial comma in running text. This is one
place where CMS is more consistent than older EoS editions — always use the serial comma.

---

## Rule 3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas

The year *1988*, it will be remembered, was a leap year.

> The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot.

This rule is difficult to apply; it is frequently hard to decide whether a single word, such
as *however*, or a brief phrase, is or is not parenthetic. If the interruption to the flow of
the sentence is but slight, the writer may safely omit the commas.

**Note**: "Restrictive" clauses (those essential to meaning) take no commas. "Nonrestrictive"
clauses (those adding supplementary information) take commas. See also *that* vs. *which*:
`../QUICK-START.md` #3.

---

## Rule 4. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause

> The situation is perilous, but if we are prepared to act promptly, there is still one chance of success.

Two-part sentences in which the second member is a command, a query, or a conditional usually
do not need the comma:

> Do this and you will succeed.
> Are you his brother or are you not?
> Either you must grant his request or incur his ill will.

**CMS cross-reference**: §6.22. CMS agrees: use a comma before a coordinating conjunction
(*and*, *but*, *or*, *nor*, *for*, *so*, *yet*) that joins two independent clauses.

---

## Rule 5. Do not join independent clauses with a comma

If two or more clauses grammatically complete and not joined by a conjunction are to form a
single compound sentence, the proper mark of punctuation is a semicolon.

> It is nearly half past five; we cannot reach town before dark.

It is of course equally correct to write the above as two sentences each with a period.

**Note**: Joining two independent clauses with only a comma (no conjunction) is a "comma splice"
and is considered an error in formal writing.

**CMS cross-reference**: §6.56. CMS agrees on the comma-splice rule.

---

## Rule 6. Do not break sentences in two

In other words, do not use periods for commas.

> I met them on a Cunard liner many years ago. Coming home from Liverpool to New York.

A participial phrase or other phrase does not make a complete sentence. Exceptions are
exclamations and sentences with which the writer wishes to produce a particular effect.

> Again and again he called out. No reply.

**CMS cross-reference**: §5.238. CMS permits sentence fragments for rhetorical effect in
non-formal prose contexts. In formal, legal, or academic writing, follow Strunk's rule.

---

## Rule 7. Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list, a quotation, or an amplifying statement

> Your dedicated whittler requires three props: a knife, a piece of wood, and a back porch.

Join two independent clauses with a colon if the second interprets or amplifies the first:

> But even so, there was a directness and dispatch about animal burial: there was no stopover in the
> undertaker's foul parlor, no wreath or spray.

**CMS cross-reference**: §6.61–6.63. CMS agrees. Also: the word following a colon is
lowercased (not capitalized) unless it is a proper noun or begins a complete sentence
that requires capitalization (§6.63).

---

## Rule 8. A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject

> Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman accompanied by two children.

The word *walking* refers to the subject of the sentence, not to the woman. If the writer
wishes to make it refer to the woman, he must recast the sentence:

> He saw a woman, accompanied by two children, walking slowly down the road.

Participial phrases preceded by a conjunction or a preposition, nouns in apposition, adjectives,
and adjective phrases come under the same rule if they begin the sentence.

> On arriving in Chicago, his friends met him at the station.

The subject of the main clause is *his friends*; *On arriving in Chicago* refers to somebody else.
The sentence must be recast to read:

> On arriving in Chicago, he was met at the station by his friends.

**CMS cross-reference**: §5.116. CMS states: introductory participial and other phrases must
modify the grammatical subject of the main clause.

---

## Notes on CMS Divergences

For a complete table of points where EoS and CMS differ, see `../cross-reference.md`.
The most important divergences affecting these 8 rules:

- **Serial comma** (#2): CMS is more consistent — always require it.
- **Sentence fragments** (#6): CMS permits them for rhetorical effect in informal prose.
- **Passive voice**: Strunk's composition principles strongly prefer active; CMS is balanced.
  See `principles-composition.md` and `../cross-reference.md` #2.
