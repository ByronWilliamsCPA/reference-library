# Numbers (CMS Chapter 9)

> **Source**: Paraphrased from *The Chicago Manual of Style*, 17th edition (2017), Chapter 9.
> CMS section references are provided for verification. No verbatim CMS prose appears here.
>
> **Authority**: CMS Ch. 9 governs. EoS's rule (spell out below 10) is overridden by CMS's
> spell-out threshold of one through one hundred. See `../cross-reference.md` #7.

---

## Spell Out vs. Numeral: Basic Rule

### Running Text (§9.2–9.3)

**Condition**: A number appears in running prose.

**Rule**: Spell out whole numbers from one through one hundred. Use numerals for 101 and above.
This rule applies in running text regardless of context, subject to the exceptions below.

**Examples**:

- "The statute lists **twenty-three** enumerated exceptions."
- "The court issued **104** opinions last term."
- "She filed **three** motions and received **two** extensions."

---

### Consistency Within a Sentence or Paragraph (§9.7)

**Condition**: Numbers in the same sentence or closely related context include both low numbers
(normally spelled out) and high numbers (normally numerals).

**Rule**: When numbers in the same category appear together, use numerals for all of them to
maintain consistency.

**Example**:

- "The committee reviewed **8** submissions in March and **147** in April."
  (Not: "The committee reviewed *eight* submissions in March and **147** in April.")

---

## Exceptions: Always Use Numerals

### Percentages (§9.18)

**Condition**: The number expresses a percentage.

**Rule**: Always use a numeral with the word *percent* (not the symbol % in running text). Use
the % symbol only in technical, statistical, or tabular contexts.

**Examples**:

- "The statute imposes a **5 percent** penalty for late filing."
- "Costs increased **12 percent** year over year."

---

### Dates (§9.30–9.33)

**Condition**: The sentence contains a date.

**Rule**: Use numerals for calendar days and years. Spell out or use ordinals for days used
without a year in formal contexts. Do not use *st*, *nd*, *rd*, *th* suffixes when the day
follows the month.

**Examples**:

- "The agreement was signed on **June 15, 2023**." (day follows month: numeral, no suffix)
- "The deadline falls on the **fifteenth of June**." (day without month: spelled out)
- "The session began in **2023**."

---

### Times (§9.37–9.39)

**Condition**: The sentence contains a time of day.

**Rule**: Use numerals with *a.m.* and *p.m.* For clock times with *o'clock*, use spelled-out
numbers in formal prose.

**Examples**:

- "The hearing begins at **9:00 a.m.**"
- "The filing deadline is **5:00 p.m.**"
- "Court reconvenes at **two o'clock**." (formal, with *o'clock*)

---

### Ages, Measurements, and Units (§9.14–9.17)

**Condition**: The number is used with a unit of measurement, or expresses an age.

**Rule**: Always use numerals for physical quantities, dimensions, measurements, ages, and
scientific units — even for numbers below one hundred.

**Examples**:

- "The plaintiff is **34** years old."
- "The document is **8.5 by 11** inches."
- "The parcel measures **12** acres."

---

### Money (§9.20–9.25)

**Condition**: The sentence states an amount of money.

**Rule**: Use numerals with the currency symbol for dollar amounts in running text. For round
numbers in the millions and billions, use a numeral with a spelled-out unit.

**Examples**:

- "The settlement totals **$2.5 million**."
- "The filing fee is **$350**."
- "Damages exceeded **$1 billion**."

---

### Statutes, Sections, and Legal References

**Condition**: The number is a statutory section number, article number, code provision, or
similar legal reference.

**Rule**: Always use numerals for legal reference numbers regardless of the normal
spell-out threshold.

**Examples**:

- "ORS **174.010**" (not: "ORS one hundred seventy-four point zero one zero")
- "Section **5** of the Administrative Procedures Act"
- "Article **IV**, Section **1**"

---

## Exceptions: Always Spell Out

### Beginning of a Sentence (§9.5)

**Condition**: A number begins a sentence.

**Rule**: Never begin a sentence with a numeral. Spell out the number, or rewrite the sentence
so the number does not appear first.

**Examples**:

- Correct: "**Twenty-three** attorneys attended the hearing."
- Rewritten: "The hearing was attended by **23** attorneys."
- Incorrect: "*23 attorneys* attended the hearing."

---

### Fractions (§9.15)

**Condition**: The sentence states a simple fraction in running text.

**Rule**: Spell out simple fractions in running text. Use numerals only for complex fractions or
when paired with whole numbers using unit symbols.

**Examples**:

- "**Two-thirds** of the board approved the resolution."
- "The contractor completed **one-half** of the project."
- "The measurement is **3½ inches**." (whole number + fraction with unit)

---

## Large Round Numbers (§9.8)

**Condition**: The sentence contains a large round number (in the millions, billions, etc.).

**Rule**: Use a numeral followed by a spelled-out unit for round numbers expressed in full
units. This improves readability.

**Examples**:

- "The budget is **$4 million**." (not: "$4,000,000")
- "The population exceeds **2.3 billion**."
- "The statute covers **50 million** accounts."

---

## Ordinal Numbers (§9.6)

### Ordinals in Running Text

**Condition**: The sentence uses an ordinal number (first, second, third, etc.).

**Rule**: Spell out ordinals from *first* through *ninth* in running text. Use numerals with
suffixes (*10th*, *21st*, *103rd*) for tenth and above.

**Examples**:

- "This is the **third** amendment to the contract."
- "The court issued its **25th** opinion of the term."
- "The **first** and **second** assignments of error were waived."

---

### Ordinals in Dates

**Condition**: An ordinal appears in a date.

**Rule**: Do not use ordinal suffixes (*st*, *nd*, *rd*, *th*) when the day follows the month.
Ordinal form is acceptable when the day precedes the month or stands alone.

**Examples**:

- "Filed on **June 15**." (day after month: no suffix)
- "Filed on the **15th of June**." (day before month: ordinal OK)
- "Filed on the **15th**." (day alone: ordinal)

---

## Roman Numerals (§9.65–9.67)

**Condition**: The text uses Roman numerals.

**Rule**: Use Roman numerals for traditional contexts: monarchs and popes (*Henry VIII*,
*John Paul II*), preliminary pages of a book (*pp. iii–xv*), major divisions of formal
documents (article or part numbers in contracts), and properly named acts or instruments.

**Examples**:

- "Article **III** of the Oregon Constitution"
- "Volume **IV** of the treatise"
- "Chapter **I** of the Administrative Procedures Act"

---

## Numbers in a Series

### Series of Numbers (§9.7)

**Condition**: A sentence lists several numbers of the same type.

**Rule**: If any number in the series is above one hundred (and thus a numeral), use numerals
for all numbers in the series. Apply the consistency rule.

**Example**:

- "The committee met **8** times in January, **12** times in February, and **143** times
  in March." (143 is a numeral, so all three become numerals for consistency)

---

### Mixed Categories

**Condition**: Numbers of different categories appear in the same sentence.

**Rule**: Treat each category separately. A number that would ordinarily be spelled out in its
category need not switch to a numeral just because another unrelated numeral appears nearby.

**Example**:

- "The **three** judges reviewed the **147-page** record."
  (*Three* = count of persons (spell out); *147-page* = measurement (numeral))
