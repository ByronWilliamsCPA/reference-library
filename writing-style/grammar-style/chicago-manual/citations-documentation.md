# Citations and Documentation (CMS Chapters 14–15)

> **Source**: Paraphrased from *The Chicago Manual of Style*, 17th edition (2017), Chapters 14–15.
> CMS section references are provided for verification. No verbatim CMS prose appears here.
>
> **Authority**: CMS Ch. 14–15 governs citation format for non-legal documents.
> For Oregon legal citation conventions (cases, statutes, rules), consult
> `../../../legal-style/QUICK-START.md` and the Appellate Style Manual.
>
> **Two systems**: CMS provides two parallel citation systems. Use notes-bibliography for
> humanities; use author-date for sciences and social sciences.

---

## Which System to Use

| Discipline | System | Citation Form |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Humanities (history, philosophy, literature, law) | Notes-Bibliography | Footnote/endnote + bibliography |
| Sciences, social sciences (psychology, sociology, economics) | Author-Date | In-text parenthetical + reference list |
| Mixed or general audience | Either; pick one and apply consistently | — |

**Note**: Legal writing uses its own citation system (Bluebook, ALWD, Oregon Appellate Style).
The CMS systems apply to academic and professional writing in non-legal documents.

---

## Notes-Bibliography System

### Footnotes vs. Endnotes (§14.24–14.28)

**Condition**: The document uses the notes-bibliography system.

**Rule**: Footnotes appear at the bottom of the page where the citation appears. Endnotes
appear at the end of the chapter or document. Both use superscript Arabic numerals in the
text (*¹*, *²*, *³*). Footnotes are preferred when the reader benefits from immediate access
to the source.

---

### Full Citation: Book (§14.18–14.26)

**Format**: Author Last Name, First Name. *Title of Work: Subtitle*. Place of Publication:
Publisher, Year.

**Example**:

- Garner, Bryan A. *Legal Writing in Plain English: A Text with Exercises*. Chicago:
  University of Chicago Press, 2001.

**Footnote form** (full citation on first reference):

- ¹ Bryan A. Garner, *Legal Writing in Plain English: A Text with Exercises* (Chicago:
  University of Chicago Press, 2001), 45.
  (Note inverted order: first name first in footnotes; last name first only in bibliography)

---

### Full Citation: Article in a Periodical (§14.170–14.172)

**Format**: Author Last Name, First Name. "Article Title." *Journal Name* Volume, no. Issue
(Year): Page range.

**Example**:

- Smith, Jane. "Statutory Interpretation in Oregon." *Oregon Law Review* 95, no. 2
  (2016): 345–82.

**Footnote form**:

- ² Jane Smith, "Statutory Interpretation in Oregon," *Oregon Law Review* 95, no. 2
  (2016): 345.

---

### Full Citation: Chapter in an Edited Book (§14.103–14.106)

**Format**: Author Last Name, First Name. "Chapter Title." In *Book Title*, edited by
Editor First Name Last Name, Page range. Place: Publisher, Year.

**Example**:

- Jones, Robert. "Plain Meaning and Statutory Purpose." In *Perspectives on Statutory
  Interpretation*, edited by Mary Williams, 67–89. New York: Foundation Press, 2010.

---

### Full Citation: Website (§14.207–14.211)

**Format**: Author Last Name, First Name (if known). "Page Title." Website Name. Last
modified or accessed Date. URL.

**Example**:

- Oregon Legislative Assembly. "Oregon Revised Statutes." Oregon Legislature. Accessed
  February 20, 2026. <https://www.oregonlegislature.gov/bills_laws/Pages/ORS.aspx>.

---

### Shortened Citations (§14.29–14.36)

**Condition**: A source cited in full in a prior footnote is cited again.

**Rule**: After the first full citation, use a shortened form: Author Last Name, *Short Title*,
Page number.

**Example**:

- First cite: ¹ Bryan A. Garner, *Legal Writing in Plain English* (Chicago: University of
  Chicago Press, 2001), 45.
- Subsequent cite: ⁵ Garner, *Legal Writing*, 67.

---

### *Ibid.* (§14.34)

**Condition**: A footnote cites the same work as the immediately preceding footnote.

**Rule**: Use *Ibid.* (set in roman, not italics) to repeat the immediately preceding citation.
Add a page number if different from the preceding citation.

**Examples**:

- ³ Garner, *Legal Writing*, 45.
- ⁴ *Ibid.* (same page as footnote 3)
- ⁵ *Ibid.*, 67. (same work as footnote 3, different page)

**Note**: CMS now considers shortened citations generally preferable to *ibid.* because
they remain clear if footnotes are renumbered during editing. Use *ibid.* only when the
source immediately precedes and confusion is impossible.

---

### Bibliography Format (§14.64–14.67)

**Condition**: The document uses the notes-bibliography system and requires a bibliography.

**Rule**: The bibliography appears at the end of the document (or chapter). Entries are
listed alphabetically by author's last name. Entries are formatted with a hanging indent
(first line flush left; subsequent lines indented). Unlike footnotes, bibliography entries
use last-name-first order for the first author.

**Format differences between footnote and bibliography entries**:

- Footnote: Author First Last, *Title* (Place: Publisher, Year), Page.
- Bibliography: Last, First. *Title*. Place: Publisher, Year.

**Example bibliography entry**:
> Garner, Bryan A. *Legal Writing in Plain English: A Text with Exercises*. Chicago:
> University of Chicago Press, 2001.

## Author-Date System

### In-Text Parenthetical Citation (§15.24–15.30)

**Condition**: The document uses the author-date system.

**Rule**: Cite sources with a parenthetical in the text immediately after the cited material.
The parenthetical contains the author's last name, year, and page number (if applicable),
separated by a comma.

**Examples**:

- "(Garner 2001)" — general reference to the work
- "(Garner 2001, 45)" — specific page
- "(Smith and Jones 2010)" — two authors
- "(Williams et al. 2015)" — three or more authors

---

### Reference List Format (§15.12–15.14)

**Condition**: The document uses author-date and requires a reference list.

**Rule**: The reference list appears at the end of the document. Entries are listed
alphabetically by author's last name, then chronologically for the same author.
Format: Last, First. Year. *Title*. Place: Publisher.

**Book**:
> Garner, Bryan A. 2001. *Legal Writing in Plain English: A Text with Exercises*. Chicago:
> University of Chicago Press.

**Article**:
> Smith, Jane. 2016. "Statutory Interpretation in Oregon." *Oregon Law Review* 95 (2): 345–82.

**Chapter**:
> Jones, Robert. 2010. "Plain Meaning and Statutory Purpose." In *Perspectives on Statutory
> Interpretation*, edited by Mary Williams, 67–89. New York: Foundation Press.

---

### Author-Date with Multiple Works in Same Year (§15.18)

**Condition**: An author has two or more works published in the same year.

**Rule**: Add a lowercase letter after the year in both the in-text citation and the reference
list to distinguish the works.

**Example**:

- "(Garner 2001a)" and "(Garner 2001b)"
- Reference list entries:
  - Garner, Bryan A. 2001a. *Legal Writing in Plain English*. …
  - Garner, Bryan A. 2001b. *The Elements of Legal Style*. …

## Special Topics

### Multiple Sources in One Footnote (§14.57)

**Condition**: A single point relies on multiple sources cited in the same footnote.

**Rule**: Separate the citations with semicolons within the footnote.

**Example**:

- ¹ Garner, *Legal Writing*, 45; Smith, "Statutory Interpretation," 350.

---

### Citing Statutes and Regulations (§14.275–14.278)

For statutes and regulations in non-legal documents, CMS uses a simplified format.
**For Oregon legal documents, use the Oregon Appellate Style Manual conventions instead.**

CMS general format for statutes:

- Name of Act, Public Law number, U.S. Statutes at Large citation, codified at U.S. Code
  citation (year if specifying an edition).
- Example: Administrative Procedures Act, Pub. L. 79-404, 60 Stat. 237, codified at
  5 U.S.C. §§ 551–559 (2018).

For Oregon Revised Statutes in legal briefs, see:
`../../../legal-style/appellate-style-manual/` for citation form conventions.

---

### Citing Cases (§14.272–14.274)

CMS acknowledges that legal citations follow specialized formats (Bluebook, ALWD, state
rules) rather than CMS conventions. For any document that may be filed with a court or
reviewed by attorneys, use the governing legal citation format.

- For Oregon appellate briefs: Oregon Appellate Courts Style Manual (2002) controls.
- For Oregon citations generally: see `../../../legal-style/QUICK-START.md`.

---

### Citing Online Sources (§14.5–14.6, §14.207–14.214)

**Condition**: A source exists only online or is most reliably accessed online.

**Rule**: Provide a DOI (digital object identifier) when available; otherwise provide the URL.
Include an access date when the source may change or lacks a publication date.

**DOI format**: <https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx>
**URL format**: Full URL, breaking the line before a slash or after a double-slash if needed.

**Example**:

- Author, Title. *Journal* volume, no. issue (year): pages. <https://doi.org/10.xxxx/xxxxx>.
