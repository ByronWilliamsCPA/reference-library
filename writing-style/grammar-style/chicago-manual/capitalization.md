## Titles of Works: Headline-Style Capitalization

### Headline-Style Rule (§8.157)

**Condition**: A work's title appears in text (book, article, statute, case name, film, etc.).

**Rule**: Capitalize headline-style: the first word, the last word, and all "major words" in
between. Lowercase articles (*a*, *an*, *the*), coordinating conjunctions (*and*, *but*, *or*,
*nor*, *for*, *so*, *yet*), and prepositions of any length — unless they are the first or last
word of the title.

**Capitalized (major words)**:

- Nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs
- Pronouns
- Subordinating conjunctions (*although*, *because*, *since*, *when*)
- Prepositions when used as adverbs or adjectives (*Look Up*; *Turn On*)

**Lowercase (minor words)**:

- Articles: *a*, *an*, *the*
- Coordinating conjunctions: *and*, *but*, *or*, *nor*, *for*, *so*, *yet*
- Prepositions: *in*, *on*, *at*, *by*, *of*, *for*, *to*, *from*, *with*, *between*, etc.

**Examples**:

- *The Rule of Law in Modern Society*
- *A Brief in Support of Summary Judgment*
- *On the Nature of Statutory Construction*
- *Between the Lines: A Guide to Legal Writing*
  (*Between* is the first word → capitalized; *the* is article → lowercase)

---

### Sentence-Style Capitalization (§8.158)

**Condition**: A document uses sentence-style capitalization for titles (common in scientific
and academic articles, and in some document management systems).

**Rule**: Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. Everything else is lowercase.

**Example**:

- Headline-style: *The Elements of Legal Style*
- Sentence-style: *The elements of legal style*

**Note**: CMS uses headline-style as the default for titles in running prose. Sentence-style
is used for reference list entries in author-date systems and in some academic disciplines.

## Titles of People

### Titles Before a Name (§8.20–8.21)

**Condition**: A title precedes a person's name.

**Rule**: Capitalize a title when it immediately precedes a personal name as part of that
name.

**Examples**:

- "**Chief Justice** Roberts wrote the opinion."
- "**Governor** Brown signed the bill."
- "**Senator** Smith introduced the amendment."

---

### Titles After a Name or Used Alone (§8.22–8.23)

**Condition**: A title follows a name, is used in place of a name in general reference, or
is used descriptively.

**Rule**: Lowercase the title when it follows a name, appears in apposition, or is used alone
as a common noun rather than as part of a name.

**Examples**:

- "John Roberts, **chief justice** of the United States, presided."
  (*chief justice* follows the name: lowercase)
- "The **governor** signed the bill yesterday."
  (Used alone as a description: lowercase)
- "Three **senators** voted against the measure."
  (Generic reference: lowercase)

**Exception**: In some formal contexts (government documents, judicial opinions), titles may
be capitalized as a convention. Check the document type's governing style. In Oregon legal
writing, *Court* (capitalized) refers specifically to the court deciding the case.

---

### Governmental and Institutional Titles (§8.19–8.20)

**Condition**: The title refers to a specific named office or institution.

**Rule**: Capitalize official titles of offices and institutions when used as proper names. Do
not capitalize when used descriptively or generically.

**Examples**:

- "The **President** signed the executive order." (specific office, formal context)
- "Three **presidents** have visited Oregon."
- "The **Oregon Legislature** convened in January."
- "State **legislatures** vary in their procedures."

## Proper Nouns and Proper Adjectives (§8.1–8.2)

### Proper Nouns (§8.1)

**Condition**: The noun is the name of a specific person, place, organization, or thing.

**Rule**: Capitalize proper nouns. A proper noun names a specific individual member of a class,
as distinguished from a general class name.

**Examples**:

- "**Oregon** amended its constitution in 2020."
- "The **Oregon Supreme Court** issued the ruling."
- "The **Administrative Procedures Act** governs this proceeding."

---

### Proper Adjectives (§8.3)

**Condition**: An adjective derives from a proper noun.

**Rule**: Capitalize proper adjectives that retain their proper-noun connection. Lowercase
adjectives that have lost that connection through common usage.

**Examples**:

- Capitalized: "**Oregon** law," "**Freudian** analysis," "**Shakespearean** sonnet"
- Lowercase: "french dressing," "venetian blinds" (conventional, no longer tied to origin)

---

### Organizations, Institutions, and Government Bodies (§8.62–8.65)

**Condition**: The sentence refers to a formal organization, agency, or government body.

**Rule**: Capitalize the full official name. Once the full name is established, a shortened
form may be capitalized or lowercased depending on context (full proper name → capitalize
shortened form).

**Examples**:

- Full name: "the **Oregon Department of Justice**"
- Short form (formal context): "the **Department**" (when referring back to the same entity)
- Generic: "the department," "a state agency"

## Commonly Lowercase Words

### Seasons (§8.88)

**Condition**: The sentence names a season (*spring*, *summer*, *fall*, *autumn*, *winter*).

**Rule**: Seasons are not proper nouns and are not capitalized.

**Examples**:

- "The legislature meets in **winter** and **summer** sessions."
- "The filing deadline is **spring** 2024."

---

### Compass Directions (§8.47)

**Condition**: The sentence contains a compass direction (*north*, *south*, *east*, *west*).

**Rule**: Lowercase compass directions when they describe a direction. Capitalize when used
as a proper noun to name a recognized geographic region.

**Examples**:

- Direction (lowercase): "Drive **north** on I-5."
- Geographic region (capitalized): "She grew up in the **South**."
- Region (capitalized): "**Pacific Northwest** timber industry"
- Description (lowercase): "the **northwest** corner of the property"

---

### *Court* and *State* in Legal Writing

**Condition**: The sentence uses *court* or *state* as a noun.

**Rule** (Oregon legal conventions): In Oregon court documents, capitalize *Court* when it
refers to the specific court deciding the present case. Capitalize *State* when it refers to
Oregon as a party. Use lowercase for generic or general references.

**Examples**:

- "The **Court** holds that the statute is constitutional." (this court, deciding the case)
- "Three courts have addressed this question." (generic)
- "The **State** charged defendant with three counts." (Oregon as party)
- "Every **state** has its own procedures." (generic)

## Capitalization of Titles in Legal Documents

### Defined Terms (Oregon Legal)

**Condition**: A document defines a term and uses that definition throughout.

**Rule**: Once a term is defined, many Oregon legal documents capitalize it throughout to
signal the reader that the defined meaning applies. CMS does not specifically address
defined-term capitalization in contracts and statutes; this practice is conventional in
legal drafting.

**Example**:

- "As used in this section, '**Director**' means the Director of the Department of Revenue."

---

### Headings and Section Labels (§8.178)

**Condition**: A document uses headings or section labels.

**Rule**: Apply consistent capitalization throughout the document. CMS recommends
headline-style for headings at all levels within a document. Sentence-style is acceptable
if applied consistently.

**Example of consistent headline-style headings**:

- `# Elements of the Claim`
- `## First Element: Duty of Care`
- `### Establishing the Standard`
