## Subject-Verb Agreement

### General Rule

If the subject is singular, the verb is singular. If the subject is plural, the verb is plural.
Errors arise when words intervene between subject and verb or when the subject appears to be a
different number than it is.

---

### Collective Nouns (§5.7–5.9)

**Condition**: The subject is a collective noun (*committee*, *jury*, *team*, *group*).

**Rule**: In American English, collective nouns take a singular verb when the group acts as a
unit. Use a plural verb only when members act individually and the individual action is the point.

**Examples**:

- "The committee **has** reached a decision." (unit action → singular)
- "The jury **were** divided in their opinions." (individual actions → plural; formal British style)
- American standard: "The jury **was** divided." (singular preferred in U.S. legal prose)

---

### *Either/Or* and *Neither/Nor* (§5.10)

**Condition**: The subject uses *either … or* or *neither … nor*.

**Rule**: The verb agrees with the nearer subject (the one closest to the verb).

**Examples**:

- "Either the attorney or the clients **are** responsible."
- "Either the clients or the attorney **is** responsible."
- "Neither the judge nor the clerks **were** present."

---

### *None* (§5.258)

**Condition**: The subject is *none*.

**Rule**: Notional agreement governs. When *none* refers to a plural concept (no members of a
group), use a plural verb. When *none* refers to a singular concept (not one), use a singular verb.

**Examples**:

- "None of the briefs **were** timely filed." (plural concept: no briefs)
- "None of the money **was** recovered." (singular concept: no amount)

---

### *Number* as Subject (§5.11)

**Condition**: The subject is the phrase *a number of* or *the number of*.

**Rule**: *A number of* (indefinite) takes a plural verb. *The number of* (definite) takes a
singular verb.

**Examples**:

- "A number of issues **remain** unresolved."
- "The number of issues **is** significant."

---

### Intervening Phrases (§5.6)

**Condition**: A phrase (*along with*, *as well as*, *in addition to*, *together with*) separates
subject and verb.

**Rule**: These phrases do not change the grammatical number of the subject. The verb agrees
with the original subject, not with the intervening phrase.

**Example**:

- "The attorney, along with two partners, **was** present."
  (Not: "The attorney, along with two partners, *were* present.")

## Pronoun Agreement and Reference

### Basic Agreement (§5.28–5.29)

**Condition**: A pronoun replaces a noun.

**Rule**: The pronoun must agree with its antecedent in number, person, and gender. Errors arise
most often with collective nouns and indefinite pronouns.

**Examples**:

- "Each attorney must submit **his or her** brief." (singular indefinite antecedent)
- "All attorneys must submit **their** briefs." (plural antecedent)

---

### Singular *They* (§5.48)

**Condition**: The antecedent is a singular person of unknown or nonbinary gender, or the
sentence requires a gender-neutral pronoun.

**Rule**: *They*, *them*, and *their* are acceptable as singular pronouns in these contexts.
This usage is grammatically established and widely accepted.

**Examples**:

- "Each participant should bring **their** identification."
- "The applicant stated that **they** had filed on time."

---

### Indefinite Pronouns (§5.63)

**Condition**: The subject is an indefinite pronoun (*anyone*, *everyone*, *someone*, *no one*,
*each*, *every*, *either*, *neither*).

**Rule**: These pronouns are grammatically singular and take singular verbs and pronouns.
*Both*, *few*, *many*, *others*, and *several* are plural.

**Examples**:

- "Everyone **has** submitted **their** brief." (singular verb; singular *they* acceptable)
- "Both parties **have** responded."

---

## Voice: Active and Passive (§5.117–5.118)

### When to Use Active Voice

**Condition**: The actor and the action are both relevant, and the sentence can be written
with the actor as subject.

**Rule**: Use active voice as the default. Active voice is direct, vigorous, and clear.
The actor appears as the grammatical subject.

**Example**: "The legislature amended §174.010 in 2023."

---

### When Passive Voice Is Appropriate

**Condition**: The actor is unknown, unimportant, or should be de-emphasized; or the recipient
of the action is the logical topic.

**Rule**: Passive voice is grammatically correct and sometimes preferable. Do not avoid it
when it serves the sentence. Three circumstances favor passive voice:

1. The actor is unknown: "The record was filed late."
2. The actor is unimportant: "The contract was executed in 2019."
3. The actor should be de-emphasized: "The motion was denied." (emphasizes outcome, not court)

---

## Mood: Indicative, Imperative, Subjunctive (§5.121–5.123)

### Subjunctive Mood (§5.121)

**Condition**: The clause expresses a condition contrary to fact, a wish, a doubt, a
recommendation, or a requirement after *that*.

**Rule**: Use subjunctive form (*were*, *be*) in these clauses.

**Examples**:

- Contrary to fact: "If the statute **were** amended, the result would differ."
  (Not: "If the statute *was* amended." — indicative; implies it may have been)
- Wish: "I wish the record **were** complete."
- Recommendation: "The court recommends that counsel **be** present."
- Requirement: "The rule requires that each filing **be** signed."

---

### Indicative vs. Subjunctive: If-Clauses

**Condition**: An *if* clause introduces a hypothetical.

**Rule**: If the condition is genuinely hypothetical or contrary to fact, use subjunctive (*were*,
*be*). If the condition is merely open to question, use indicative (*was*, *is*).

**Examples**:

- Contrary to fact (subjunctive): "If the deadline **were** extended, the parties could respond."
- Open question (indicative): "If the deadline **was** extended, we need to confirm the date."

---

## Parallel Structure (§5.239–5.241)

### Basic Parallel Structure Rule

**Condition**: A sentence joins two or more elements in a list, a comparison, or a coordinated
structure.

**Rule**: Parallel elements must use the same grammatical form. Nouns with nouns, verb phrases
with verb phrases, infinitives with infinitives, and clauses with clauses.

**Examples**:

- Correct: "The brief must be clear, concise, and accurate." (three adjectives)
- Incorrect: "The brief must be clear, concise, and it must be accurate." (adjective, adjective, clause)

---

### Parallel Structure with Correlatives (§5.240)

**Condition**: The sentence uses a correlative pair: *both … and*, *either … or*, *neither … nor*,
*not only … but also*.

**Rule**: The element following the first correlative must be grammatically parallel to the
element following the second.

**Examples**:

- Correct: "The court considered **both** the text **and** the legislative history."
- Incorrect: "The court considered **both** the text **and** it reviewed the legislative history."
- Correct: "She **not only** argued the motion **but also** filed the brief."

---

### Faulty Parallelism with Lists

**Condition**: A sentence contains a bulleted or numbered list, or a list set off by a colon.

**Rule**: Every item must be the same grammatical form (all nouns, all gerunds, all complete
clauses). Mix one form with another and the list fails.

**Example of correction**:

- Incorrect: "The statute governs: (1) filing deadlines, (2) how service is made, and
  (3) to respond to motions."
- Correct: "The statute governs: (1) filing deadlines, (2) service requirements, and
  (3) response procedures."

---

## Modifiers: Dangling and Misplaced (§5.115–5.116)

### Dangling Participles (§5.116)

**Condition**: A participial phrase opens the sentence.

**Rule**: The participial phrase must modify the grammatical subject of the main clause.
If it modifies any other noun, it is dangling.

**Examples**:

- Dangling: "**Having reviewed the record**, the motion was denied."
  (The motion did not review the record.)
- Correct: "**Having reviewed the record**, the court denied the motion."

---

### Misplaced *Only* (§5.220)

**Condition**: The modifier *only* appears in the sentence.

**Rule**: Place *only* immediately before the word or phrase it modifies. Misplacing *only*
changes meaning.

**Examples**:

- "She **only** filed the brief." (She only filed it; she did nothing else to it.)
- "She filed **only** the brief." (She filed nothing else.)
- "**Only** she filed the brief." (No one else filed it.)

---

## Restrictive vs. Nonrestrictive Clauses (§5.254)

### Restrictive Relative Clauses

**Condition**: A relative clause is essential to identifying which noun is meant.

**Rule**: Use *that* (no comma) for restrictive clauses in American formal prose.

**Example**: "The statute **that** governs this transaction is ORS 174.010."
(The *that*-clause identifies which statute; omitting it changes meaning.)

---

### Nonrestrictive Relative Clauses

**Condition**: A relative clause adds information about a noun already sufficiently identified.

**Rule**: Use *which* preceded by a comma for nonrestrictive clauses. The clause can be removed
without changing the meaning of the sentence.

**Example**: "ORS 174.010, **which** was amended in 2023, governs this transaction."
(The statute is already identified; the *which*-clause adds information only.)

---

## Pronoun Case (§5.46–5.62)

### *Who* vs. *Whom* (§5.62)

**Condition**: A relative or interrogative pronoun must be either *who* or *whom*.

**Rule**: *Who* is the subject form; *whom* is the object form. Substitute *he* for *who* and
*him* for *whom*: if *him* fits, use *whom*.

**Examples**:

- "**Who** wrote the brief?" → "**He** wrote the brief." → *who*
- "The attorney **whom** you hired." → "you hired **him**." → *whom*
- "Give the document to **whoever** asks." → "give to **him** who asks" → *whoever*

---

### Pronoun Case After Prepositions

**Condition**: A pronoun follows a preposition (*to*, *for*, *by*, *with*, *between*).

**Rule**: Always use the object form of the pronoun after a preposition.

**Examples**:

- Correct: "between you and **me**" (not: "between you and *I*")
- Correct: "The decision applies to **him** and **her**."
- Correct: "**To whom** should I address the letter?"

---

## Tense Consistency (§5.128–5.132)

### Literary Present Tense (§5.130)

**Condition**: The sentence discusses the content of a written work (statute, case, contract,
rule) that is still in force or remains the subject of discussion.

**Rule**: Use present tense. The work "says," "provides," "holds," "states," or "requires" —
not "said," "provided," or "held" (past tense), even if the work was written long ago.

**Examples**:

- "ORS 174.010 **provides** that statutory words have their ordinary meaning."
- "The court **holds** that the statute is constitutional."
- "The contract **requires** thirty days' written notice."

**Exception**: Past tense for historical facts and procedural events that are over:
"The trial court **denied** the motion." "The legislature **amended** the statute in 2023."
