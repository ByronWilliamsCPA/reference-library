# Punctuation (CMS Chapters 6–7)

> **Source**: Paraphrased from *The Chicago Manual of Style*, 17th edition (2017), Chapters 6–7.
> CMS section references are provided for verification. No verbatim CMS prose appears here.
>
> **Authority**: CMS Ch. 6–7 governs. The one user-confirmed PCP override applies here:
> **no em-dashes** (cross-reference.md #21). Substitute commas, colons, semicolons, or parentheses.
>
> **Note**: This file covers comma, semicolon, colon, en dash, quotation marks, and apostrophe.
> Em-dash is documented only as a banned substitute (PCP override).

---

## Comma

### Serial (Oxford) Comma (§6.19)

**Condition**: Three or more items appear in a series in running text.

**Rule**: Place a comma after every item in the series, including the item immediately before
the final coordinating conjunction (*and*, *or*, *nor*).

**Examples**:

- "The brief addresses jurisdiction, standing, and the merits."
- "The attorney argued clearly, persuasively, or at least forcefully."

**Why this matters**: Without the serial comma, "jurisdiction, standing and the merits" could
imply that *standing and the merits* is a single compound item. The serial comma prevents that
misreading.

---

### Comma Before Coordinating Conjunction (§6.22)

**Condition**: Two independent clauses are joined by a coordinating conjunction
(*and*, *but*, *or*, *nor*, *for*, *so*, *yet*).

**Rule**: Place a comma before the conjunction. The comma is required when both clauses are
independent (each has a subject and verb that could stand as a sentence).

**Examples**:

- "She drafted the motion, and he filed it."
- "The court granted the continuance, but the deadline remained."
- "The statute is clear, so no interpretation is needed."

**Exception**: If both clauses are very short and closely related, the comma may be omitted.
Use judgment; when in doubt, include the comma.

---

### Comma After Introductory Elements (§6.29–6.31)

**Condition**: The sentence opens with an introductory phrase, clause, or word before the
main subject-verb.

**Rule**: Use a comma after the introductory element. For a very short introductory phrase
(roughly four words or fewer) where there is no risk of misreading, the comma is optional.
For longer introductory elements, always use the comma.

**Examples**:

- "After reviewing the record, the court denied the motion."
  (Introductory prepositional phrase: comma required)
- "If the statute is ambiguous, the court looks to legislative history."
  (Introductory clause: comma required)
- "In 2023, the legislature amended the provision."
  (Short phrase: comma standard)
- "Today the legislature convened." (Very short; comma optional)

---

### Nonrestrictive (Parenthetical) Clauses and Phrases (§6.27–6.28)

**Condition**: A clause or phrase is not essential to identifying the noun it modifies. Removing
it would not change the sentence's meaning.

**Rule**: Set off nonrestrictive elements with commas on both sides (or with a single comma if
the element ends the sentence).

**Examples**:

- "ORS 174.010, which governs statutory construction, was amended in 2023."
  (Nonrestrictive: the statute is already identified)
- "The defendant, a licensed contractor, failed to obtain a permit."
  (Nonrestrictive appositive: both commas required)

**Contrast — restrictive (no commas)**:

- "The statute that governs statutory construction was amended in 2023."
  (Restrictive: the clause identifies which statute)

---

### Comma with Direct Address and Interjections (§6.35–6.36)

**Condition**: The sentence includes a direct address (using a name or title) or an introductory
interjection (*Yes*, *No*, *Well*, *Indeed*).

**Rule**: Set off direct address and interjections with commas.

**Examples**:

- "Counsel, please approach the bench."
- "Yes, the motion was timely filed."
- "Well, the record supports the finding."

## Semicolon

### Semicolon Between Independent Clauses (§6.56–6.59)

**Condition**: Two independent clauses are closely related in thought and should appear in
the same sentence without a coordinating conjunction.

**Rule**: Use a semicolon to join the two clauses. The semicolon signals a close relationship
without subordinating either clause.

**Examples**:

- "The statute is clear; no interpretation is required."
- "The motion was timely; the court must address it on the merits."

---

### Semicolon with Conjunctive Adverbs (§6.57)

**Condition**: Two independent clauses are joined by a conjunctive adverb (*however*,
*therefore*, *moreover*, *consequently*, *nevertheless*, *indeed*, *thus*, *hence*).

**Rule**: Place a semicolon before the conjunctive adverb and a comma after it.

**Examples**:

- "The record is incomplete; **however**, the court can decide the question of law."
- "The statute is ambiguous; **therefore**, the court consults legislative history."
- "The motion was untimely; **nevertheless**, the court granted relief."

---

### Semicolon in Complex Series (§6.60)

**Condition**: A list already contains internal commas, so using commas between items would
be confusing.

**Rule**: Use semicolons to separate items in a series when the items themselves contain commas.

**Example**:

- "The committee included Smith, the chair; Jones, the treasurer; and Williams, the secretary."
  (Without semicolons, the list of three would look like six items.)

---

## Colon

### Colon to Introduce a List (§6.61)

**Condition**: A complete clause is followed by a list, examples, or enumerated items.

**Rule**: Use a colon after the complete introductory clause. Do not use a colon after an
incomplete clause such as *the rule is* or *including*.

**Examples**:

- Correct: "The statute requires three elements: notice, opportunity to be heard, and a decision."
  (Complete clause before colon)
- Incorrect: "The statute requires: notice, opportunity to be heard, and a decision."
  (Verb without a complement before colon; remove the colon)

---

### Colon to Introduce a Quotation or Elaboration (§6.63–6.65)

**Condition**: A complete clause is followed by a block quotation, an explanatory statement,
or a restatement that amplifies what the clause said.

**Rule**: Use a colon after the introducing clause.

**Examples**:

- "The court stated the rule clearly: statutes are read according to their plain meaning."
- "One conclusion follows: the motion must be denied."

---

### Colon Between Title and Subtitle (§6.68)

**Condition**: A title has both a title and a subtitle.

**Rule**: Separate them with a colon and a space. Capitalize the first word of the subtitle.

**Example**: *Legal Writing in Plain English: A Text with Exercises*

---

### Capitalization After a Colon (§6.66)

**Condition**: A colon introduces material in the same sentence.

**Rule**: In running prose, lowercase the word after a colon unless the material following the
colon is a proper noun, a complete sentence that the author treats as independent, or the
colon introduces two or more sentences.

**Examples**:

- Lowercase after colon (single phrase): "The rule has one exception: a voluntary waiver."
- Capitalize after colon (complete sentence): "The rule is clear: Statutes are read as written."
  (Either convention is acceptable; apply consistently)

---

## En Dash (§6.78–6.80)

### En Dash for Ranges

**Condition**: The sentence expresses a range of numbers, dates, or pages.

**Rule**: Use an en dash (–) between the first and last values of a range. The en dash means
"through" or "to."

**Examples**:

- "pages **12–27**"
- "**2019–2023**"
- "**§§ 174.010–174.090**"
- "the **January–March** quarter"

---

### En Dash with Open-Compound Modifiers (§6.80)

**Condition**: A compound modifier contains an open compound (two words that normally appear
without a hyphen) as one of its elements.

**Rule**: Use an en dash instead of a hyphen to join the compound to the other element.

**Examples**:

- "**post–World War II** legislation" (*World War II* is an open compound)
- "**pre–Civil War** statutes"
- "a **New York–based** firm"

---

### Em Dash (Banned — PCP Override)

The em dash has valid uses under CMS §6.85–6.87 (parenthetical insertions, abrupt breaks,
amplification). However, the user has confirmed a Tier 3 PromptCraft Pro override: **no em-dashes**.

**Substitutes**:

- For a parenthetical aside: use **commas** — "The motion, filed late, was denied."
- For elaboration after a clause: use a **colon** — "The result is clear: the motion fails."
- For an insertion: use **parentheses** — "The statute (first enacted in 1981) governs this case."
- For a break between clauses: use a **semicolon** — "The record is clear; the motion must fail."

See `../cross-reference.md` #15 and #21.

## Quotation Marks

### Punctuation Inside or Outside Closing Marks (§6.9)

**Condition**: A sentence ends with or contains a closing quotation mark, and a period or
comma is also needed at that point.

**Rule (U.S. style)**: Periods and commas always go inside the closing quotation mark.
Colons and semicolons always go outside. Question marks and exclamation points go inside
only when they are part of the quoted matter; otherwise they go outside.

**Examples**:

- Period inside: "The statute says 'plain meaning.'"
- Comma inside: "She argued 'plain meaning,' but the court disagreed."
- Colon outside: She cited "plain meaning": a well-established canon.
- Semicolon outside: The court rejected "plain meaning"; it applied legislative history.
- Question mark inside (part of quote): "Did the statute mean 'plain meaning'?"
  (the question is inherent in the quote)
- Question mark outside (not part of quote): Did the court apply "plain meaning"?
  (the question is the writer's, not the quoted material's)

---

### Block Quotations (§13.10–13.12)

**Condition**: A quotation is fifty words or more (CMS guideline), or five lines or more in
the formatted document.

**Rule**: Display it as a block quotation: indented from both margins, no quotation marks,
introduced by a colon (or other appropriate punctuation) in the sentence above it. The period
at the end of a block quotation precedes the footnote number (if any).

**Example format**:
> The court stated the governing rule:
>
> *[indented block quotation here, no quotation marks, citation in parenthetical after period]*

---

### Single Quotation Marks (§6.30)

**Condition**: A quotation appears within another quotation.

**Rule**: Use single quotation marks for the inner quotation.

**Example**:

- "The court noted that 'plain meaning' controls." →
  "She testified, 'The court said that "plain meaning" controls.'"

## Apostrophe and Possessives

### Singular Possessives (§7.17)

**Condition**: A singular noun needs the possessive form.

**Rule**: Add *'s* to singular nouns to form the possessive, regardless of the final letter.

**Examples**:

- "the **court's** ruling"
- "the **statute's** text"
- "the **boss's** decision"
- "the **class's** requirements"

---

### Singular Proper Names Ending in *s* (§7.20)

**Condition**: A singular proper name ends in *s* and needs the possessive form.

**Rule**: Add *'s* to most singular proper names ending in *s*. Exception: classical and
biblical names (*Jesus*, *Moses*, *Achilles*, *Xerxes*) take only an apostrophe.

**Examples**:

- "**Burns's** poetry"
- "**James's** brief"
- "**Williams's** argument"
- "**Jesus'** teachings" (classical exception)
- "**Moses'** law" (biblical exception)

---

### Plural Possessives (§7.18–7.19)

**Condition**: A plural noun needs the possessive form.

**Rule**: For plural nouns ending in *s*, add only an apostrophe. For irregular plurals not
ending in *s*, add *'s*.

**Examples**:

- "the **attorneys'** arguments" (plural ending in *s*: apostrophe only)
- "the **witnesses'** testimony"
- "the **children's** guardian" (irregular plural: add *'s*)
- "the **people's** choice"

---

### Contractions vs. Possessive Pronouns (§7.27)

**Condition**: The sentence uses *it's* or *its*, *who's* or *whose*, *they're* or *their*.

**Rule**: The apostrophe marks a contraction, not a possessive pronoun. Possessive pronouns
(*its*, *whose*, *their*, *hers*, *his*, *ours*, *yours*, *theirs*) never take apostrophes.

**Examples**:

- "*It's* cold today." (*It's* = *it is*)
- "The statute has *its* own definition." (*Its* = possessive; no apostrophe)
- "*Who's* arguing the motion?" (*Who's* = *who is*)
- "The attorney *whose* brief was late." (*Whose* = possessive; no apostrophe)
