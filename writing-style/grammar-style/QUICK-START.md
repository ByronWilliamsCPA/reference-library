# Grammar and Style Quick-Start

> Start here for 80% of daily lookups.
>
> **Three-Tier Authority** — read the FINAL RULE column; ignore the others unless you need the
> reason.
>
> | Tier | Source | When It Governs |
> | --- | --- | --- |
> | 1 | Elements of Style (EoS) | Drafting baseline; applies when CMS is silent |
> | 2 | Chicago Manual of Style, 17th ed. (CMS) | Final answer on grammar, punctuation, numbers |
> | 3 | PromptCraft Pro defaults (PCP) | User-confirmed overrides of CMS |
>
> **Only one confirmed Tier 3 override**: no em-dashes (PCP bans them; CMS §6.85–6.87 permits).
> Use a comma, colon, semicolon, or parentheses instead.

For a full conflict table, see `cross-reference.md`.
For deeper rules, see the source-specific files in `elements-of-style/` and `chicago-manual/`.

## High-Frequency Rules

| # | Topic | EoS | CMS | PCP Override | **FINAL RULE** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | **Serial (Oxford) comma** | Inconsistent; not reliably required | §6.19: required in a series of three or more | — | **Always use.** "red, white, and blue" |
| 2 | **Comma in compound sentence** | Use comma before coordinating conjunction | §6.22: required before *and/but/or/nor/for/so/yet* joining two independent clauses | — | **Use the comma.** "She drafted it, and he filed it." |
| 3 | ***That* vs. *which*** | Restrict *which* to nonrestrictive clauses (use comma) | §5.254: same general rule; *that* for restrictive, *which* + comma for nonrestrictive | — | *That* = restrictive (no comma). *Which* = nonrestrictive (with comma). "The rule **that** governs"; "ORS 174.010, **which** controls." |
| 4 | **Active vs. passive voice** | Strongly prefer active (Rule 14 / Principle 11) | §5.117: active is generally preferred; passive is valid when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or should be de-emphasized | — | **Active by default.** Passive when actor doesn't matter: "The motion was denied." |
| 5 | **Sentence fragments** | Avoid (Rule 5) | §5.238: acceptable for rhetorical effect — transition sentences, responses, emphasis | — | **Avoid in formal writing.** Permitted judiciously for emphasis or rhythm in less formal contexts. |
| 6 | **Split infinitives** | Discourage | §5.168: split infinitives are grammatically acceptable; splitting is often preferable when an adverb modifies only the infinitive | — | **Acceptable.** "to carefully review" is fine when the adverb belongs there. |
| 7 | **Prepositions at end of sentence** | Acceptable (explicitly permits) | §5.180: ending a sentence with a preposition is well established | — | **Acceptable.** "the rule to comply with" |
| 8 | **Numbers: spell out vs. numeral** | Spell out numbers below 10 | §9.2–9.3: spell out one through one hundred in running text; §9.7 exceptions for physical quantities, percentages, dates | — | **Spell out one through one hundred** in running text. Use numerals for dates, percentages, measurements, and any number used with a unit symbol. |
| 9 | **Em-dash** | Not addressed | §6.85–6.87: used parenthetically or to signal abrupt break | **PCP ban** | **No em-dashes.** Rewrite with commas, colons, semicolons, or parentheses. |
| 10 | **En dash** | Not addressed | §6.78–6.80: for ranges (pages 12–15) and compound adjectives with an open compound (post–World War II) | — | **Use for ranges and open-compound modifiers.** "pages 12–15"; "the pre–Civil War period" |
| 11 | **Colon vs. semicolon** | Colon introduces; limited semicolon guidance | §6.56: semicolon joins closely related independent clauses; §6.61: colon introduces list, quote, or elaboration after a complete clause | — | **Semicolon** between two related independent clauses. **Colon** to introduce or elaborate after a complete clause. |
| 12 | **Possessives: singular** | Form with 's (including names ending in *s*) | §7.17: add 's to singular nouns; §7.20: add 's to singular proper names ending in *s* (Burns's, James's) with exceptions for ancient names (Jesus', Moses') | — | **Add 's** to singular nouns and most proper names. Exceptions: Jesus', Moses', Achilles'. |
| 13 | **Possessives: plural** | Not explicitly addressed | §7.18: add only ' to plural nouns ending in *s* (witnesses'); add 's to irregular plurals (children's) | — | **Plural ending in *s***: add only '. **Irregular plural**: add 's. |
| 14 | ***Who* vs. *whom*** | Use *who* for subjects, *whom* for objects | §5.62: same rule; *who* = subject; *whom* = object of verb or preposition | — | **Who** does something. **Whom** receives the action or follows a preposition. "Who wrote it?"; "To whom was it sent?" |
| 15 | ***None*: singular or plural** | Treat as singular | §5.258: notional agreement — *none* takes a plural verb when it refers to a plural concept; singular when referring to a single unit | — | **Notional agreement.** "None of the arguments **were** persuasive." "None of the money **was** spent." |
| 16 | **Conjunctions starting sentences** | Explicitly permits (addresses the myth) | §5.206: starting a sentence with *and*, *but*, or *or* is well established | — | **Acceptable.** "But the court disagreed." |
| 17 | **Dangling modifier** | Participial phrase at start must refer to subject | §5.116: a participial or other introductory phrase must modify the grammatical subject of the main clause | — | **Error if the phrase doesn't refer to the subject.** "Walking to court, the brief was reviewed" is wrong. |
| 18 | **Capitalization: titles of works** | Not addressed in detail | §8.157: headline-style for titles — capitalize first and last word plus all major words; lowercase articles, prepositions, and coordinating conjunctions unless first or last | — | **Headline-style.** *The Rule of Law*; *A Brief on Standing* |
| 19 | **Quotation mark punctuation** | Not addressed | §6.9: periods and commas go inside closing quotation marks in U.S. style; colons and semicolons go outside | — | **Periods and commas inside.** "the 'reasonable person,'" not "the 'reasonable person'," |
| 20 | ***Due to* vs. *because of*** | Restrict to adjectival use ("Due to illness, …" where *due* is adjective) | §5.250: "due to" is widely accepted as a compound preposition meaning "because of" in formal prose | — | **Acceptable as compound preposition.** "Due to the amendment, the section changed." |
| 21 | **Parallel structure** | Express coordinate ideas in similar form (Principle 8) | §5.239–5.244: items joined by coordinating conjunctions or correlative pairs (*both…and*, *not only…but also*) must be grammatically parallel | — | **Match the form.** "She reviewed, drafted, and filed" — not "She reviewed, drafted, and the filing was hers." |
| 22 | **Conjunctive adverbs** (*however*, *therefore*, *moreover*, *thus*, *consequently*) | Not addressed | §6.57: use a semicolon before a conjunctive adverb joining two independent clauses; place a comma after the conjunctive adverb | — | **Semicolon before, comma after.** "The rule applies; however, the exception controls here." Error: "The rule applies, however the exception controls." |

## Authority Matrix

**When writing (drafting):** Apply EoS defaults. Keep sentences active, omit needless words,
use definite specific language.

**When editing (reviewing output):** Apply CMS as the final arbiter. Check the table above
first; see `cross-reference.md` for the full conflict list.

**When a PCP default conflicts with CMS:** Use the PCP default. Currently: em-dashes only.

**When neither EoS nor CMS addresses the issue:** Prefer the plain-English answer. If the
document type is legal or formal, consult `cross-reference.md` for any relevant guidance.
