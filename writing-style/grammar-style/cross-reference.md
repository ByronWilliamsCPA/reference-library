# EoS / CMS / PCP Cross-Reference

> Where the three sources diverge. Read this before drafting when you know a rule is contested.
>
> **Authority**: CMS always wins over EoS. PCP overrides win only where the user has
> confirmed the override (currently: em-dashes only).
>
> **Scope**: 26 confirmed divergences. Expanding toward the ~50+ actual EoS/CMS divergence
> points as high-frequency conflicts emerge in practice.
> Add new entries at the bottom; do not renumber existing entries.
>
> **CMS edition**: 17th edition (2017). Tag any update from a later edition as
> `[CMS 18th ed. change]`.

## Divergence Table

| # | Topic | What EoS Says | What CMS Says | PCP Override | Resolution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | **Serial (Oxford) comma** | Not consistently required; practice is inconsistent across editions | §6.19: required in a series of three or more items in running text | — | **CMS governs.** Always use the serial comma. |
| 2 | **Active voice** | Strongly preferred as a default rule (Rule 14 / Principle 11): "use the active voice" | §5.117: active is generally preferred but passive is valid when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or should be de-emphasized | — | **CMS governs.** Use passive when it serves the sentence: "The motion was denied." |
| 3 | **Sentence fragments** | Prohibited: "Do not break sentences in two" (Rule 5 equivalent) | §5.238: acceptable for rhetorical effect — transition sentences, short responses, emphatic statements | — | **CMS governs.** Permitted judiciously in less formal contexts. Avoid in formal legal or academic writing. |
| 4 | **Split infinitives** | Discouraged (White's revisions soften but do not endorse) | §5.168: split infinitives are grammatically correct; splitting is often preferable when the adverb modifies only the infinitive | — | **CMS governs.** Split when it produces the clearest sentence. "to clearly state" is fine. |
| 5 | ***None*: singular or plural** | Treat as singular: "None of them **is** guilty" | §5.258: notional agreement — *none* takes plural when the reference is clearly plural ("None of the arguments **were** compelling"); singular for a single-unit reference | — | **CMS governs.** Match notional meaning: "None of the briefs **were** filed" (plural concept). |
| 6 | **Paragraph structure** | One idea per paragraph; topic sentence at or near opening | No fixed rule; unity still valued but paragraph structure serves content and argument | — | **CMS governs.** Structure paragraphs to serve the argument. Single-topic paragraphs are still good practice. |
| 7 | **Numbers: spell-out threshold** | Spell out numbers one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above | §9.2–9.3: spell out one through one hundred in running text; many exceptions (dates, percentages, measurements, technical quantities) | — | **CMS governs.** Spell out one through one hundred; follow CMS ch. 9 exceptions. |
| 8 | ***Due to* as compound preposition** | Restrict to adjectival use: "Due to illness" where *due* modifies a noun | §5.250: "due to" is widely accepted as a compound preposition equivalent to "because of" in formal prose | — | **CMS governs.** "Due to the amendment, §174.010 changed" is acceptable. |
| 9 | **Comma after short introductory phrase** | Use a comma after introductory elements | §6.29: a comma may be omitted after a very short (roughly four words or fewer) introductory phrase when there is no risk of misreading | — | **CMS governs.** Optional for very short introductory phrases when meaning is clear. Always use for longer introductory elements. |
| 10 | ***Which* in restrictive clauses** | *Which* in a restrictive clause is an error; always use *that* for restrictive | §5.254: *that* is standard for restrictive relative clauses; *which* is standard for nonrestrictive clauses (with comma); however, *which* can occasionally appear in a restrictive clause in formal British usage | — | **CMS governs** (U.S. standard): *that* for restrictive (no comma), *which* + comma for nonrestrictive. Maintain this distinction rigorously in American legal and formal writing. |
| 11 | **Possessives: proper names ending in *s*** | Always add *'s*: "Burns's," "James's" | §7.20: same general rule; add *'s* to most singular proper names ending in *s*; exception for classical and biblical names: Jesus', Moses', Achilles', Xerxes' | — | **CMS governs.** Add *'s* to most names; classical names take only '. |
| 12 | **Compound adjective hyphenation** | Limited general guidance: hyphenate compound modifiers before a noun | §7.81–7.85 and hyphenation table (§7.89): detailed rules for permanent compounds, temporary compounds, prefixes, and open compounds; many exceptions | — | **CMS governs.** Consult CMS §7.89 hyphenation table for specific compounds. General rule: hyphenate when the compound precedes the noun ("well-known fact") and do not hyphenate after a linking verb ("the fact is well known"). |
| 13 | **Capitalization of titles** | Not addressed in detail | §8.157–8.167: headline-style — capitalize first word, last word, and all "major words" (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, some conjunctions); lowercase articles (*a*, *an*, *the*), prepositions (unless first/last), and coordinating conjunctions | — | **CMS governs.** Headline-style for titles of works. *The Brief in Support of Summary Judgment*. |
| 14 | **Quotation mark punctuation placement** | Not explicitly addressed | §6.9: periods and commas always go inside closing quotation marks in U.S. style; colons and semicolons go outside; question marks and exclamation points go inside only when part of the quoted matter | — | **CMS governs** (U.S. standard): commas and periods inside; colons and semicolons outside. |
| 15 | **Parenthetical em-dash** | Sparing use recommended | §6.85–6.87: specific valid uses — parenthetical insertions, abrupt break or shift, amplification after an introductory clause | **PCP ban** | **PCP governs.** No em-dashes. Rewrite: use commas for asides, colons for elaboration, parentheses for insertions, or restructure the sentence. |
| 16 | **Tense in summaries of texts** | Present tense for summaries | §5.130: present tense is standard for discussing the content of a written work (literary present) | — | **Both agree.** Use present tense: "The statute provides"; "The court holds." |
| 17 | ***Shall* vs. *will*** | Not strongly addressed in grammar sections | §5.152: *will* is the standard form in contemporary American English; *shall* is archaic except in legal drafting, first-person questions, and emphatic contexts | — | **CMS governs.** Use *will* in general prose. *Shall* remains appropriate in legislative, contractual, and legal contexts where it signals a mandatory obligation. |
| 18 | **Italics for emphasis** | Use sparingly; rely on word order and sentence structure for emphasis | §7.47–7.49: same counsel — italics for emphasis are legitimate but overuse dilutes their effect | — | **Both agree.** Use italics for emphasis sparingly; restructure rather than italicize when possible. |
| 19 | **Ellipsis form** | Three spaced dots: `. . .` or `...` | §13.51–13.58: three dots for omission within a sentence; four dots (period + ellipsis) when omission follows a complete sentence; square brackets or other notation for changes | — | **CMS governs.** Follow CMS §13.51–13.58 bracketing rules when quoting and omitting. In general prose, three dots for trailing off or pause. |
| 20 | **Paragraph length** | Short paragraphs preferred; one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis | No fixed rule; paragraph length should be proportional to the content and the rhythm of the argument | — | **CMS governs.** No length rule; length serves content. Short paragraphs are still a legitimate stylistic tool. |
| 21 | **Em-dash** | Not addressed as a punctuation mark | §6.85–6.87: em-dash has specific valid uses (parenthetical insertion, abrupt break, amplification, attribution of block quotation) | **PCP ban** | **PCP governs (user-confirmed override).** No em-dashes. Substitute: commas for parenthetical asides, colons for amplification, parentheses for insertions, semicolons or new sentences for breaks. |
| 22 | **Abbreviation punctuation** (*e.g.*, *i.e.*, *et al.*, *ibid.*) | Silent; no guidance | §10.4: periods required on each letter of *e.g.* and *i.e.*; period after *al.* in *et al.*; *ibid.* requires its period | — | **CMS governs.** Always write *e.g.*, *i.e.*, *et al.*, *ibid.* with periods. Comma follows *e.g.* and *i.e.* in U.S. style. See `elements-of-style/words-expressions.md` abbreviations section. |
| 23 | **Collective noun agreement** (*jury*, *committee*, *staff*, *team*) | American default: singular | §5.7–5.9: notional agreement — singular when the group acts as a unit ("The committee **was** unanimous"); plural when members act individually ("The committee **were** divided") | — | **CMS governs.** Notional agreement applies. Default to singular in American usage when in doubt. |
| 24 | **Subjunctive mood in *if*-clauses** | Silent; no guidance | §5.121–5.123: use subjunctive (*were*, *be*) for hypothetical, contrary-to-fact, and wished-for conditions: "If this **were** true…"; "I wish it **were** different." | — | **CMS governs.** Use *were* (not *was*) in hypothetical *if*-clauses: "If the statute **were** amended…" Use *was* only for factual past conditions: "If the bill **was** filed on time, it proceeded." |
| 25 | **Italics for foreign and Latin words** | Silent; no guidance | §7.47–7.49: italicize foreign words and phrases not yet anglicized on first use; do not italicize if the term is in Merriam-Webster as a standard English entry | — | **CMS governs.** Italicize *e.g.*, *i.e.*, *supra*, *infra*, *inter alia*, *prima facie*, *res judicata* in running text. Do not italicize in legal citation strings where the form is conventional. |
| 26 | **Capitalization of acronyms in titles** | Silent; no guidance | §8.157: in headline-style capitalization, acronyms and initialisms retain their all-caps form regardless of position | — | **CMS governs.** Write "The Role of the **IRS** in Tax Administration" — *IRS* stays all-caps even mid-title. Abbreviations like *U.S.* retain their standard form. |

## How to Add New Entries

When you encounter a new EoS/CMS/PCP divergence in practice:

1. Add a new row at the bottom with the next sequential number.
2. State what each source says (paraphrase; do not quote CMS verbatim).
3. Include the CMS section reference.
4. Note any PCP override only if the user has confirmed it.
5. State the resolution in the final column.
6. Update `index.md` if the new topic needs a routing entry.
